Inferno * Canto V

Dante and his guide, Virgil, descend from the First to the Second Circle of Hell. At its entrance, they encounter Minos, a monstrous figure who judges newly arrived souls. He wraps his tail around himself to indicate which circle of Hell the sinner is assigned to. Minos warns Dante against proceeding, but Virgil rebukes him, stating the journey is divinely willed. Inside the circle, a violent and eternal whirlwind buffets the souls of the lustful. Virgil points out famous figures such as Semiramis, Dido, and Cleopatra. Moved by pity, Dante asks to speak with two souls, Francesca and Paolo, who are swept along together. Francesca tells Dante their story: while reading a book about Lancelot, they were overcome with passion and shared a kiss, which led to their demise. As she speaks, Paolo weeps. Overwhelmed by the account, Dante faints and collapses to the ground.
Così discesi del cerchio primaio giù nel secondo, che men loco cinghia e tanto più dolor, che punge a guaio. Stavvi Minòs orribilmente, e ringhia: essamina le colpe ne l’intrata; giudica e manda secondo ch’avvinghia.
Thus I descended from the first circle down into the second, which girds less space, and with so much more pain that it provokes wailing. There stands Minos, horrific, and he growls: he examines the sins upon entry; he judges and sends them down as he enfolds them.
Dico che quando l’anima mal nata li vien dinanzi, tutta si confessa; e quel conoscitor de le peccata vede qual loco d’inferno è da essa; cignesi con la coda tante volte quantunque gradi vuol che giù sia messa.
I say that when the ill-born soul appears before him, it confesses everything; and he who understands sins sees which place in Hell is meant for it; he wraps himself with his tail as many times as the degrees he wants it sent down.
Sempre dinanzi a lui ne stanno molte: vanno a vicenda ciascuna al giudizio, dicono e odono e poi son giù volte. «O tu che vieni al doloroso ospizio», disse Minòs a me quando mi vide, lasciando l’atto di cotanto offizio,
Always before him stand many: each goes in turn to judgment, they speak and hear, and then are cast down. "O you who come to the house of sorrow," Minos said to me when he saw me, abandoning the act of his office,
«guarda com’ entri e di cui tu ti fide; non t’inganni l’ampiezza de l’intrare!». E ’l duca mio a lui: «Perché pur gride?
"Consider how you enter and whom you trust; do not be deceived by the spaciousness of the entrance!" And my guide to him: "Why do you keep shouting?"
Non impedir lo suo fatale andare: vuolsi così colà dove si puote ciò che si vuole, e più non dimandare». Or incomincian le dolenti note a farmisi sentire; or son venuto là dove molto pianto mi percuote.
Do not impede his fated path: so it is willed where what is willed can be, and ask no more." Now the mournful notes begin to make themselves felt by me; now I have come to where much weeping strikes me.
Io venni in loco d’ogne luce muto, che mugghia come fa mar per tempesta, se da contrari venti è combattuto. La bufera infernal, che mai non resta, mena li spirti con la sua rapina; voltando e percotendo li molesta.
I came to a place mute of all light, which bellows like the sea in a tempest, if it is assailed by opposing winds. The infernal tempest, which never ceases, drives the spirits with its rapine; whirling and striking, it molests them.
Quando giungon davanti a la ruina, quivi le strida, il compianto, il lamento; bestemmian quivi la virtù divina. Intesi ch’a così fatto tormento enno dannati i peccator carnali, che la ragion sommettono al talento.
When they arrive before the ruin, there are shrieks, laments, and wails; they blaspheme against the divine power. I understood that those sinners of the flesh, who subject reason to their desires, are condemned to such torment.
E come li stornei ne portan l’ali nel freddo tempo, a schiera larga e piena, così quel fiato li spiriti mali di qua, di là, di giù, di sù li mena; nulla speranza li conforta mai, non che di posa, ma di minor pena.
And just as starlings are borne on their wingsin the cold season, in a large and full flock,so does that blast drive the evil spirits,this way, that way, down, up;no hope ever comforts them,not even for rest, but for lesser pain.
E come i gru van cantando lor lai, faccendo in aere di sé lunga riga, così vid’ io venir, traendo guai, ombre portate da la detta briga; per ch’i’ dissi: «Maestro, chi son quelle genti che l’aura nera sì gastiga?».
And as cranes go chanting their cries,making in the air a long line of themselves,so I saw coming, drawing out woes, shades carried by the said strife;for which I said: “Master, who are thosepeople whom the black air so chastises?”
«La prima di color di cui novelle tu vuo’ saper», mi disse quelli allotta, «fu imperadrice di molte favelle. A vizio di lussuria fu sì rotta, che libito fé licito in sua legge, per tòrre il biasmo in che era condotta.
"The first of those you wish to know about," he said then, "was an empress of many tongues. She was so given to the vice of lust that she made lust licit in her law, to remove the blame she was under."
Ell’ è Semiramìs, di cui si legge che succedette a Nino e fu sua sposa: tenne la terra che ’l Soldan corregge. L’altra è colei che s’ancise amorosa, e ruppe fede al cener di Sicheo; poi è Cleopatràs lussurïosa.
It is Semiramis, of whom it is written that she succeeded Ninus and was his wife: she held the land that the Sultan governs. The other is she who killed herself for love, and broke faith with the ashes of Sychaeus; then there is Cleopatra, the lustful one.
Elena vedi, per cui tanto reo tempo si volse, e vedi ’l grande Achille, che con amore al fine combatteo. Vedi Parìs, Tristano»; e più di mille ombre mostrommi e nominommi a dito, ch’amor di nostra vita dipartille.
Elena, you see, for whom so much wicked time did turn, and see the great Achilles, who at the end fought with love. See Paris, Tristan»; and with more than a thousand shades he showed me and pointed them out, whom love of our life separated.
Poscia ch’io ebbi ’l mio dottore udito nomar le donne antiche e ’ cavalieri, pietà mi giunse, e fui quasi smarrito. I’ cominciai: «Poeta, volontieri parlerei a quei due che ’nsieme vanno, e paion sì al vento esser leggeri».
After I had heard my teacher name the ancient women and knights, pity seized me, and I was almost lost. I began: "Poet, I would gladly speak to those two who go together, and seem so light upon the wind."
Ed elli a me: «Vedrai quando saranno più presso a noi; e tu allor li priega per quello amor che i mena, ed ei verranno». Sì tosto come il vento a noi li piega, mossi la voce: «O anime affannate, venite a noi parlar, s’altri nol niega!».
And he to me: "You will see when they are closer to us; and then you should implore them, for that love that drives them, and they will come." As soon as the wind bends them toward us, I raised my voice: "O weary souls, come speak with us, if no one else denies it!"
Quali colombe dal disio chiamate con l’ali alzate e ferme al dolce nido vegnon per l’aere, dal voler portate; cotali uscir de la schiera ov’ è Dido, a noi venendo per l’aere maligno, sì forte fu l’affettüoso grido.
Like doves summoned by desire, with wings raised and steady, they come through the air to their sweet nest, borne by their will; so did these spirits emerge from the flock where Dido is, coming to us through the foul air, so powerful was the affectionate cry.
«O animal grazïoso e benigno che visitando vai per l’aere perso noi che tignemmo il mondo di sanguigno, se fosse amico il re de l’universo, noi pregheremmo lui de la tua pace, poi c’hai pietà del nostro mal perverso.
"O gracious and benevolent creature, who, journeying through the somber air, visits us, who stained the world with crimson, if the King of the Universe were our friend, we would pray to Him for your peace, since you take pity on our twisted suffering."
Di quel che udire e che parlar vi piace, noi udiremo e parleremo a voi, mentre che ’l vento, come fa, ci tace. Siede la terra dove nata fui su la marina dove ’l Po discende per aver pace co’ seguaci sui.
Of what you wish to hear and speak,we will listen and speak to you,while the wind, as it does, is silent.The land where I was born sitsupon the shore where the Po descendsto find peace with its followers.
Amor, ch’al cor gentil ratto s’apprende, prese costui de la bella persona che mi fu tolta; e ’l modo ancor m’offende. Amor, ch’a nullo amato amar perdona, mi prese del costui piacer sì forte, che, come vedi, ancor non m’abbandona.
Love, which is quickly kindled in the gentle heart, seized this man with the fair form that was taken from me; the way of it still wounds me. Love, which forgives no loved one from loving, took me so strongly with his pleasure that, as you see, it still does not abandon me.
Amor condusse noi ad una morte. Caina attende chi a vita ci spense».
Love led us to one death. Cain awaits the one who extinguished our lives.”
Queste parole da lor ci fuor porte. Quand’ io intesi quell’ anime offense, china’ il viso, e tanto il tenni basso, fin che ’l poeta mi disse: «Che pense?».
These words were carried to us from them. When I understood the suffering of those souls, I bowed my face, and held it low for so long, until the poet said to me, "What are you thinking?"
Quando rispuosi, cominciai: «Oh lasso, quanti dolci pensier, quanto disio menò costoro al doloroso passo!». Poi mi rivolsi a loro e parla’ io, e cominciai: «Francesca, i tuoi martìri a lagrimar mi fanno tristo e pio.
When I answered, I began: "Alas, how many sweet thoughts, how much desire led these souls to this woeful pass!" Then, turning to them, I spoke, and began: "Francesca, your torments make me weep with sorrow and pity."
Ma dimmi: al tempo d’i dolci sospiri, a che e come concedette amore che conosceste i dubbiosi disiri?». E quella a me: «Nessun maggior dolore che ricordarsi del tempo felice ne la miseria; e ciò sa ’l tuo dottore.
But tell me: in the time of sweet sighs, by what means and how did love allow you to recognize those uncertain desires?" And she to me: "There is no greater sorrow than to recall a happy time in misery; and your teacher knows this."
Ma s’a conoscer la prima radice del nostro amor tu hai cotanto affetto, dirò come colui che piange e dice. Noi leggiavamo un giorno per diletto di Lancialotto come amor lo strinse; soli eravamo e sanza alcun sospetto.
But if you have such a desire to know the first root of our love, I will speak like one who weeps and tells. One day, for pleasure, we read of Lancelot, how love constrained him; we were alone and without any suspicion.
Per più fïate li occhi ci sospinse quella lettura, e scolorocci il viso; ma solo un punto fu quel che ci vinse. Quando leggemmo il disïato riso esser basciato da cotanto amante, questi, che mai da me non fia diviso,
Our eyes, many times, were forced to meet by that reading, and our faces lost their color; but it was only one point that defeated us. When we read of the smiling mouth being kissed by such a lover, this one, who shall never be parted from me,
la bocca mi basciò tutto tremante. Galeotto fu ’l libro e chi lo scrisse: quel giorno più non vi leggemmo avante».
My mouth was kissed, all trembling. The book, and he who wrote it, were a pander. We read no further that day.
Mentre che l’uno spirto questo disse, l’altro piangëa; sì che di pietade io venni men così com’ io morisse. E caddi come corpo morto cade.
While one spirit spoke these words, the other wept; moved by pity, I felt myself failing as though I were dying. I fell like a dead body falls.
