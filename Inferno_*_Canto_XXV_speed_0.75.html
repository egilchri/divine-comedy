Inferno * Canto XXV

The canto begins as the thief Vanni Fucci makes a defiant, obscene gesture toward God. In response, serpents immediately coil around his neck and arms, silencing him before he flees. A centaur named Cacus, covered in snakes and with a fire-breathing dragon on his shoulders, appears in pursuit. Virgil identifies Cacus and explains he is punished here for theft. After Cacus passes, three new spirits arrive. A six-legged serpent attacks one of them, Agnello, and the two beings physically merge, their forms and colors blending until they become a single, grotesque creature that slowly walks away. Subsequently, a small, lizard-like serpent bites a second spirit, Buoso, in the navel. Smoke emanates from both the wound and the serpent's mouth, causing the two to exchange forms. The man slowly transforms into a serpent while the serpent takes on human features. The newly-formed man speaks to the third spirit, whom Dante recognizes as Puccio Sciancato, the only one of the three to remain unchanged.
Da indi in qua mi fuor le serpi amiche, perch’ una li s’avvolse allora al collo, come dicesse ‘Non vo’ che più diche’; e un’altra a le braccia, e rilegollo, ribadendo sé stessa sì dinanzi, che non potea con esse dare un crollo.
From then on, the serpents became my friends, as one then wrapped itself around his neck, as if to say, “I don’t want you to speak anymore.” And another bound his arms, knotting itself in front in such a way that he couldn’t shake them off.
Ahi Pistoia, Pistoia, ché non stanzi d’incenerarti sì che più non duri, poi che ’n mal fare il seme tuo avanzi? Per tutt’ i cerchi de lo ’nferno scuri non vidi spirto in Dio tanto superbo, non quel che cadde a Tebe giù da’ muri.
Ah, Pistoia, Pistoia, why do you not decree your own incineration, that you might no longer endure, since your seed excels at wickedness? Through all the shadowy circles of Hell, I did not see a spirit so proud against God, not even the one who fell from the walls of Thebes.
El si fuggì che non parlò più verbo; e io vidi un centauro pien di rabbia venir chiamando: «Ov’ è, ov’ è l’acerbo?». Maremma non cred’ io che tante n’abbia, quante bisce elli avea su per la groppa infin ove comincia nostra labbia.
He fled, speaking not another word; and I saw a centaur, filled with rage, come calling, "Where is he? Where is the bitter one?" I do not believe that the Maremma holds as many snakes as he had upon his back, right up to where our form begins.
Sovra le spalle, dietro da la coppa, con l’ali aperte li giacea un draco; e quello affuoca qualunque s’intoppa. Lo mio maestro disse: «Questi è Caco, che, sotto ’l sasso di monte Aventino, di sangue fece spesse volte laco.
Upon his shoulders, behind the neck,a dragon lay with open wings;and it scorches anyone who gets in the way.My master said: “This is Cacus,who, beneath the rock of Mount Aventine,made lakes of blood many times.”
Non va co’ suoi fratei per un cammino, per lo furto che frodolente fece del grande armento ch’elli ebbe a vicino; onde cessar le sue opere biece sotto la mazza d’Ercule, che forse gliene diè cento, e non sentì le diece».
He does not travel with his brothers on a shared path, because of the theft he deceitfully committed, taking the great herd that was nearby. Thus his wicked deeds ceased beneath Hercules' club, who may have given him a hundred blows, and he felt none of the ten.
Mentre che sì parlava, ed el trascorse, e tre spiriti venner sotto noi, de’ quai né io né ’l duca mio s’accorse, se non quando gridar: «Chi siete voi?»; per che nostra novella si ristette, e intendemmo pur ad essi poi.
While he was speaking, he sped on, and three spirits came beneath us, of whom neither I nor my guide was aware, except when they cried: “Who are you?” At which our new speech ceased, and we turned our attention only to them.
Io non li conoscea; ma ei seguette, come suol seguitar per alcun caso, che l’un nomar un altro convenette, dicendo: «Cianfa dove fia rimaso?»; per ch’io, acciò che ’l duca stesse attento, mi puosi ’l dito su dal mento al naso.
I didn’t know them, but one followed, as often happens by chance, that one had to name another, saying: “Where is Cianfa?” So that the guide would pay attention, I put my finger from my chin to my nose.
Se tu se’ or, lettore, a creder lento ciò ch’io dirò, non sarà maraviglia, ché io che ’l vidi, a pena il mi consento. Com’ io tenea levate in lor le ciglia, e un serpente con sei piè si lancia dinanzi a l’uno, e tutto a lui s’appiglia.
If you are slow, reader, to believewhat I will say, it will be no surprise,since I who saw it barely consent to it.As I held my eyes fixed on them,a serpent with six feet launched itselfin front of one, and completely clung to him.
Co’ piè di mezzo li avvinse la pancia e con li anterïor le braccia prese; poi li addentò e l’una e l’altra guancia; li diretani a le cosce distese, e miseli la coda tra ’mbedue e dietro per le ren sù la ritese.
With its middle feet, it clasped his belly, and with its forefeet, it seized his arms; then it bit him on both cheeks; it stretched its hind legs along his thighs, and thrust its tail between his two, and up behind the loins, it raised it.
Ellera abbarbicata mai non fue ad alber sì, come l’orribil fiera per l’altrui membra avviticchiò le sue. Poi s’appiccar, come di calda cera fossero stati, e mischiar lor colore, né l’un né l’altro già parea quel ch’era:
Never did ivy attach itself to a tree as this horrible beast entwined its limbs around the other's. Then they stuck together like they were hot wax, and mixed their colors, neither one of them looking like they used to.
come procede innanzi da l’ardore, per lo papiro suso, un color bruno che non è nero ancora e ’l bianco more. Li altri due ’l riguardavano, e ciascuno gridava: «Omè, Agnel, come ti muti!
Come forth, advancing from the heat, upon the parchment above, a brown color, which is not yet black, and the white fades. The other two were watching him, and each cried: "Alas, Agnel, how you change!"
Vedi che già non se’ né due né uno». Già eran li due capi un divenuti, quando n’apparver due figure miste in una faccia, ov’ eran due perduti.
See that you are no longer two nor one." Now the two heads had become a single one, when two forms appeared to us, mixed together in a single face, where two were lost.
Fersi le braccia due di quattro liste; le cosce con le gambe e ’l ventre e ’l casso divenner membra che non fuor mai viste. Ogne primaio aspetto ivi era casso: due e nessun l’imagine perversa parea; e tal sen gio con lento passo.
The arms became four limbs;the thighs, legs, belly, and chestbecame members never before seen.Every prior appearance was erased:two and none the perverse imageseemed; and thus it went with slow step.
Come ’l ramarro sotto la gran fersa dei dì canicular, cangiando sepe, folgore par se la via attraversa, sì pareva, venendo verso l’epe de li altri due, un serpentello acceso, livido e nero come gran di pepe;
As a lizard beneath the lashof the dog days, changing skin,a flash of lightning seems to cross the way;so it seemed, coming towards the other two,a small, burning serpent,livid and black like a peppercorn.
e quella parte onde prima è preso nostro alimento, a l’un di lor trafisse; poi cadde giuso innanzi lui disteso. Lo trafitto ’l mirò, ma nulla disse; anzi, co’ piè fermati, sbadigliava pur come sonno o febbre l’assalisse.
And in one of them, it pierced the part where our sustenance is first taken; then it fell, stretched out before him. The pierced one stared, but said nothing; instead, with his feet planted, he yawned as if sleep or fever had assailed him.
Elli ’l serpente e quei lui riguardava; l’un per la piaga e l’altro per la bocca fummavan forte, e ’l fummo si scontrava. Taccia Lucano ormai là dov’ e’ tocca del misero Sabello e di Nasidio, e attenda a udir quel ch’or si scocca.
The serpent gazed at him;from the wound and from the mouth,they both smoked fiercely, and the fumes collided.Let Lucan now be silent, where he touchesupon the wretched Sabellus and Nasidius,and let him attend to hear what now unfolds.
Taccia di Cadmo e d’Aretusa Ovidio, ché se quello in serpente e quella in fonte converte poetando, io non lo ’nvidio; ché due nature mai a fronte a fronte non trasmutò sì ch’amendue le forme a cambiar lor matera fosser pronte.
Ovid should now be silent, when he speaks of Cadmus and Arethusa; for if, with his poetry, he converts one to a serpent, and the other to a spring, I do not envy him; for never has he transposed two natures face to face so that both forms were ready to change their substance.
Insieme si rispuosero a tai norme, che ’l serpente la coda in forca fesse, e ’l feruto ristrinse insieme l’orme. Le gambe con le cosce seco stesse s’appiccar sì, che ’n poco la giuntura non facea segno alcun che si paresse.
Together they responded to those terms, so that the serpent split its tail in two, and the wounded one drew his legs back together. The legs stuck to the thighs, so that in a short time the joining showed no sign of itself.
Togliea la coda fessa la figura che si perdeva là, e la sua pelle si facea molle, e quella di là dura. Io vidi intrar le braccia per l’ascelle, e i due piè de la fiera, ch’eran corti, tanto allungar quanto accorciavan quelle.
The form that had split its tail, it shed, and disappeared. Its skin softened, while the other's hardened. I saw the arms withdraw into the armpits, and the beast's two feet, which were short, lengthen as those of the other shortened.
Poscia li piè di rietro, insieme attorti, diventaron lo membro che l’uom cela, e ’l misero del suo n’avea due porti. Mentre che ’l fummo l’uno e l’altro vela di color novo, e genera ’l pel suso per l’una parte e da l’altra il dipela,
Then the hind feet, twisted together, became the member that a man conceals, and the wretched creature had two of them. While the smoke veiled one and the other with new color, and generated hair on one part and stripped it from the other,
l’un si levò e l’altro cadde giuso, non torcendo però le lucerne empie, sotto le quai ciascun cambiava muso. Quel ch’era dritto, il trasse ver’ le tempie, e di troppa matera ch’in là venne uscir li orecchi de le gote scempie;
one rose, the other fell below,without twisting the wicked lamps,beneath which each face was transformed.What was upright, he drew toward the temples,and from too much matter that came there,the ears emerged from the simple cheeks;
ciò che non corse in dietro e si ritenne di quel soverchio, fé naso a la faccia e le labbra ingrossò quanto convenne. Quel che giacëa, il muso innanzi caccia, e li orecchi ritira per la testa come face le corna la lumaccia;
That which didn't recede and was held backfrom that excess, formed a nose on the face,and thickened the lips as was fitting.That which lay flat, thrust the snout forward,and pulled the ears back over the head,as snails do with their horns;
e la lingua, ch’avëa unita e presta prima a parlar, si fende, e la forcuta ne l’altro si richiude; e ’l fummo resta. L’anima ch’era fiera divenuta, suffolando si fugge per la valle, e l’altro dietro a lui parlando sputa.
and the tongue, which had been unified and swiftbefore in speech, splits, and the forked partcloses in the other; and the smoke remains.The soul, which had become fierce,hisses and flees through the valley,and the other spits as it speaks behind him.
Poscia li volse le novelle spalle, e disse a l’altro: «I’ vo’ che Buoso corra, com’ ho fatt’ io, carpon per questo calle». Così vid’ io la settima zavorra mutare e trasmutare; e qui mi scusi la novità se fior la penna abborra.
Then he turned his shoulders to the others, and said to the second: "I want Buoso to crawl, as I have done, on all fours along this road." So I saw the seventh ballast change and transform; and may novelty excuse me if my pen hesitates to speak of it.
E avvegna che li occhi miei confusi fossero alquanto e l’animo smagato, non poter quei fuggirsi tanto chiusi, ch’i’ non scorgessi ben Puccio Sciancato; ed era quel che sol, di tre compagni che venner prima, non era mutato;
And though my eyes were somewhat clouded and my spirit weakened, they could not hide themselves so completely that I did not clearly see Puccio Sciancato; and he alone, of the three companions who came first, had not been changed.
l’altr’ era quel che tu, Gaville, piagni. Inferno • Canto XXVI
