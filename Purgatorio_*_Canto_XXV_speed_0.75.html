Purgatorio * Canto XXV

As Dante, Virgil, and Statius ascend a narrow stairway, Dante asks how souls, who do not need food, can appear emaciated. Virgil defers the question to Statius, who delivers a lengthy discourse on the generation of the human body and the creation of the soul. He explains that after death, the soul impresses its form upon the surrounding air, creating an "aerial body" that can reflect suffering and desire, which accounts for the appearance of the shades. Upon reaching the seventh terrace, the poets find a wall of fire erupting from the mountainside, forcing them to walk single-file along a narrow outer ledge. From within the flames, they hear spirits singing the hymn ‘Summae Deus clementiae’. After completing the hymn, the souls shout examples of chastity, mentioning Mary and Diana, before resuming their song. Dante observes them carefully while navigating the dangerous path.
Ora era onde ’l salir non volea storpio; ché ’l sole avëa il cerchio di merigge lasciato al Tauro e la notte a lo Scorpio: per che, come fa l’uom che non s’affigge ma vassi a la via sua, che che li appaia, se di bisogno stimolo il trafigge,
Now the ascent was no longer hampered, for the sun had left the meridian circle to Taurus, and night to Scorpio. Thus, as a man who does not linger but continues on his way, whatever may appear, if the spur of necessity pricks him,
così intrammo noi per la callaia, uno innanzi altro prendendo la scala che per artezza i salitor dispaia. E quale il cicognin che leva l’ala per voglia di volare, e non s’attenta d’abbandonar lo nido, e giù la cala;
so we entered into that passage, one before the other, taking to the stairs, whose narrowness separated those climbing. And as a young stork that lifts its wing with the urge to fly, but does not dare to leave the nest, and lowers it;
tal era io con voglia accesa e spenta di dimandar, venendo infino a l’atto che fa colui ch’a dicer s’argomenta. Non lasciò, per l’andar che fosse ratto, lo dolce padre mio, ma disse: «Scocca l’arco del dir, che ’nfino al ferro hai tratto».
Such was I, with desire both ignited and extinguished, to ask my question, coming to the point where one readies themselves to speak. My sweet father did not stop, though our pace was quick, but said: "Release the arrow of your words, for you have already drawn it to the bow."
Allor sicuramente apri’ la bocca e cominciai: «Come si può far magro là dove l’uopo di nodrir non tocca?». «Se t’ammentassi come Meleagro si consumò al consumar d’un stizzo, non fora», disse, «a te questo sì agro;
Then I opened my mouth for sure and began: "How can one grow thin where the need to feed does not apply?" "If you remembered how Meleager wasted away as a burning brand was consumed, this would not be so bitter to you," he said.
e se pensassi come, al vostro guizzo, guizza dentro a lo specchio vostra image, ciò che par duro ti parrebbe vizzo. Ma perché dentro a tuo voler t’adage, ecco qui Stazio; e io lui chiamo e prego che sia or sanator de le tue piage».
And if you considered how, at your glance, your image leaps within the mirror, that which seems harsh would seem to you withered. But because you are content within your own will, here is Statius; and I call upon him and beg that he now be the healer of your wounds."
«Se la veduta etterna li dislego», rispuose Stazio, «là dove tu sie, discolpi me non potert’ io far nego». Poi cominciò: «Se le parole mie, figlio, la mente tua guarda e riceve, lume ti fiero al come che tu die.
"If the eternal vision is revealed to them," Statius replied, "there where you are, absolve me, for I cannot refuse this. Then he began: "If, my son, your mind heeds and receives my words, they will be a light to you as to what you ask."
Sangue perfetto, che poi non si beve da l’assetate vene, e si rimane quasi alimento che di mensa leve, prende nel core a tutte membra umane virtute informativa, come quello ch’a farsi quelle per le vene vane.
Perfect blood, which is never drunkfrom the thirsty veins, and remainsalmost nourishment that, lifted from the table, takesin the heart, to all human limbs,an informative virtue, like thatwhich makes them through the empty veins.
Ancor digesto, scende ov’ è più bello tacer che dire; e quindi poscia geme sovr’ altrui sangue in natural vasello. Ivi s’accoglie l’uno e l’altro insieme, l’un disposto a patire, e l’altro a fare per lo perfetto loco onde si preme;
Still digested, it descends where it is more beautifulto be silent than to speak; and then it groansover other blood in a natural vessel. There the one and the other gather together,the one disposed to suffer, and the other to actfor the perfect place from which it is pressed;
e, giunto lui, comincia ad operare coagulando prima, e poi avviva ciò che per sua matera fé constare. Anima fatta la virtute attiva qual d’una pianta, in tanto differente, che questa è in via e quella è già a riva,
And when it arrives, it begins to work, first coagulating, and then bringing to life that which it caused to be its matter. The active force then becomes a soul, like that of a plant, the difference being that one is on its way, and the other is already at the shore.
tanto ovra poi, che già si move e sente, come spungo marino; e indi imprende ad organar le posse ond’ è semente. Or si spiega, figliuolo, or si distende la virtù ch’è dal cor del generante, dove natura a tutte membra intende.
It works so much, that it moves and feels, like a sea sponge; and then it begins to organize the powers that are its seed. Now it expands, my son, now the virtue that comes from the heart of the one who generates spreads out, where nature attends to all the limbs.
Ma come d’animal divegna fante, non vedi tu ancor: quest’ è tal punto, che più savio di te fé già errante, sì che per sua dottrina fé disgiunto da l’anima il possibile intelletto, perché da lui non vide organo assunto.
But how it turns from beast to man,you still don't see: this is the point,that made one wiser than you err,so that through his doctrine he dividedthe potential intellect from the soul,because he did not see an organ assumed by it.
Apri a la verità che viene il petto; e sappi che, sì tosto come al feto l’articular del cerebro è perfetto, lo motor primo a lui si volge lieto sovra tant’ arte di natura, e spira spirito novo, di vertù repleto,
Open your breast to the truth that comes; and know that, as soon as the articulation of the brain is perfect in the fetus, the prime mover turns to it happily over such art of nature, and breathes a new spirit, filled with virtue.
che ciò che trova attivo quivi, tira in sua sustanzia, e fassi un’alma sola, che vive e sente e sé in sé rigira. E perché meno ammiri la parola, guarda il calor del sole che si fa vino, giunto a l’omor che de la vite cola.
And whatever it finds active there, it pullsinto its own substance, and becomes a single soul,that lives and feels and turns within itself. And so you marvel less at the word,observe the sun’s heat, which becomes wine,joined to the liquid that flows from the vine.
Quando Làchesis non ha più del lino, solvesi da la carne, e in virtute ne porta seco e l’umano e ’l divino: l’altre potenze tutte quante mute; memoria, intelligenza e volontade in atto molto più che prima agute.
When Lachesis no longer has flax,she separates from the flesh, and in essence,she carries with her both the human and the divine:the other powers all mute;memory, intelligence, and willsharpened in action far beyond before.
Sanza restarsi, per sé stessa cade mirabilmente a l’una de le rive; quivi conosce prima le sue strade. Tosto che loco lì la circunscrive, la virtù formativa raggia intorno così e quanto ne le membra vive.
Without pausing, it falls by itself,miraculously, to one of the shores;there, it first recognizes its paths.As soon as place circumscribes it there,the formative virtue radiates aroundas much and as it does in living limbs.
E come l’aere, quand’ è ben pïorno, per l’altrui raggio che ’n sé si reflette, di diversi color diventa addorno; così l’aere vicin quivi si mette e in quella forma ch’è in lui suggella virtüalmente l’alma che ristette;
And as the air, when it is quite saturated,with light reflected from another source,is adorned with colors diverse and bright;so the nearby air places itself thereand in that form which it seals within itselfvirtually the soul which had remained;
e simigliante poi a la fiammella che segue il foco là ’vunque si muta, segue lo spirto sua forma novella. Però che quindi ha poscia sua paruta, è chiamata ombra; e quindi organa poi ciascun sentire infino a la veduta.
And then, similar to the small flamethat follows the fire wherever it shifts,the spirit follows its new form.Because from that, it later takes its appearance,it is called a shadow; and from it, then,each sense is organized, even sight.
Quindi parliamo e quindi ridiam noi; quindi facciam le lagrime e ’ sospiri che per lo monte aver sentiti puoi. Secondo che ci affliggono i disiri e li altri affetti, l’ombra si figura; e quest’ è la cagion di che tu miri».
So then we speak, and then we laugh; so then we make the tears and sighs that you can hear throughout the mountain. According to the desires and other affections that afflict us, the shadow is shaped; and this is the reason for what you see.”
E già venuto a l’ultima tortura s’era per noi, e vòlto a la man destra, ed eravamo attenti ad altra cura. Quivi la ripa fiamma in fuor balestra, e la cornice spira fiato in suso che la reflette e via da lei sequestra;
And we had come to the final torment, turning to the right, intent on other concerns. There, the ledge shoots out flames, and the cornice breathes upward, reflecting them and keeping them away.
ond’ ir ne convenia dal lato schiuso ad uno ad uno; e io temëa ’l foco quinci, e quindi temeva cader giuso. Lo duca mio dicea: «Per questo loco si vuol tenere a li occhi stretto il freno, però ch’errar potrebbesi per poco».
And there, one by one, we had to go from the open side; I feared the fire here, and there I feared falling down. My guide said: "In this place, it is necessary to hold the reins tight on the eyes, because one could easily go astray."
‘Summae Deus clementïae’ nel seno al grande ardore allora udi’ cantando, che di volger mi fé caler non meno; e vidi spirti per la fiamma andando; per ch’io guardava a loro e a’ miei passi compartendo la vista a quando a quando.
"O God of utmost clemency," I heard them sing withinthat great fire, which made me want to turn back even more.And I saw spirits going through the flame,so I looked at them and at my steps,dividing my gaze from time to time.
Appresso il fine ch’a quell’ inno fassi, gridavano alto: ‘Virum non cognosco’; indi ricominciavan l’inno bassi. Finitolo, anco gridavano: «Al bosco si tenne Diana, ed Elice caccionne che di Venere avea sentito il tòsco».
After that hymn was finished, they cried out loudly: "I do not know the man," then they began the hymn again, softly. When it was done, they cried out again: "Diana remained in the forest, and drove out Helice, who felt the poison of Venus."
Indi al cantar tornavano; indi donne gridavano e mariti che fuor casti come virtute e matrimonio imponne. E questo modo credo che lor basti per tutto il tempo che ’l foco li abbruscia: con tal cura conviene e con tai pasti
Then they returned to their song; after that, womenand husbands cried out who were chaste,as virtue and marriage command. And in this way, I believe that it is enough for themfor all the time that the fire scorches them:with such care is fitting and with such sustenance,
