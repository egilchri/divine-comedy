Inferno * Canto XIV

Dante and Virgil leave the Wood of the Suicides and enter the third ring of the seventh circle. They arrive at a vast, barren plain of burning sand upon which large flakes of fire continuously fall. On the sand are groups of naked souls: some lie supine, some are crouched and sitting, and others wander without rest, all weeping and attempting to swat the flames away. Dante notices a large, defiant figure lying on the sand who appears to scorn the torment. The shade identifies himself as Capaneus, one of the seven kings who besieged Thebes, boasting that he is as unconquered in death as he was in life. Virgil rebukes Capaneus, telling him that his unceasing rage is his true punishment. The poets then come to a small, red stream that flows across the burning plain. Its banks and bed are made of stone, and the fiery rain is extinguished above it, providing a safe path. Virgil explains that this stream, Phlegethon, and all the rivers of Hell originate from the tears of a giant statue, the Old Man of Crete, before they continue along the stone bank.
