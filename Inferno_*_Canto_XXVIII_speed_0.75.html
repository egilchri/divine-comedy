Inferno * Canto XXVIII

Dante and Virgil arrive at the ninth bolgia of the eighth circle and observe souls who are perpetually wounded and dismembered by a demon’s sword. The first sinner Dante speaks to is Mohammed, who is vertically split open. He identifies a nearby soul, Ali, as having his head cloven. Mohammed explains their torment: their wounds heal as they walk the circle, only to be reopened by the demon on each pass. After Virgil explains Dante's living status, Mohammed gives a message for Fra Dolcino. Another sinner, Pier da Medicina, whose throat is pierced and nose severed, delivers a prophecy about two men from Fano. He then identifies two others: Curio, with his tongue cut out, and Mosca de' Lamberti, whose hands have been amputated. Finally, Dante sees a walking torso carrying its own severed head by the hair. The head speaks, identifying itself as Bertran de Born, and displays its wound to Dante before the poets move on.
Chi poria mai pur con parole sciolte dicer del sangue e de le piaghe a pieno ch’i’ ora vidi, per narrar più volte? Ogne lingua per certo verria meno per lo nostro sermone e per la mente c’hanno a tanto comprender poco seno.
Who could ever, even with untangled words, fully describe the blood and wounds that I saw then, even if I spoke of them many times over? Surely any tongue would fail, as would our speech and the mind, which is so limited in its capacity to understand such things.
S’el s’aunasse ancor tutta la gente che già, in su la fortunata terra di Puglia, fu del suo sangue dolente per li Troiani e per la lunga guerra che de l’anella fé sì alte spoglie, come Livïo scrive, che non erra,
If all the people who once, upon the blessed land of Apulia, grieved for their blood, were gathered, both those who fought for the Trojans and those who suffered the long war, the one from which the rings made such high spoils, as Livy writes, who does not err,
con quella che sentio di colpi doglie per contastare a Ruberto Guiscardo; e l’altra il cui ossame ancor s’accoglie a Ceperan, là dove fu bugiardo ciascun Pugliese, e là da Tagliacozzo, dove sanz’ arme vinse il vecchio Alardo;

Between the legs hung the entrails; the guts were visible, and the wretched sack that turns to shit what's been swallowed. While I fixed my gaze on him in order to see everything, he looked at me and with his hands tore open his chest, saying: "Now see how I rip myself open!"

See how Muhammad is mangled! Ahead of me goes Ali, weeping, his face split open from chin to the forelock.
E tutti li altri che tu vedi qui, seminator di scandalo e di scisma fuor vivi, e però son fessi così. Un diavolo è qua dietro che n’accisma sì crudelmente, al taglio de la spada rimettendo ciascun di questa risma,
And all the others that you see here, were sowers of scandal and schism while alive, and therefore they are cleft in this way. A devil is here behind us, who now so cruelly cuts us up, renewing the wounds on each of this kind, with the sword.
quand’ avem volta la dolente strada; però che le ferite son richiuse prima ch’altri dinanzi li rivada. Ma tu chi se’ che ’n su lo scoglio muse, forse per indugiar d’ire a la pena ch’è giudicata in su le tue accuse?».
When we’d turned along the mournful road, because the wounds have closed again before another can reach them. But who are you, that you linger on the cliff, perhaps to delay going to the punishment that is judged upon your accusations?
«Né morte ’l giunse ancor, né colpa ’l mena», rispuose ’l mio maestro, «a tormentarlo; ma per dar lui esperïenza piena, a me, che morto son, convien menarlo per lo ’nferno qua giù di giro in giro; e quest’ è ver così com’ io ti parlo».
"Death has yet to touch him, nor does any sin bring him here," my master answered, "to torment him; but to give him full experience, it is necessary for me, who am dead, to lead him through Hell, going round and round; and this is as true as I speak to you."
Più fuor di cento che, quando l’udiro, s’arrestaron nel fosso a riguardarmi per maraviglia, oblïando il martiro. «Or dì a fra Dolcin dunque che s’armi, tu che forse vedra’ il sole in breve, s’ello non vuol qui tosto seguitarmi,
More than a hundred spirits, when they heard my words, stopped in their pit to stare, marveling, forgetting their torment. "Now tell Fra Dolcino, then, to arm himself, you who will soon perhaps see the sun, if he does not wish to follow me quickly here,

Un altro, che forata avea la gola e tronco ’l naso infin sotto le ciglia, e non avea mai ch’una orecchia sola, ristato a riguardar per maraviglia con li altri, innanzi a li altri aprì la canna, ch’era di fuor d’ogne parte vermiglia,
Another, whose throat had been pierced, and whose nose was sliced off beneath the eyebrows, and who had only one ear remaining, stopped to stare in wonder with the others. Before the rest, he opened his throat, which was red on every side.
e disse: «O tu cui colpa non condanna e cu’ io vidi su in terra latina, se troppa simiglianza non m’inganna, rimembriti di Pier da Medicina, se mai torni a veder lo dolce piano che da Vercelli a Marcabò dichina.
And he said: "Oh, you, whom no guilt condemns, and whom I saw in the Latin lands, if too much similarity does not deceive me, remember Pier da Medicina, if ever you return to see the sweet plain that slopes from Vercelli to Marcabò.
E fa saper a’ due miglior da Fano, a messer Guido e anco ad Angiolello, che, se l’antiveder qui non è vano, gittati saran fuor di lor vasello e mazzerati presso a la Cattolica per tradimento d’un tiranno fello.
And tell the two best men of Fano, Sir Guido and also Angiolello, that if my foresight here is not in vain, they will be thrown out of their ship and butchered near Cattolica through the treachery of a cruel tyrant.
Tra l’isola di Cipri e di Maiolica non vide mai sì gran fallo Nettuno, non da pirate, non da gente argolica. Quel traditor che vede pur con l’uno, e tien la terra che tale qui meco vorrebbe di vedere esser digiuno,
Between the isle of Cyprus and Majorca, Neptune never witnessed such a great offense, not from pirates, nor from Argive people. That traitor, who sees only with one eye, and holds the land, desires to see that man with me here, starving.
farà venirli a parlamento seco; poi farà sì, ch’al vento di Focara non sarà lor mestier voto né preco». E io a lui: «Dimostrami e dichiara, se vuo’ ch’i’ porti sù di te novella, chi è colui da la veduta amara».
He will summon them to speak with him; then he will arrange things so that they won't need vows or prayers to the wind of Focara." And I said to him: "Show me and tell me, if you want me to bring back news of you, who is he of the bitter gaze."
Allor puose la mano a la mascella d’un suo compagno e la bocca li aperse, gridando: «Questi è desso, e non favella. Questi, scacciato, il dubitar sommerse in Cesare, affermando che ’l fornito sempre con danno l’attender sofferse».

Oh, how terrified Curio seemed to me,with his tongue cut out at the throat,he who was so bold in speech!And one who had both hands cut off,raising his stumps in the murky air,so that blood made his face filthy,
gridò: «Ricordera’ti anche del Mosca, che disse, lasso!, “Capo ha cosa fatta”, che fu mal seme per la gente tosca». E io li aggiunsi: «E morte di tua schiatta»; per ch’elli, accumulando duol con duolo, sen gio come persona trista e matta.
He cried out: "Also remember Mosca, who said, alas!, 'What's done is done', which was a wicked seed for the Tuscan people." And I added to him: "And death to your kin"; at which, piling grief upon grief, he went away like a wretched and mad person.
Ma io rimasi a riguardar lo stuolo, e vidi cosa ch’io avrei paura, sanza più prova, di contarla solo; se non che coscïenza m’assicura, la buona compagnia che l’uom francheggia sotto l’asbergo del sentirsi pura.
But I stayed to look upon that throng, and I saw something of which I would fear, without further proof, to speak alone; were it not that conscience assures me, the good companion that emboldens a man beneath the breastplate of feeling pure.
Io vidi certo, e ancor par ch’io ’l veggia, un busto sanza capo andar sì come andavan li altri de la trista greggia; e ’l capo tronco tenea per le chiome, pesol con mano a guisa di lanterna: e quel mirava noi e dicea: «Oh me!».
I certainly saw, and still it seems I see it, a body without a head walking as the others of that wretched flock were doing; and the severed head it held by its hair, swinging in its hand like a lantern: and it looked at us and said, "Oh me!".
Di sé facea a sé stesso lucerna, ed eran due in uno e uno in due; com’ esser può, quei sa che sì governa. Quando diritto al piè del ponte fue, levò ’l braccio alto con tutta la testa per appressarne le parole sue,
He made a lantern of himself, and was two in one and one in two; how this can be, he who governs knows. When he was right at the foot of the bridge, he raised his arm high, with his whole head, to bring his words closer to us.
che fuoro: «Or vedi la pena molesta, tu che, spirando, vai veggendo i morti: vedi s’alcuna è grande come questa. E perché tu di me novella porti, sappi ch’i’ son Bertram dal Bornio, quelli che diedi al re giovane i ma’ conforti.
They said: "Now behold this grievous torment, you who, breathing, go seeing the dead: see if any is as great as this. And so that you may bring news of me, know that I am Bertram of Born, he who gave the young king evil counsel."
Io feci il padre e ’l figlio in sé ribelli; Achitofèl non fé più d’Absalone e di Davìd coi malvagi punzelli. Perch’ io parti’ così giunte persone, partito porto il mio cerebro, lasso!, dal suo principio ch’è in questo troncone.
I set father and son against each other; Achitophel did no worse with Absalom and David, using wicked goads. Because I sundered those joined souls, I carry my brain, alas!, parted from its source in this trunk.
