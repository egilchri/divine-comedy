Inferno * Canto X

Dante and Virgil walk through the Sixth Circle of Hell, a landscape of open, fiery tombs. A voice from one tomb addresses Dante, recognizing his Florentine accent. The shade of Farinata degli Uberti rises to his waist and questions Dante about his ancestry. They engage in a tense political dialogue about their families' historical rivalry. Their conversation is interrupted by another soul, Cavalcante de’ Cavalcanti, who rises from the same tomb and asks why his son is not with Dante. Based on Dante’s reply, Cavalcante assumes his son is dead and collapses back into the sepulcher in despair. Farinata, unmoved, continues his discussion and prophesies Dante’s future exile. Dante then asks how the souls can foresee the future but remain ignorant of the present. Farinata explains their foresight is limited and fades as events draw near. Before departing, Dante asks Farinata to inform Cavalcante that his son is still alive. Virgil then leads a troubled Dante away.

Suddenly this sound issued from one of the tombs; I approached, fearing, a bit closer to my guide. And he said to me: "Turn around!"

Io avea già il mio viso nel suo fitto; ed el s’ergea col petto e con la fronte com’ avesse l’inferno a gran dispitto. E l’animose man del duca e pronte mi pinser tra le sepulture a lui, dicendo: «Le parole tue sien conte».
I had already fixed my gaze upon his,and he stood, chest and brow thrust forward,as if he held all of Hell in utter disdain.And my guide's resolute, ready handspushed me among the tombs toward him,saying, "Let your words be measured."
Com’ io al piè de la sua tomba fui, guardommi un poco, e poi, quasi sdegnoso, mi dimandò: «Chi fuor li maggior tui?». Io ch’era d’ubidir disideroso, non gliel celai, ma tutto gliel’ apersi; ond’ ei levò le ciglia un poco in suso;
As I stood before his tomb, he looked at me for a moment, then, as if in disdain, asked, "Who were your ancestors?" I, eager to obey, did not hide the truth from him, but revealed all; at which he raised his eyebrows a little.
poi disse: «Fieramente furo avversi a me e a miei primi e a mia parte, sì che per due fïate li dispersi». «S’ei fur cacciati, ei tornar d’ogne parte», rispuos’ io lui, «l’una e l’altra fïata; ma i vostri non appreser ben quell’ arte».
Then he said: "They were fiercely opposedto me, my forebears, and my faction,so that I scattered them twice." "If they were driven out, they returned from every place,"I replied to him, "both times;but yours did not properly learn that art."
Allor surse a la vista scoperchiata un’ombra, lungo questa, infino al mento: credo che s’era in ginocchie levata. Dintorno mi guardò, come talento avesse di veder s’altri era meco; e poi che ’l sospecciar fu tutto spento,
Then, at the uncovered view, a shadow rose, extending along it to the chin: I believe it had risen on its knees. It looked around me, as if desiring to see if anyone else was with me; and when its suspicion was entirely gone,
piangendo disse: «Se per questo cieco carcere vai per altezza d’ingegno, mio figlio ov’ è? e perché non è teco?».
weeping, it said: "If through this blind prison you travel for the height of your intellect, where is my son? And why is he not with you?"

And I to him: “I come not of myself: he who awaits yonder, leads me here, perhaps he whom your Guido held in disdain.” His words and the manner of his suffering had already read his name to me; thus my reply was so full.
Di sùbito drizzato gridò: «Come? dicesti “elli ebbe”?

Quando s’accorse d’alcuna dimora ch’io facëa dinanzi a la risposta, supin ricadde e più non parve fora. Ma quell’ altro magnanimo, a cui posta restato m’era, non mutò aspetto, né mosse collo, né piegò sua costa;
When he realized I hesitated before answering, he fell backward, and I saw him no more. But the other magnanimous spirit, to whom I had turned, did not change appearance, nor move his neck, nor bend his side.
e sé continüando al primo detto, «S’elli han quell’ arte», disse, «male appresa, ciò mi tormenta più che questo letto. Ma non cinquanta volte fia raccesa la faccia de la donna che qui regge, che tu saprai quanto quell’ arte pesa.
And continuing from the first speech, he said, "If they have learned that craft poorly, that torments me more than this bed. But the face of the woman who reigns here will not have been rekindled fifty times before you will know how much that craft weighs."
E se tu mai nel dolce mondo regge, dimmi: perché quel popolo è sì empio incontr’ a’ miei in ciascuna sua legge?». Ond’ io a lui: «Lo strazio e ’l grande scempio che fece l’Arbia colorata in rosso, tal orazion fa far nel nostro tempio».
And if you ever rule in the sweet world, tell me: why is that people so cruel towards my kin in every one of their laws?" And I to him: "The slaughter and the great carnage that dyed the Arbia red, causes such prayers to be made in our temple."
Poi ch’ebbe sospirando il capo mosso, «A ciò non fu’ io sol», disse, «né certo sanza cagion con li altri sarei mosso. Ma fu’ io solo, là dove sofferto fu per ciascun di tòrre via Fiorenza, colui che la difesi a viso aperto».
Then, having moved his head with a sigh, he said, "I was not alone in this, and certainly, I wouldn't have been moved with the others without reason. But I alone, where it was endured by everyone to take Florence away, I was the one who defended her openly."
«Deh, se riposi mai vostra semenza», prega’ io lui, «solvetemi quel nodo che qui ha ’nviluppata mia sentenza. El par che voi veggiate, se ben odo, dinanzi quel che ’l tempo seco adduce, e nel presente tenete altro modo».
"If you ever find rest for your descendants," I prayed to him, "untangle the knot that has my judgment tangled. It seems that you see, if I hear correctly, what time brings, and you deal with the present in another way."
«Noi veggiam, come quei c’ha mala luce, le cose», disse, «che ne son lontano; cotanto ancor ne splende il sommo duce. Quando s’appressano o son, tutto è vano nostro intelletto; e s’altri non ci apporta, nulla sapem di vostro stato umano.
“We see things,” he said, “as those with poor eyesight do, things far off; that much the highest leader still shines for us. When they draw near or are present, our understanding is entirely useless; and if others don't bring us news, we know nothing of your human state.”
Però comprender puoi che tutta morta fia nostra conoscenza da quel punto che del futuro fia chiusa la porta». Allor, come di mia colpa compunto, dissi: «Or direte dunque a quel caduto che ’l suo nato è co’ vivi ancor congiunto;
But you can understand that all of our knowledge will be dead from the moment the door to the future is closed.” Then, as if stung by my guilt, I said: “Then you will tell the fallen one that his son is still joined with the living;
e s’i’ fui, dianzi, a la risposta muto, fate i saper che ’l fei perché pensava già ne l’error che m’avete soluto». E già ’l maestro mio mi richiamava; per ch’i’ pregai lo spirto più avaccio che mi dicesse chi con lu’ istava.
And if I was silent just now in response, have it known that I did so because I was already considering the error that you have absolved me of.” And already my master was calling for me; because of this, I implored the spirit more quickly to tell me who was with him.
Dissemi: «Qui con più di mille giaccio: qua dentro è ’l secondo Federico e ’l Cardinale; e de li altri mi taccio». Indi s’ascose; e io inver’ l’antico poeta volsi i passi, ripensando a quel parlar che mi parea nemico.
Dissemi: "Here lie more than a thousand: within is the second Frederick and the Cardinal; and of the others I say nothing." Then he hid himself; and I turned my steps toward the ancient poet, rethinking that speech which seemed to me hostile.
Elli si mosse; e poi, così andando, mi disse: «Perché se’ tu sì smarrito?». E io li sodisfeci al suo dimando.
He moved on; and then, as we walked, he said to me: "Why are you so troubled?" And I answered him, fulfilling his question.
«La mente tua conservi quel ch’udito hai contra te», mi comandò quel saggio; «e ora attendi qui», e drizzò ’l dito: «quando sarai dinanzi al dolce raggio di quella il cui bell’ occhio tutto vede, da lei saprai di tua vita il vïaggio».
"Keep in mind what you've heard spoken against you," the wise man commanded me, "and now wait here," and he raised his finger. "When you are before the sweet light of her whose beautiful eyes see everything, from her you will learn the journey of your life."
Appresso mosse a man sinistra il piede: lasciammo il muro e gimmo inver’ lo mezzo per un sentier ch’a una valle fiede, che ’nfin là sù facea spiacer suo lezzo.
Then he moved his foot to the left, and we left the wall and entered a place that led to a valley, whose stench displeased us even from up there.
