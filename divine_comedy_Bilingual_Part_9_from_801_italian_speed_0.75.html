PURGATORIO Purgatorio • Canto I
Midway along the journey of our life, I woke to find myself in a dark wood, for I had wandered off from the straight path. Ah, how harsh a thing to say was that wood, savage and rough and strong, which brings the fear of death to mind anew. And it was so bitter that death is scarcely more so; but to treat of the good that I found there, I will tell of the other things I saw. I cannot rightly say just how I entered there, so full of sleep was I at the moment when I left the true way. But when I had reached the foot of a hill, where that valley, which had pierced my heart with fear, closed in, I looked up, and I saw its shoulders already adorned with the rays of the planet which leads men straight along every path. Then my fear was somewhat calmed, which had endured in the lake of my heart throughout the night that I passed so wretchedly. And even as a man, escaped from the sea, who, panting, has come to shore, turns to the dangerous water and gazes upon it, so did my soul, still fleeing, turn back to look at the pass that had never spared a living person. After my weary body, I rested a while, and then I resumed my way along the desert slope, so that the firm foot was always the lower one. And, behold, just as at the beginning of the ascent, a leopard, agile and very swift, which was covered with a spotted hide, appeared before me; and it never went away from before my face, but instead blocked my path so much that many times I was turning back to return. The time was at the beginning of the morning, and the sun was ascending with those stars that were with it when the Divine Love first moved those beautiful things; so that the fair appearance of that beast, with the time of the day, gave me hope of the colorful skin. Yet not so much did it remove the fear that my appearance brought me, that the sight of a lion appeared, and it seemed to be coming at me with its head held high and with furious hunger, so that the air seemed to tremble at it. And then a she-wolf, which was full of every desire, which with its leanness, had made many people live wretchedly, appeared before me, and it had caused such a weight upon me, with the fear that its appearance brought me, that I lost the hope of the ascent. And just as a man, who is made happy, rejoices, and is saddened when the time comes when he loses, so the beast, making me return, step by step, was pushing me back to where the sun is silent. While I was falling back to the low place, a man appeared before me, who seemed to have failed from lack of voice. When I saw him in the great desert, I cried out: "Have pity on me, whoever you are, shade or true man!" He answered me: "Not man, I once was a man, and my parents were Lombards, and both were Mantuan by birth. I was born *sub Iulio*, though late, and I lived in Rome under the good Augustus, at a time when the false and lying gods were still present. I was a poet, and I sang of that just son of Anchises who came from Troy, when proud Ilium was burned."
Per correr miglior acque alza le vele omai la navicella del mio ingegno, che lascia dietro a sé mar sì crudele; e canterò di quel secondo regno dove l’umano spirito si purga e di salire al ciel diventa degno.
To navigate better waters, my intellect's little ship now raises its sails, leaving behind such a cruel sea. And I will sing of that second kingdom where the human spirit purges itself and becomes worthy to ascend to heaven.
Ma qui la morta poesì resurga, o sante Muse, poi che vostro sono; e qui Calïopè alquanto surga, seguitando il mio canto con quel suono di cui le Piche misere sentiro lo colpo tal, che disperar perdono.
