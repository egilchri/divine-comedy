Purgatorio * Canto XXII

As Dante, Virgil, and Statius ascend to the sixth terrace, an angel removes a "P" from Dante’s forehead, making his climb easier. Virgil asks Statius how he could have been guilty of avarice. Statius clarifies that his sin was the opposite, prodigality (wastefulness), and that he was inspired to repent by a line in Virgil's *Aeneid*. Virgil then asks how Statius converted to Christianity. Statius explains that Virgil's own Fourth Eclogue, which he read as a prophecy, guided him toward the new faith, along with the example of persecuted Christians. He confesses he was a secret Christian out of fear, which is why he spent so long on the terrace of sloth. Statius asks about the fate of other classical poets, and Virgil confirms they reside in Limbo. Upon reaching the sixth terrace, they encounter an unusual tree with fragrant fruit. A voice from the branches warns them away from the food and recites examples of temperance.
Già era l’angel dietro a noi rimaso, l’angel che n’avea vòlti al sesto giro, avendomi dal viso un colpo raso; e quei c’hanno a giustizia lor disiro detto n’avea beati, e le sue voci con ‘sitiunt’, sanz’ altro, ciò forniro.
The angel had remained behind us already, the one who had turned us toward the sixth circle, having erased a stroke from my face; and he had called those who desire justice blessed, and with "sitiunt," without anything else, his words were finished.
E io più lieve che per l’altre foci m’andava, sì che sanz’ alcun labore seguiva in sù li spiriti veloci; quando Virgilio incominciò: «Amore, acceso di virtù, sempre altro accese, pur che la fiamma sua paresse fore;
And I went more lightly than at the other passes, so that without any effort I followed upward the swift spirits; when Virgil began: "Love, ignited by virtue, always ignited another, as long as its flame was apparent outside;
onde da l’ora che tra noi discese nel limbo de lo ’nferno Giovenale, che la tua affezion mi fé palese, mia benvoglienza inverso te fu quale più strinse mai di non vista persona, sì ch’or mi parran corte queste scale.
From the hour that Juvenal came down among us in the limbo of Hell, and your affection was made known to me, my good will towards you has been such as one has never felt for a person unseen, so that these stairs will now seem short to me.
Ma dimmi, e come amico mi perdona se troppa sicurtà m’allarga il freno, e come amico omai meco ragiona: come poté trovar dentro al tuo seno loco avarizia, tra cotanto senno di quanto per tua cura fosti pieno?».
But tell me, and as a friend, forgive me if too much confidence loosens my rein, and speak with me now as a friend: how could avarice find a place within your breast, amidst so much wisdom as you were filled with due to your own care?
Queste parole Stazio mover fenno un poco a riso pria; poscia rispuose: «Ogne tuo dir d’amor m’è caro cenno. Veramente più volte appaion cose che danno a dubitar falsa matera per le vere ragion che son nascose.
These words made Statius smile a little at first; then he replied: "Every word of your love is a dear sign to me. Truly, things appear many times that give false matter for doubt because of the hidden true reasons."
La tua dimanda tuo creder m’avvera esser ch’i’ fossi avaro in l’altra vita, forse per quella cerchia dov’ io era. Or sappi ch’avarizia fu partita troppo da me, e questa dismisura migliaia di lunari hanno punita.
Your question confirms your beliefthat I was a miser in the other life,perhaps in that circle where I was.Now know that avarice was completely banishedfrom me, and this immoderate sinhas been punished for thousands of lunar cycles.
E se non fosse ch’io drizzai mia cura, quand’ io intesi là dove tu chiame, crucciato quasi a l’umana natura: ‘Per che non reggi tu, o sacra fame de l’oro, l’appetito de’ mortali?’, voltando sentirei le giostre grame.
And if I hadn't corrected my concerns, when I heard the place you called, almost angered at human nature: "Why don't you rule, O sacred hunger for gold, the appetite of mortals?", I would still be experiencing the bitter games.
Allor m’accorsi che troppo aprir l’ali potean le mani a spendere, e pente’mi così di quel come de li altri mali. Quanti risurgeran coi crini scemi per ignoranza, che di questa pecca toglie ’l penter vivendo e ne li stremi!
Then I realized my hands had opened too wide to spend, and I repented of that, as of the other evils. How many will rise again with their hair shorn in ignorance, for this sin takes away repentance in life and at the end!
E sappie che la colpa che rimbecca per dritta opposizione alcun peccato, con esso insieme qui suo verde secca; però, s’io son tra quella gente stato che piange l’avarizia, per purgarmi, per lo contrario suo m’è incontrato».
And know that the sin that directly opposes another sin withers its green along with it; therefore, if I have been among those people who weep for avarice, I have encountered its opposite here in order to purify myself.
«Or quando tu cantasti le crude armi de la doppia trestizia di Giocasta», disse ’l cantor de’ buccolici carmi, «per quello che Clïò teco lì tasta, non par che ti facesse ancor fedele la fede, sanza qual ben far non basta.
“Or when you sang the cruel arms of Jocasta’s double sorrow,” said the singer of bucolic poems, “from what Clio then tasted with you, it does not seem that faith made you faithful yet, without which doing good is not enough.”
Se così è, qual sole o quai candele ti stenebraron sì, che tu drizzasti poscia di retro al pescator le vele?». Ed elli a lui: «Tu prima m’invïasti verso Parnaso a ber ne le sue grotte, e prima appresso Dio m’alluminasti.
If that’s the way it was, what sun or candles dispelled the shadows, so that you later steered your sails behind the fisherman?” And he to him, “You first sent me toward Parnassus to drink within its grottoes, and you first enlightened me in God’s presence.”
Facesti come quei che va di notte, che porta il lume dietro e sé non giova, ma dopo sé fa le persone dotte, quando dicesti: ‘Secol si rinova; torna giustizia e primo tempo umano, e progenïe scende da ciel nova’.
You acted like someone who travels by night, carrying the lamp behind him, and it does him no good, but it enlightens others after him, when you said: ‘The world is renewed; justice and the first age of humanity return, and a new generation descends from Heaven.’
Per te poeta fui, per te cristiano: ma perché veggi mei ciò ch’io disegno, a colorare stenderò la mano. Già era ’l mondo tutto quanto pregno de la vera credenza, seminata per li messaggi de l’etterno regno;
Through you I was a poet, through you a Christian: but so that you may see better what I depict, I will extend my hand to color it. Already the whole world was pregnant with the true belief, sown by the messengers of the eternal kingdom;
e la parola tua sopra toccata si consonava a’ nuovi predicanti; ond’ io a visitarli presi usata. Vennermi poi parendo tanto santi, che, quando Domizian li perseguette, sanza mio lagrimar non fur lor pianti;
and your word, once touched upon,harmonized with the new preachers;wherefore, I grew accustomed to visiting them.Then, they seemed to me so holy,that when Domitian persecuted them,I could not help but weep at their suffering;
e mentre che di là per me si stette, io li sovvenni, e i lor dritti costumi fer dispregiare a me tutte altre sette. E pria ch’io conducessi i Greci a’ fiumi di Tebe poetando, ebb’ io battesmo; ma per paura chiuso cristian fu’mi,
And while I remained there, I aided them, and their righteous customs caused me to despise all other sects. And before I led the Greeks to the rivers of Thebes through my poetry, I received baptism; but, out of fear, I remained a closet Christian.
lungamente mostrando paganesmo; e questa tepidezza il quarto cerchio cerchiar mi fé più che ’l quarto centesmo. Tu dunque, che levato hai il coperchio che m’ascondeva quanto bene io dico, mentre che del salire avem soverchio,
...for a long time feigning paganism, and this lukewarmness made me circle the fourth realm, more than a fourth of a hundred times. You, then, who have lifted the cover that hid from me the good I speak of, while we have ample time to ascend...
dimmi dov’ è Terrenzio nostro antico, Cecilio e Plauto e Varro, se lo sai: dimmi se son dannati, e in qual vico». «Costoro e Persio e io e altri assai», rispuose il duca mio, «siam con quel Greco che le Muse lattar più ch’altri mai,
"Tell me where our ancient Terence is, and Caecilius, and Plautus, and Varro, if you know: tell me if they are damned, and in what circle.""These, and Persius, and I, and many others," my guide replied, "are with that Greek whom the Muses nursed more than any other."
nel primo cinghio del carcere cieco; spesse fïate ragioniam del monte che sempre ha le nutrice nostre seco. Euripide v’è nosco e Antifonte, Simonide, Agatone e altri piùe Greci che già di lauro ornar la fronte.
In the first circle of the blind prison; often we speak of the mountain that always has our nurses with it. Euripides is there with us, and Antiphon, Simonides, Agathon, and many other Greeks who once adorned their brows with laurel.
Quivi si veggion de le genti tue Antigone, Deïfile e Argia, e Ismene sì trista come fue. Védeisi quella che mostrò Langia; èvvi la figlia di Tiresia, e Teti, e con le suore sue Deïdamia».
Here you see people of your time: Antigone, Deiphile, and Argia, and Ismene, as sorrowful as she ever was. There is she who showed Langia; there is Tiresias's daughter, and Thetis, and with her sisters, Deidamia."
Tacevansi ambedue già li poeti, di novo attenti a riguardar dintorno, liberi da saliri e da pareti; e già le quattro ancelle eran del giorno rimase a dietro, e la quinta era al temo, drizzando pur in sù l’ardente corno,
The two poets had already fallen silent, newly attentive to look around, free of the ascent and the walls. The four handmaidens of the day had already remained behind, and the fifth was at the helm, directing its fiery horn upwards.
quando il mio duca: «Io credo ch’a lo stremo le destre spalle volger ne convegna, girando il monte come far solemo». Così l’usanza fu lì nostra insegna, e prendemmo la via con men sospetto per l’assentir di quell’ anima degna.
Then my guide said: "I believe that to the edgewe must turn our right shoulders,circling the mountain as we are accustomed." Thus our custom served as a guide there,and we took the path with less concern,due to the consent of that worthy soul.
Elli givan dinanzi, e io soletto di retro, e ascoltava i lor sermoni, ch’a poetar mi davano intelletto. Ma tosto ruppe le dolci ragioni un alber che trovammo in mezza strada, con pomi a odorar soavi e buoni;
They went on ahead, and I alonebehind, and I listened to their words,which gave my mind the power to poetize. But soon the sweet discourse was brokenby a tree that we found in the middle of the path,with fruits that smelled sweet and good.
e come abete in alto si digrada di ramo in ramo, così quello in giuso, cred’ io, perché persona sù non vada. Dal lato onde ’l cammin nostro era chiuso, cadea de l’alta roccia un liquor chiaro e si spandeva per le foglie suso.
And as a fir tree tapers at its peakfrom branch to branch, so did that one descend,I think, so that no person might ascend.From the side where our path was shut,a clear liquid fell from the high rockand spread across the upper leaves.
Li due poeti a l’alber s’appressaro; e una voce per entro le fronde gridò: «Di questo cibo avrete caro». Poi disse: «Più pensava Maria onde fosser le nozze orrevoli e intere, ch’a la sua bocca, ch’or per voi risponde.
The two poets approached the tree, and a voice from within the branches cried, "You will cherish this food." Then it said, "Mary thought more about how the wedding would be honorable and complete, than her own mouth, which now responds for you."
E le Romane antiche, per lor bere, contente furon d’acqua; e Danïello dispregiò cibo e acquistò savere. Lo secol primo, quant’ oro fu bello, fé savorose con fame le ghiande, e nettare con sete ogne ruscello.
And ancient Romans, for their thirst, were content with water; and Daniel scorned food and acquired wisdom. The first age, when gold was beautiful, made acorns savory with hunger, and every stream a nectar with thirst.
Mele e locuste furon le vivande che nodriro il Batista nel diserto; per ch’elli è glorïoso e tanto grande quanto per lo Vangelio v’è aperto».
Locusts and honey were the victualsThat nourished the Baptist in the desert;Wherefore he is glorious and so greatAs the Gospel reveals to you.
