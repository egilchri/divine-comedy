Purgatorio * Canto I

Dante and Virgil emerge from the darkness of Hell onto the shore of an island at dawn. In the sky, Dante observes a constellation of four stars not visible in the northern hemisphere. They are then confronted by a solitary, reverend old man with a long white beard, Cato, the island's guardian. Cato questions how they have escaped from the "eternal prison." Virgil explains that their journey is divinely willed, prompted by a lady from Heaven, and that Dante is still alive. Satisfied by this authority, Cato instructs Virgil to prepare Dante for the ascent of Mount Purgatory. He must first wash the stains of Hell from Dante's face with the morning dew and then gird him with a simple reed from the shore. After Cato vanishes, Virgil cleanses Dante's face and then plucks a reed, which he fastens around Dante's waist. Instantly, another reed grows in the exact spot.
Per correr miglior acque alza le vele omai la navicella del mio ingegno, che lascia dietro a sé mar sì crudele; e canterò di quel secondo regno dove l’umano spirito si purga e di salire al ciel diventa degno.
To navigate better waters, my intellect's little ship now raises its sails, leaving behind such a cruel sea. And I will sing of that second kingdom where the human spirit purges itself and becomes worthy to ascend to heaven.
Ma qui la morta poesì resurga, o sante Muse, poi che vostro sono; e qui Calïopè alquanto surga, seguitando il mio canto con quel suono di cui le Piche misere sentiro lo colpo tal, che disperar perdono.
But here, let the dead poetry rise again, O holy Muses, since I am yours; and here let Calliope somewhat ascend, following my song with that sound that struck the wretched Piche so hard they despaired of pardon.
Dolce color d’orïental zaffiro, che s’accoglieva nel sereno aspetto del mezzo, puro infino al primo giro, a li occhi miei ricominciò diletto, tosto ch’io usci’ fuor de l’aura morta che m’avea contristati li occhi e ’l petto.
The sweet color of an eastern sapphire, which gathered in the serene aspect of the air, pure to the first circle, began to bring delight to my eyes, as soon as I emerged from the dead air that had saddened my eyes and chest.
Lo bel pianeto che d’amar conforta faceva tutto rider l’orïente, velando i Pesci ch’erano in sua scorta. I’ mi volsi a man destra, e puosi mente a l’altro polo, e vidi quattro stelle non viste mai fuor ch’a la prima gente.
Behold, the lovely planet, which comforts with love, made all the Orient smile, veiling the Fish that were its escort. I turned to the right and paid attention to the other pole, and saw four stars, never seen except by the first people.
Goder pareva ’l ciel di lor fiammelle: oh settentrïonal vedovo sito, poi che privato se’ di mirar quelle! Com’ io da loro sguardo fui partito, un poco me volgendo a l ’altro polo, là onde ’l Carro già era sparito,
God seemed to gleam from their flames; oh northern void, bereft, since you are kept from seeing them! As I turned away from their sight, shifting slightly toward the other pole, where the Wagon had already vanished,
vidi presso di me un veglio solo, degno di tanta reverenza in vista, che più non dee a padre alcun figliuolo. Lunga la barba e di pel bianco mista portava, a’ suoi capelli simigliante, de’ quai cadeva al petto doppia lista.
I saw near me an old man, alone, deserving of such reverence in appearance that no son owes more to a father. He wore a long beard, mixed with white hair, like his own locks, which fell in two strands upon his chest.
Li raggi de le quattro luci sante fregiavan sì la sua faccia di lume, ch’i’ ’l vedea come ’l sol fosse davante. «Chi siete voi che contro al cieco fiume fuggita avete la pregione etterna?», diss’ el, movendo quelle oneste piume.
The rays of the four holy lights so adorned his face with radiance that I saw him as if the sun were before me. “Who are you, who have fled against the blind river, the eternal prison?” he said, moving those honest plumes.
«Chi v’ha guidati, o che vi fu lucerna, uscendo fuor de la profonda notte che sempre nera fa la valle inferna? Son le leggi d’abisso così rotte?
"Who has guided you, or what served as your light, emerging from the deep night that perpetually darkens the infernal valley? Have the laws of the abyss been so broken?"

"Or has a new counsel changed in Heaven, that you, the damned, come to my caverns?" Then my guide took hold of me, and with words, with hands, and with gestures, he reverently compelled my legs and my brow.
Poscia rispuose lui: «Da me non venni: donna scese del ciel, per li cui prieghi de la mia compagnia costui sovvenni. Ma da ch’è tuo voler che più si spieghi di nostra condizion com’ ell’ è vera, esser non puote il mio che a te si nieghi.
Then he replied: "I came not of myself; a lady descended from Heaven, by whose prayers I came to assist this man in his plight. But since you desire that I explain further our true condition, my words cannot be withheld from you."
Questi non vide mai l’ultima sera; ma per la sua follia le fu sì presso, che molto poco tempo a volger era. Sì com’ io dissi, fui mandato ad esso per lui campare; e non lì era altra via che questa per la quale i’ mi son messo.
This one never saw the final evening, but due to his folly, it was so close that only a short time remained before it arrived. As I said, I was sent to rescue him, and there was no other path but the one I have taken.
Mostrata ho lui tutta la gente ria; e ora intendo mostrar quelli spirti che purgan sé sotto la tua balìa. Com’ io l’ho tratto, saria lungo a dirti; de l’alto scende virtù che m’aiuta conducerlo a vederti e a udirti.
I have shown him all the damned souls; and now I intend to show those spirits who purify themselves under your charge. How I brought him here would be long to tell you; a power descends from above to help me guide him to see and hear you.
Or ti piaccia gradir la sua venuta: libertà va cercando, ch’è sì cara, come sa chi per lei vita rifiuta. Tu ’l sai, ché non ti fu per lei amara in Utica la morte, ove lasciasti la vesta ch’al gran dì sarà sì chiara.
Now may it please you to welcome his arrival: he seeks freedom, which is so dear, as one knows who gives up life for it. You know it, for death was not bitter for you in Utica, where you left the garment that will be so bright on the great day.
Non son li editti etterni per noi guasti, ché questi vive e Minòs me non lega; ma son del cerchio ove son li occhi casti di Marzia tua, che ’n vista ancor ti priega, o santo petto, che per tua la tegni: per lo suo amore adunque a noi ti piega.
The eternal decrees are not violated for us, since he lives, and Minos does not bind me. I am of the circle where the chaste eyes of your Marcia are, who still implores you in sight, O holy breast, that you hold her as your own. Therefore, bend yourself to us for her love.
Lasciane andar per li tuoi sette regni; grazie riporterò di te a lei, se d’esser mentovato là giù degni». «Marzïa piacque tanto a li occhi miei mentre ch’i’ fu’ di là», diss’ elli allora, «che quante grazie volse da me, fei.
"Allow me to pass through your seven kingdoms; I will bring news of you back to her, if you deem it worthy to be mentioned down there." "Marcia pleased my eyes so much when I was on that side," he then said, "that I did everything she wished of me."
Or che di là dal mal fiume dimora, più muover non mi può, per quella legge che fatta fu quando me n’usci’ fora. Ma se donna del ciel ti move e regge, come tu di’, non c’è mestier lusinghe: bastisi ben che per lei mi richegge.
Now that I dwell beyond the wicked river, I can no longer move, bound by that law established when I left there. But if a woman from Heaven moves and guides you, as you say, there is no need for flattery. It is enough that you ask me on her behalf.
Va dunque, e fa che tu costui ricinghe d’un giunco schietto e che li lavi ’l viso, sì ch’ogne sucidume quindi stinghe; ché non si converria, l’occhio sorpriso d’alcuna nebbia, andar dinanzi al primo ministro, ch’è di quei di paradiso.
Go then, and see that you gird himwith a smooth reed and wash his face,so that all filth is purged from it;for it would not befit him, his eye coveredby some mist, to go before the firstminister, who is of those of paradise.
Questa isoletta intorno ad imo ad imo, là giù colà dove la batte l’onda, porta di giunchi sovra ’l molle limo: null’ altra pianta che facesse fronda o indurasse, vi puote aver vita, però ch’a le percosse non seconda.
This little isle, all around the base,down there where the wave crashes,bears rushes over the soft mud:no other plant that made leavesor grew hard, can have life there,because it does not yield to the blows.
Poscia non sia di qua vostra reddita; lo sol vi mosterrà, che surge omai, prendere il monte a più lieve salita». Così sparì; e io sù mi levai sanza parlare, e tutto mi ritrassi al duca mio, e li occhi a lui drizzai.
Then do not return here; the sun, which now rises, will show you the path to take up the mountain with an easier climb.” Thus he vanished; and I rose without speaking, and drew myself entirely towards my guide, and fixed my eyes upon him.
El cominciò: «Figliuol, segui i miei passi: volgianci in dietro, ché di qua dichina questa pianura a’ suoi termini bassi». L’alba vinceva l’ora mattutina che fuggia innanzi, sì che di lontano conobbi il tremolar de la marina.
He began, “Son, follow in my steps: let us turn back, for this plain slopes downward toward its lower bounds.” Dawn was surpassing the morning hour that fled before it, so that from afar I recognized the trembling of the sea.
Noi andavam per lo solingo piano com’ om che torna a la perduta strada, che ’nfino ad essa li pare ire in vano. Quando noi fummo là ’ve la rugiada pugna col sole, per essere in parte dove, ad orezza, poco si dirada,
We went across the lonely plain like a man returning to a lost road, who until he finds it, thinks his efforts are in vain. When we were there where the dew struggles with the sun, being in a place where, to the breeze, it thins out little,
ambo le mani in su l’erbetta sparte soavemente ’l mio maestro pose: ond’ io, che fui accorto di sua arte, porsi ver’ lui le guance lagrimose; ivi mi fece tutto discoverto quel color che l’inferno mi nascose.
Both hands upon the grass, he then did spread,My master, gently. And I, being awareOf his skill, turned my tear-streaked cheeks to red,And there he made me wholly to declareThat color that Hell had kept me from;unveiled.
Venimmo poi in sul lito diserto, che mai non vide navicar sue acque omo, che di tornar sia poscia esperto. Quivi mi cinse sì com’ altrui piacque: oh maraviglia!
Then we arrived upon the desolate shore, which never saw its waters navigated by any man who later knew how to return. There he girded me as it pleased him: oh, marvel!
