Inferno * Canto XXXII

Dante and Virgil descend to the ninth circle of Hell, Cocytus, a vast, frozen lake. Sinners are trapped in the ice up to their necks, their heads exposed to the frigid air. The poets first see two figures frozen so closely together that their hair is mingled. When Dante questions them, their tears freeze their eyes shut, and they butt their heads together in rage. A nearby soul, Camiscion de’ Pazzi, identifies them as the Alberti brothers. Continuing across the ice, Dante accidentally kicks another sinner in the face. This soul, Bocca degli Abati, refuses to reveal his identity. Dante grabs him by the hair and pulls out several tufts, but he remains silent until another spirit calls out his name. Enraged, Bocca then names other sinners around him. Moving on, Dante and Virgil observe two figures frozen in the same hole, one gnawing savagely on the back of the other's head. Dante asks the aggressor to explain his hatred.
S’ïo avessi le rime aspre e chiocce, come si converrebbe al tristo buco sovra ’l qual pontan tutte l’altre rocce, io premerei di mio concetto il suco più pienamente; ma perch’ io non l’abbo, non sanza tema a dicer mi conduco;
If I possessed the rough and raucous rhymes,As befits the wretched hole to whichAll other rocks converge, I would press the juiceOf my conception more fully; but since I have not,I venture to speak, not without fear;
ché non è impresa da pigliare a gabbo discriver fondo a tutto l’universo, né da lingua che chiami mamma o babbo. Ma quelle donne aiutino il mio verso ch’aiutaro Anfïone a chiuder Tebe, sì che dal fatto il dir non sia diverso.
For it's no undertaking to be mocked,to describe the depths of the universe entire,nor is it a task for a tongue that cries "mama" or "daddy."But let those women aid my verse,who aided Amphion in enclosing Thebes,so that the telling not stray from the deed.
Oh sovra tutte mal creata plebe che stai nel loco onde parlare è duro, mei foste state qui pecore o zebe! Come noi fummo giù nel pozzo scuro sotto i piè del gigante assai più bassi, e io mirava ancora a l’alto muro,
Oh, you wretched rabble, above all others,who dwell in the place where speech is forbidden,better you had been sheep or goats here!When we were down in the dark well,far beneath the giant's feet,and I was still gazing at the high wall,
dicere udi’mi: «Guarda come passi: va sì, che tu non calchi con le piante le teste de’ fratei miseri lassi». Per ch’io mi volsi, e vidimi davante e sotto i piedi un lago che per gelo avea di vetro e non d’acqua sembiante.
I heard him say: "Watch how you walk: go so that you don't tread with your feet on the heads of your wretched, weary brothers." So I turned, and I saw before me and beneath my feet a lake which, due to the ice, had the look of glass and not of water.
Non fece al corso suo sì grosso velo di verno la Danoia in Osterlicchi, né Tanaï là sotto ’l freddo cielo, com’ era quivi; che se Tambernicchi vi fosse sù caduto, o Pietrapana, non avria pur da l’orlo fatto cricchi.
Not even the Danube in Austria ever cloaked its course with such a thick veil of winter, nor did the Tanais under that cold sky, as did this lake. If Mount Tambo or Pietrapana had fallen upon it, it wouldn't have even cracked the edge.
E come a gracidar si sta la rana col muso fuor de l’acqua, quando sogna di spigolar sovente la villana, livide, insin là dove appar vergogna eran l’ombre dolenti ne la ghiaccia, mettendo i denti in nota di cicogna.
And as a frog stays croaking,with its snout above the water, dreamingof gleaning often by the peasant woman,livid, even there where shame appears,the suffering shadows were in the ice,chattering their teeth like a stork's note.
Ognuna in giù tenea volta la faccia; da bocca il freddo, e da li occhi il cor tristo tra lor testimonianza si procaccia. Quand’ io m’ebbi dintorno alquanto visto, volsimi a’ piedi, e vidi due sì stretti, che ’l pel del capo avieno insieme misto.
Each one held their face downwards; from their mouths the cold came, and from their eyes the sad heart sought witness between them. When I had looked around a bit, I turned to the feet, and saw two so close together that the hair of their heads was mixed.
«Ditemi, voi che sì strignete i petti», diss’ io, «chi siete?». E quei piegaro i colli; e poi ch’ebber li visi a me eretti,
"Tell me, you who press yourselves together," I said, "who are you?" And they bent their necks; and after they had raised their faces to me,
li occhi lor, ch’eran pria pur dentro molli, gocciar su per le labbra, e ’l gelo strinse le lagrime tra essi e riserrolli. Con legno legno spranga mai non cinse forte così; ond’ ei come due becchi cozzaro insieme, tanta ira li vinse.
Their eyes, which had been moist within, now dripped onto their lips, and the frost clenched the tears between them, locking them there. Never did wood bind wood with a stronger clasp; thus, they butted against each other like two billy goats, so much did rage overcome them.
E un ch’avea perduti ambo li orecchi per la freddura, pur col viso in giùe, disse: «Perché cotanto in noi ti specchi? Se vuoi saper chi son cotesti due, la valle onde Bisenzo si dichina del padre loro Alberto e di lor fue.
And one who had lost both ears to the cold, face still bowed, said, "Why do you stare so at us? If you want to know who those two are, the valley where the Bisenzio slopes is their father, Alberto, and their origin."
D’un corpo usciro; e tutta la Caina potrai cercare, e non troverai ombra degna più d’esser fitta in gelatina: non quelli a cui fu rotto il petto e l’ombra con esso un colpo per la man d’Artù; non Focaccia; non questi che m’ingombra
From a single body they issued; and throughout all Caina you could search, and you would not find a shade more worthy of being fixed in gelatine: not those whose chests and shadows were broken by a blow from Arthur’s hand; not Focaccia; not this one who troubles me.
col capo sì, ch’i’ non veggio oltre più, e fu nomato Sassol Mascheroni; se tosco se’, ben sai omai chi fu. E perché non mi metti in più sermoni, sappi ch’i’ fu’ il Camiscion de’ Pazzi; e aspetto Carlin che mi scagioni».
Head first, and I see nothing further on, and he was called Sassol Mascheroni; if you are Tuscan, you know well enough who he was. And so you do not put me in more sermons, know that I was Camiscion de’ Pazzi; and I await Carlin to absolve me.”
Poscia vid’ io mille visi cagnazzi fatti per freddo; onde mi vien riprezzo, e verrà sempre, de’ gelati guazzi. E mentre ch’andavamo inver’ lo mezzo al quale ogne gravezza si rauna, e io tremava ne l’etterno rezzo;
Then I saw a thousand faces made like hounds, contorted by the cold; this gives me a shudder, and always will, of frozen wastes. And while we went towards the center to which all weight gathers, and I trembled in the eternal dark.
se voler fu o destino o fortuna, non so; ma, passeggiando tra le teste, forte percossi ’l piè nel viso ad una. Piangendo mi sgridò: «Perché mi peste?
If it was will, destiny, or fortune, I don’t know; but, walking among the heads, I kicked one hard in the face. Weeping, he scolded me: “Why do you trample me?”
se tu non vieni a crescer la vendetta di Montaperti, perché mi moleste?». E io: «Maestro mio, or qui m’aspetta, sì ch’io esca d’un dubbio per costui; poi mi farai, quantunque vorrai, fretta».
"If you haven't come to fuel the revenge of Montaperti, why torment me?" And I: "My master, wait here for me, so I can resolve this question about him; then you can hurry me as much as you like."
Lo duca stette, e io dissi a colui che bestemmiava duramente ancora: «Qual se’ tu che così rampogni altrui?». «Or tu chi se’ che vai per l’Antenora, percotendo», rispuose, «altrui le gote, sì che, se fossi vivo, troppo fora?».
The guide stopped, and I said to the one who was still cursing furiously, "Who are you to insult others like that?""And who are you," he replied, "who walks through Antenora, striking others' cheeks, so that, if you were alive, it would be too much?"
«Vivo son io, e caro esser ti puote», fu mia risposta, «se dimandi fama, ch’io metta il nome tuo tra l’altre note». Ed elli a me: «Del contrario ho io brama.
"I am alive, and it may be dear to you," I answered, "if you desire fame, that I put your name among the other notes." And he to me: "I have a desire for the opposite."
Lèvati quinci e non mi dar più lagna, ché mal sai lusingar per questa lama!». Allor lo presi per la cuticagna e dissi: «El converrà che tu ti nomi, o che capel qui sù non ti rimagna».
"Get out of here and stop bothering me, because you don't know how to flatter with this blade!" Then I grabbed him by the scruff of his neck and said, "You must tell me your name, or not a single hair will remain on your head."
Ond’ elli a me: «Perché tu mi dischiomi, né ti dirò ch’io sia, né mosterrolti, se mille fiate in sul capo mi tomi». Io avea già i capelli in mano avvolti, e tratti glien’ avea più d’una ciocca, latrando lui con li occhi in giù raccolti,
And he to me: "Because you tear at me, I will not tell you who I am, nor will I show you, even if you strike me on the head a thousand times." I already had his hair wrapped in my hand, and I had pulled out more than one lock, him yelping with his eyes cast down.
quando un altro gridò: «Che hai tu, Bocca? non ti basta sonar con le mascelle, se tu non latri?
When another one cried, "What's wrong with you, Bocca? Isn't it enough to make noise with your jaws, that you have to bark as well?"
qual diavol ti tocca?». «Omai», diss’ io, «non vo’ che più favelle, malvagio traditor; ch’a la tua onta io porterò di te vere novelle».
"What devil possesses you?" I said. "I don't want you to speak anymore, wicked traitor, because I will bring back true stories about you to your shame."
«Va via», rispuose, «e ciò che tu vuoi conta; ma non tacer, se tu di qua entro eschi, di quel ch’ebbe or così la lingua pronta. El piange qui l’argento de’ Franceschi: “Io vidi”, potrai dir, “quel da Duera là dove i peccatori stanno freschi”.
"Go away," he answered, "and what you want is what matters; but don't be silent, if you leave here, about the one who had his tongue so ready just now. He weeps here for the Frenchmen's silver: 'I saw,' you can say, 'the one from Duera there where the sinners are kept cool.'"
Se fossi domandato “Altri chi v’era?”, tu hai dallato quel di Beccheria di cui segò Fiorenza la gorgiera. Gianni de’ Soldanier credo che sia più là con Ganellone e Tebaldello, ch’aprì Faenza quando si dormia».
If you were asked, "Who else was there?"You have at your side that one from Beccheria,Whose throat Florence had severed.Gianni de' Soldanieri, I believe, isFurther on with Ganelon and Tebaldello,Who betrayed Faenza while they slept.
Noi eravam partiti già da ello, ch’io vidi due ghiacciati in una buca, sì che l’un capo a l’altro era cappello; e come ’l pan per fame si manduca, così ’l sovran li denti a l’altro pose là ’ve ’l cervel s’aggiugne con la nuca:
We had already moved away when I saw two frozen in a single hole, so that one's head was a hat to the other. And as bread is devoured from hunger, so the one above set his teeth into the other there where the brain joins with the nape of the neck.
non altrimenti Tidëo si rose le tempie a Menalippo per disdegno, che quei faceva il teschio e l’altre cose. «O tu che mostri per sì bestial segno odio sovra colui che tu ti mangi, dimmi ’l perché», diss’ io, «per tal convegno,
In the same way that Tydeus gnawed at the temples of Menalippus, in anger, that one did to the skull and other things. "Oh, you, who demonstrate with such a beastly act the hatred you feel for the one you devour, tell me why," I said, "under such an agreement,
che se tu a ragion di lui ti piangi, sappiendo chi voi siete e la sua pecca, nel mondo suso ancora io te ne cangi, se quella con ch’io parlo non si secca».
that if you lament his fate, knowing who you are and his transgression, I will restore him to the world above, so long as the one I speak with doesn't dry up."

That sinner lifted his mouth from its savage feast, wiping it on the hair of the head he had ravaged from behind.
