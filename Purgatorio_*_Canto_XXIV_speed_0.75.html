Purgatorio * Canto XXIV

As Dante, Virgil, and Statius walk, Dante continues his conversation with the soul of Forese Donati. Forese points out other souls being purged for gluttony, including Bonagiunta da Lucca and Pope Martin IV. Bonagiunta addresses Dante, making a prophecy concerning a woman named Gentucca and inquiring about Dante’s new style of poetry. After this exchange, the group of souls rushes onward. Forese lingers to prophesy the violent death of his brother, Corso, before rejoining them. The three poets continue and find another tree with unreachable fruit. A voice from the branches warns them to keep their distance and cites examples of temperance. Further on, they encounter the radiant Angel of Temperance. The angel directs them to the ascent to the next terrace. As Dante passes, he feels a wing brush his brow, removing the sixth 'P' from his forehead, and he hears the sixth beatitude recited.
Né ’l dir l’andar, né l’andar lui più lento facea, ma ragionando andavam forte, sì come nave pinta da buon vento; e l’ombre, che parean cose rimorte, per le fosse de li occhi ammirazione traean di me, di mio vivere accorte.
Neither speaking nor walking, nor his slower pace, did he change, but reasoning we went forward, like a ship driven by a good wind; and the shadows, which seemed as things re-dead, through the hollows of their eyes, drew forth amazement at me, aware of my living.
E io, continüando al mio sermone, dissi: «Ella sen va sù forse più tarda che non farebbe, per altrui cagione. Ma dimmi, se tu sai, dov’ è Piccarda; dimmi s’io veggio da notar persona tra questa gente che sì mi riguarda».
And I, continuing my speech, said: "She goes upward perhaps more slowly than she would otherwise, for another's reason. But tell me, if you know, where Piccarda is; tell me if I see any noteworthy person among these people who regard me so."
«La mia sorella, che tra bella e buona non so qual fosse più, trïunfa lieta ne l’alto Olimpo già di sua corona». Sì disse prima; e poi: «Qui non si vieta di nominar ciascun, da ch’è sì munta nostra sembianza via per la dïeta.
"My sister, of whom it's hard to say which she was more, beautiful or good, now triumphs happily in the high Olympus, crowned with her glory." She said this first, and then: "Here, no one is forbidden to be named, since our likeness is so attenuated by the diet."
Questi», e mostrò col dito, «è Bonagiunta, Bonagiunta da Lucca; e quella faccia di là da lui più che l’altre trapunta ebbe la Santa Chiesa in le sue braccia: dal Torso fu, e purga per digiuno l’anguille di Bolsena e la vernaccia».
“This one,” and she gestured with her finger, “is Bonagiunta, Bonagiunta of Lucca; and that face over there, more scarred than the others, took Holy Church into its arms. He was from the Torso, and he atones with fasting for the eels of Bolsena and the Vernaccia wine.”
Molti altri mi nomò ad uno ad uno; e del nomar parean tutti contenti, sì ch’io però non vidi un atto bruno. Vidi per fame a vòto usar li denti Ubaldin da la Pila e Bonifazio che pasturò col rocco molte genti.
Many others he named to me, one by one; and in naming them, they all seemed pleased, so that I did not see a single sullen expression. I saw Ubaldin della Pila and Boniface, who pastured many people with his staff, using their teeth in a hunger-driven, empty gnawing.
Vidi messer Marchese, ch’ebbe spazio già di bere a Forlì con men secchezza, e sì fu tal, che non si sentì sazio. Ma come fa chi guarda e poi s’apprezza più d’un che d’altro, fei a quel da Lucca, che più parea di me aver contezza.
I saw Messer Marchese, who once had the spaceto drink at Forlì with less dryness,and was such that he was never sated.But as one who observes and then favorsone more than another, I turned to the one from Lucca,who seemed to have more understanding of me.
El mormorava; e non so che «Gentucca» sentiv’ io là, ov’ el sentia la piaga de la giustizia che sì li pilucca. «O anima», diss’ io, «che par sì vaga di parlar meco, fa sì ch’io t’intenda, e te e me col tuo parlare appaga».
He murmured; and I heard a “Gentucca” there, where he felt the wound of the justice that gnaws at him so. “O soul,” I said, “who seems so eager to speak with me, make it so that I understand you, and that you and I find satisfaction in your words.”
«Femmina è nata, e non porta ancor benda», cominciò el, «che ti farà piacere la mia città, come ch’om la riprenda. Tu te n’andrai con questo antivedere: se nel mio mormorar prendesti errore, dichiareranti ancor le cose vere.
"A woman is born, and still does not wear a veil," he began, "who will make your city pleasing to you, no matter how men disparage it. You shall depart with this premonition: if you erred in my murmuring, the truth will be made clear to you."
Ma dì s’i’ veggio qui colui che fore trasse le nove rime, cominciando ‘Donne ch’avete intelletto d’amore’». E io a lui: «I’ mi son un che, quando Amor mi spira, noto, e a quel modo ch’e’ ditta dentro vo significando».
But tell me if I see here the one who first drew forth the new rhymes, beginning "Ladies who have intelligence of love." And I to him: "I am one who, when Love inspires me, takes note, and in that manner which he dictates within, I go signifying."
«O frate, issa vegg’ io», diss’ elli, «il nodo che ’l Notaro e Guittone e me ritenne di qua dal dolce stil novo ch’i’ odo! Io veggio ben come le vostre penne di retro al dittator sen vanno strette, che de le nostre certo non avvenne;
"O brother," he said, "now I see the knot that held the Notary and Guittone and me back from the sweet new style that I hear! I see well how your pens follow the dictator closely, which certainly never happened with ours;
e qual più a gradire oltre si mette, non vede più da l’uno a l’altro stilo»; e, quasi contentato, si tacette. Come li augei che vernan lungo ’l Nilo, alcuna volta in aere fanno schiera, poi volan più a fretta e vanno in filo,
and whoever strives to please further, sees no more from one style to the other”; and, as if satisfied, he fell silent. As the birds that winter along the Nile, sometimes gather in the air, then fly more swiftly and go in a line,
così tutta la gente che lì era, volgendo ’l viso, raffrettò suo passo, e per magrezza e per voler leggera. E come l’uom che di trottare è lasso, lascia andar li compagni, e sì passeggia fin che si sfoghi l’affollar del casso,

Forese let the holy flock pass by, and came behind me, saying, "When will I see you again?" I replied to him, "I do not know how long I will live, but my return will not be so soon that I will not first be at the shore with my will."

"But the place where I was set to live is, day by day, more and more stripped of its good, and seems destined for wretched ruin." "Go, then," he said; "for I see him who is most at fault dragged by the tail of a beast toward the valley where there is no pardon."
La bestia ad ogne passo va più ratto, crescendo sempre, fin ch’ella il percuote, e lascia il corpo vilmente disfatto. Non hanno molto a volger quelle ruote», e drizzò li occhi al ciel, «che ti fia chiaro ciò che ’l mio dir più dichiarar non puote.
The beast quickens its pace with every step, growing larger until it strikes, leaving the body vilely undone. They don't have much time left to turn those wheels," and he raised his eyes to the heavens, "until what my words cannot further explain becomes clear to you.
Tu ti rimani omai; ché ’l tempo è caro in questo regno, sì ch’io perdo troppo venendo teco sì a paro a paro». Qual esce alcuna volta di gualoppo lo cavalier di schiera che cavalchi, e va per farsi onor del primo intoppo,

Thus he departed from us with greater strides; and I remained on the road with the two who were such great marshals of the world. And when he had entered before us, my eyes followed him, as the mind does his words.

I saw the heavy, lively branches of another fruit tree, not far off, since I had just turned toward them. I saw people beneath it raising their hands and shouting something at the foliage, like children, eager and vain.
che pregano, e ’l pregato non risponde, ma, per fare esser ben la voglia acuta, tien alto lor disio e nol nasconde. Poi si partì sì come ricreduta; e noi venimmo al grande arbore adesso, che tanti prieghi e lagrime rifiuta.
Who pray, and that which is prayed to does not answer, but, to make the longing keener, holds their desire aloft and does not hide it. Then she departed as if disabused; and we came now to the great tree, which refuses so many prayers and tears.
«Trapassate oltre sanza farvi presso: legno è più sù che fu morso da Eva, e questa pianta si levò da esso». Sì tra le frasche non so chi diceva; per che Virgilio e Stazio e io, ristretti, oltre andavam dal lato che si leva.
"Pass onward without drawing near; higher up is the tree that Eve tasted, and this plant sprouted from it." Thus, I know not who was saying among the branches; wherefore, Virgil and Statius and I, compressed together, proceeded onward from the side that ascends.
«Ricordivi», dicea, «d’i maladetti nei nuvoli formati, che, satolli, Tesëo combatter co’ doppi petti; e de li Ebrei ch’al ber si mostrar molli, per che no i volle Gedeon compagni, quando inver’ Madïan discese i colli».
"Recall," he said, "the accursed ones fashioned in the clouds, who, sated, fought Theseus with their double chests; and of the Hebrews who showed themselves faint-hearted at the drinking, for which Gideon would not have them as companions when he descended the hills toward Midian."
Sì accostati a l’un d’i due vivagni passammo, udendo colpe de la gola seguite già da miseri guadagni. Poi, rallargati per la strada sola, ben mille passi e più ci portar oltre, contemplando ciascun sanza parola.
We drew near one of the two banks, and passed along, hearing of the sins of gluttony, sins now followed by wretched gains. Then, widening out, the single path brought us onward a good thousand paces or more, each of us contemplating in silence.
«Che andate pensando sì voi sol tre?». sùbita voce disse; ond’ io mi scossi come fan bestie spaventate e poltre.
“What are you thinking about, you three, all alone?” a sudden voice said. I started, just as beasts do, startled and weary.
Drizzai la testa per veder chi fossi; e già mai non si videro in fornace vetri o metalli sì lucenti e rossi, com’ io vidi un che dicea: «S’a voi piace montare in sù, qui si convien dar volta; quinci si va chi vuole andar per pace».
I lifted my head to see who it was. Never had glass or metal been seen so bright and red in a furnace as the one I saw, who was saying, “If it pleases you to ascend, you must turn this way. This is the path for those who wish to find peace.”
L’aspetto suo m’avea la vista tolta; per ch’io mi volsi dietro a’ miei dottori, com’ om che va secondo ch’elli ascolta. E quale, annunziatrice de li albori, l’aura di maggio movesi e olezza, tutta impregnata da l’erba e da’ fiori;
His appearance had robbed me of sight; thus I turned behind my teachers, like a man who goes only as he hears. And as the breeze of May, herald of the dawn, moves and exhales, entirely laden with grass and with flowers;
tal mi senti’ un vento dar per mezza la fronte, e ben senti’ mover la piuma, che fé sentir d’ambrosïa l’orezza. E senti’ dir: «Beati cui alluma tanto di grazia, che l’amor del gusto nel petto lor troppo disir non fuma,
