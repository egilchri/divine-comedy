Paradiso * Canto XXVIII

In the Ninth Sphere, the Primum Mobile, Dante sees a reflection in Beatrice’s eyes of a light behind him. He turns and beholds a single, intensely bright point of light. Around this point, nine concentric circles of fire spin rapidly. Dante observes that the circle closest to the point moves the fastest, while the circles farther away are progressively slower. He expresses his confusion to Beatrice, noting that this is the inverse of the motion of the physical celestial spheres. Beatrice explains that the heavens and all of nature depend on that central point and that the vision is a direct model of the angelic hierarchies, where speed corresponds to proximity. As Dante understands, the circles flare with light and he hears them sing "Hosanna." Beatrice then identifies the nine circles as the nine orders of angels, naming them from the innermost outward: Seraphim, Cherubim, Thrones, Dominions, Virtues, Powers, Principalities, Archangels, and Angels.

Each spark was followed by its own flame; and there were so many of them that their number exceeded the doubling of the chessboard. I heard hosannas from choir to choir to the fixed point that holds them in place, and will always hold them, where they always were.

And she who saw the doubtful thoughts in my mind said, "The first circles have shown you Seraphim and Cherubim. They follow so swiftly on their threads, in order to resemble the point as much as they can; and they can do so because they are so sublime in sight."

Those other loves that circle them are called Thrones of the divine aspect, and they complete the first trinity; and you should know that they all find delight in proportion to how deeply their sight penetrates into the truth in which every intellect finds rest.
Quinci si può veder come si fonda l’esser beato ne l’atto che vede, non in quel ch’ama, che poscia seconda; e del vedere è misura mercede, che grazia partorisce e buona voglia: così di grado in grado si procede.
Here one can see how blessedness is founded in the act of seeing, not in the act of loving, which comes afterward; and the reward is measured by the seeing, which gives birth to grace and good will: thus one proceeds from grade to grade.
