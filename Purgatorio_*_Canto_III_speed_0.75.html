Purgatorio * Canto III

Following Cato’s rebuke, Dante and Virgil hurry to the base of Mount Purgatory. Dante sees his shadow on the ground but not Virgil's, and he panics, believing he has been abandoned. Virgil reassures him, explaining that as a spirit, his body does not cast a shadow. They arrive at a cliff face that is too steep to climb. While Virgil contemplates a route, Dante spots a group of souls approaching slowly. The spirits are startled when they see Dante’s shadow, a sign that he is a living man. Virgil explains Dante’s mortal status to them. One soul steps forward, a handsome, blond man with battle scars. He identifies himself as Manfred, King of Sicily. He explains that although he died excommunicated, he repented at the last moment. He asks Dante to tell his daughter, Costanza, of his true fate and to request her prayers, which can shorten the time he must wait before beginning his ascent.
Avvegna che la subitana fuga dispergesse color per la campagna, rivolti al monte ove ragion ne fruga, i’ mi ristrinsi a la fida compagna: e come sare’ io sanza lui corso?
Although the sudden flight scattered those across the plain, turning toward the mountain that calls us to reason, I drew closer to my faithful companion: How could I have run without him?
chi m’avria tratto su per la montagna? El mi parea da sé stesso rimorso: o dignitosa coscïenza e netta, come t’è picciol fallo amaro morso!
Who would have pulled me up the mountain? He seemed remorseful on his own: O noble and pure conscience, how small a fault can bite you bitterly!
Quando li piedi suoi lasciar la fretta, che l’onestade ad ogn’ atto dismaga, la mente mia, che prima era ristretta, lo ’ntento rallargò, sì come vaga, e diedi ’l viso mio incontr’ al poggio che ’nverso ’l ciel più alto si dislaga.
When his feet abandoned their haste, which dishonors honesty in every act, my mind, which had been constrained, widened its intent, as if eager, and I turned my face toward the hill that rises highest toward heaven.
Lo sol, che dietro fiammeggiava roggio, rotto m’era dinanzi a la figura, ch’avëa in me de’ suoi raggi l’appoggio. Io mi volsi dallato con paura d’essere abbandonato, quand’ io vidi solo dinanzi a me la terra oscura;
The sun, which blazed red behind me, was broken before the figure that had been the support for its rays within me. I turned to the side in fear of being abandoned, when I saw only the dark earth before me.
e ’l mio conforto: «Perché pur diffidi?», a dir mi cominciò tutto rivolto; «non credi tu me teco e ch’io ti guidi? Vespero è già colà dov’ è sepolto lo corpo dentro al quale io facea ombra; Napoli l’ha, e da Brandizio è tolto.
And my comfort began to say, turning completely toward me: "Why do you still doubt?" "Don't you believe I am with you, and that I guide you? Vespers have already come where the body is buried, the body in which I cast a shadow; Naples has it, and it was taken from Brindisi."
Ora, se innanzi a me nulla s’aombra, non ti maravigliar più che d’i cieli che l’uno a l’altro raggio non ingombra. A sofferir tormenti, caldi e geli simili corpi la Virtù dispone che, come fa, non vuol ch’a noi si sveli.
Now, if nothing casts a shadow before me, do not be more amazed than at the heavens, where one ray does not impede the other. To endure torments, heat and cold, a similar virtue disposes these bodies, and how it does so, it does not wish to reveal to us.
Matto è chi spera che nostra ragione possa trascorrer la infinita via che tiene una sustanza in tre persone. State contenti, umana gente, al quia; ché, se potuto aveste veder tutto, mestier non era parturir Maria;
Mad is he who hopes that our reasoncan traverse the infinite paththat holds one substance in three persons.Be content, O human folk, with the *quia*;for, if you had been able to see all,Mary would not have needed to give birth.

and you saw those who desired without fruit,such that their desire would be quieted,which eternally is given to them as sorrow:I speak of Aristotle and Platoand of many others”; and here he bowed his face,and said no more, and remained troubled.
Noi divenimmo intanto a piè del monte; quivi trovammo la roccia sì erta, che ’ndarno vi sarien le gambe pronte. Tra Lerice e Turbìa la più diserta, la più rotta ruina è una scala, verso di quella, agevole e aperta.
We arrived, meanwhile, at the mountain's base; there we found the rock so steep that legs would strain in vain. Between Lerici and Turbia, the most desolate, the most ruined path is a staircase, compared to this one, open and accessible.
«Or chi sa da qual man la costa cala», disse ’l maestro mio fermando ’l passo, «sì che possa salir chi va sanz’ ala?». E mentre ch’e’ tenendo ’l viso basso essaminava del cammin la mente, e io mirava suso intorno al sasso,
“Now, who knows from what side the slope descends,”my master said, halting his pace,“so that one without wings might ascend?”And while he, holding his face downward,examined the path in his mind,I gazed upward, around the stone.
da man sinistra m’apparì una gente d’anime, che movieno i piè ver’ noi, e non pareva, sì venïan lente. «Leva», diss’ io, «maestro, li occhi tuoi: ecco di qua chi ne darà consiglio, se tu da te medesmo aver nol puoi».
Then, on the left, a gathering of souls appeared to me, moving their feet toward us, so slowly that they seemed not to be moving at all. "Look," I said, "Master, look over there: they will give us advice, if you can't find it within yourself."
Guardò allora, e con libero piglio rispuose: «Andiamo in là, ch’ei vegnon piano; e tu ferma la spene, dolce figlio». Ancora era quel popol di lontano, i’ dico dopo i nostri mille passi, quanto un buon gittator trarria con mano,
He looked then, and with a free spirit, responded: "Let's go over there, for they come slowly; and you, my sweet son, keep hope alive." The people were still far off, I say, after our thousand paces, about as far as a good thrower could cast with his hand,
quando si strinser tutti ai duri massi de l’alta ripa, e stetter fermi e stretti com’ a guardar, chi va dubbiando, stassi. «O ben finiti, o già spiriti eletti», Virgilio incominciò, «per quella pace ch’i’ credo che per voi tutti s’aspetti,
when they all pressed against the hard rocksof the high bank, and stood still and tight,like one who hesitates to watch, they stay."O you who are well-finished, o spirits now elect,"Virgil began, "by that peacethat I believe is expected by you all,

e ciò che fa la prima, e l’altre fanno, addossandosi a lei, s’ella s’arresta, semplici e quete, e lo ’mperché non sanno; sì vid’ io muovere a venir la testa di quella mandra fortunata allotta, pudica in faccia e ne l’andare onesta.
and what the first does, the others do as well, pressing against her, if she stops, simple and still, and do not know the why; so I saw the head of that fortunate flock move forward then, modest in face and honest in their gait.
Come color dinanzi vider rotta la luce in terra dal mio destro canto, sì che l’ombra era da me a la grotta, restaro, e trasser sé in dietro alquanto, e tutti li altri che venieno appresso, non sappiendo ’l perché, fenno altrettanto.
When they saw the light broken ahead, cast down onto the ground by my right side, so that the shadow lay towards the grotto, they stopped and drew back a bit, and all the others coming up behind, not knowing why, did the same.
«Sanza vostra domanda io vi confesso che questo è corpo uman che voi vedete; per che ’l lume del sole in terra è fesso. Non vi maravigliate, ma credete che non sanza virtù che da ciel vegna cerchi di soverchiar questa parete».
“Without you asking, I confess to you that this is a human body you see; that's why the sun's light is broken on the ground. Don't be amazed, but believe that it seeks to overcome this barrier not without a power that comes from heaven.”
Così ’l maestro; e quella gente degna «Tornate», disse, «intrate innanzi dunque», coi dossi de le man faccendo insegna. E un di loro incominciò: «Chiunque tu se’, così andando, volgi ’l viso: pon mente se di là mi vedesti unque».
Thus spoke the master; and to those worthy people he said, "Turn back, then come forward," gesturing with the backs of his hands. And one of them began: "Whoever you are, as you walk, turn your face: consider if you ever saw me there."
Io mi volsi ver’ lui e guardail fiso: biondo era e bello e di gentile aspetto, ma l’un de’ cigli un colpo avea diviso. Quand’ io mi fui umilmente disdetto d’averlo visto mai, el disse: «Or vedi»; e mostrommi una piaga a sommo ’l petto.
I turned towards him and stared intently. He was blonde and handsome, with a noble appearance, but a wound had split one of his eyebrows. When I humbly denied ever having seen him, he said, “Now, look,” and showed me a wound on his upper chest.
Poi sorridendo disse: «Io son Manfredi, nepote di Costanza imperadrice; ond’ io ti priego che, quando tu riedi, vadi a mia bella figlia, genitrice de l’onor di Cicilia e d’Aragona, e dichi ’l vero a lei, s’altro si dice.
Then, smiling, he said: “I am Manfred, grandson of the Empress Constance. Therefore, I pray that when you return, you go to my beautiful daughter, mother to the honor of Sicily and Aragon, and tell her the truth, if other things are said.”
Poscia ch’io ebbi rotta la persona di due punte mortali, io mi rendei, piangendo, a quei che volontier perdona. Orribil furon li peccati miei; ma la bontà infinita ha sì gran braccia, che prende ciò che si rivolge a lei.
After I had broken my body with two mortal wounds, I surrendered myself, weeping, to Him who pardons willingly. Horrible were my sins; but infinite goodness has such great arms that it receives whatever turns to it.
Se ’l pastor di Cosenza, che a la caccia di me fu messo per Clemente allora, avesse in Dio ben letta questa faccia, l’ossa del corpo mio sarieno ancora in co del ponte presso a Benevento, sotto la guardia de la grave mora.
If the shepherd of Cosenza, who was sent after me by Clement then, had read this face of mine well in God, the bones of my body would still be in the mouth of the bridge near Benevento, under the guard of the heavy stone.
Or le bagna la pioggia e move il vento di fuor dal regno, quasi lungo ’l Verde, dov’ e’ le trasmutò a lume spento. Per lor maladizion sì non si perde, che non possa tornar, l’etterno amore, mentre che la speranza ha fior del verde.
Now the rain soaks them and the wind stirs them, outside the realm, almost along the Verde, where he changed them in the dim light. Because of their curse, eternal love is not so lost that it cannot return, as long as hope has the flower of green.
Vero è che quale in contumacia more di Santa Chiesa, ancor ch’al fin si penta, star li convien da questa ripa in fore, per ognun tempo ch’elli è stato, trenta, in sua presunzïon, se tal decreto più corto per buon prieghi non diventa.
It is true that whoever dies excommunicated from Holy Church, even if they repent at the end, must remain outside this shore for thirty times the length of time they were presumptuous, unless this decree is shortened by good prayers.
Vedi oggimai se tu mi puoi far lieto, revelando a la mia buona Costanza come m’hai visto, e anco esto divieto; ché qui per quei di là molto s’avanza».
See now if you can make me joyful, revealing to my good Costanza how you have seen me, and also this prohibition; because here, for those on the other side, much is gained.
