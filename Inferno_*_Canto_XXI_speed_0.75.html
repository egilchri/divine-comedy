Inferno * Canto XXI

Dante and Virgil arrive at the fifth pouch of the eighth circle, observing a ditch filled with boiling black pitch. Virgil alerts Dante to a black demon running toward them along the ridge. The demon carries a sinner, identified as an elder of Lucca, and throws him into the pitch before departing. Other demons, called the Malebranche, emerge from beneath the bridge and use hooks to force the struggling sinner back under the surface. Virgil instructs Dante to hide, then confronts the demons alone. He explains their journey is divinely willed, and the demons’ leader, Malacoda, orders his troop not to harm them. Malacoda informs them that the bridge ahead is broken but offers an escort of ten demons to guide them to another crossing. Despite Dante's expressed fear of the menacing escort, Virgil reassures him, and they set off with the demons, who are led by Barbariccia.

Thus, from bridge to bridge, speaking of things other than those my comedy cares to sing, we came; and we were at the peak when we stopped to see the other fissure of Malebolge and the other vain laments; and I saw it was marvelously dark.

His shoulder, sharp and haughty, pressed down on a sinner with both haunches, while he gripped the sinew of the foot with his talons. He said, of our bridge, "Oh, Malebranche, here's one of the elders of Santa Zita!"

The good master said, "So that it's not obvious you're with us, crouch down behind a rock, where you'll have some cover. And don't be afraid, no matter what they do to me, because I've been through this before. I've been in this kind of fight already."

Per ch’io mi mossi e a lui venni ratto; e i diavoli si fecer tutti avanti, sì ch’io temetti ch’ei tenesser patto; così vid’ ïo già temer li fanti ch’uscivan patteggiati di Caprona, veggendo sé tra nemici cotanti.
Whereupon I moved and quickly went to him; and all the devils surged forward, so that I feared they would break their agreement. As I did, I saw the infantry once fear, when they marched out of Caprona under treaty, seeing themselves surrounded by so many enemies.
I’ m’accostai con tutta la persona lungo ’l mio duca, e non torceva li occhi da la sembianza lor ch’era non buona. Ei chinavan li raffi e «Vuo’ che ’l tocchi», diceva l’un con l’altro, «in sul groppone?».
I drew near my guide, keeping close to him, and he did not avert his eyes from their wicked visages. They lowered their hooks, and one said to the other, "Shall I give him a taste of them on his rump?"

Then he said to us: "You cannot go any farther along this crag, because the sixth arch is lying entirely broken at the bottom. And if you still wish to go forward, go up through this grotto; nearby is another crag that makes a way."
