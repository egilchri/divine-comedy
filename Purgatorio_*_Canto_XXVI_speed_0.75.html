Purgatorio * Canto XXVI

On the seventh terrace, Dante and Virgil proceed along a path engulfed in flames. The souls of the lustful notice that Dante’s physical body casts a shadow on the fire, and one of them asks him to explain his presence. Before Dante can answer, a second group of souls approaches from the opposite direction. The two groups briefly greet each other with a kiss. As they separate, one group shouts "Sodom and Gomorrah," and the other cries out a reference to Pasiphaë. The soul who first spoke to Dante then identifies himself as the poet Guido Guinizzelli. He explains that his group is being punished for heterosexual lust and the other for homosexual lust. Overcome with admiration, Dante speaks with Guinizzelli, who in turn points out another soul he declares a "better craftsman," the troubadour Arnaut Daniel. After asking for prayers, Guinizzelli vanishes. Dante then addresses Arnaut, who speaks in his native Provençal, asks to be remembered, and disappears back into the purifying fire.
Mentre che sì per l’orlo, uno innanzi altro, ce n’andavamo, e spesso il buon maestro diceami: «Guarda: giovi ch’io ti scaltro»; feriami il sole in su l’omero destro, che già, raggiando, tutto l’occidente mutava in bianco aspetto di cilestro;
While we were going along the edge, one before the other, and often the good master said to me, "Look: it's good that I am shrewd," the sun struck my right shoulder, which, already, radiating, all of the west was changing into a white aspect of the sky blue.

And I made the shadow, the most scorching one, seem like the flame; and through such a sign, I saw many shadows, as we went, take notice. This was the reason that gave them the beginning to speak of me; and they began to say: "That one does not seem a fictitious body."
poi verso me, quanto potëan farsi, certi si fero, sempre con riguardo di non uscir dove non fosser arsi. «O tu che vai, non per esser più tardo, ma forse reverente, a li altri dopo, rispondi a me che ’n sete e ’n foco ardo.
Then toward me, as close as they could get, certain shades drew near, always being careful not to step outside where they wouldn't be scorched. "Oh, you who go, not in order to be slow, but perhaps reverent, behind the others, answer me, for I burn in thirst and in fire."
Né solo a me la tua risposta è uopo; ché tutti questi n’hanno maggior sete che d’acqua fredda Indo o Etïopo. Dinne com’ è che fai di te parete al sol, pur come tu non fossi ancora di morte intrato dentro da la rete».
"Your answer isn't just necessary for me,""but for all of these, who thirst for it more deeplythan the Indian or the Ethiopian for cold water.Tell us how it is that you make a wall of yourselfagainst the sun, as though you had yet to enterthe snare of death."
Sì mi parlava un d’essi; e io mi fora già manifesto, s’io non fossi atteso ad altra novità ch’apparve allora; ché per lo mezzo del cammino acceso venne gente col viso incontro a questa, la qual mi fece a rimirar sospeso.
Thus spoke one of them, and I would have answered, had I not been occupied by another novelty that appeared then. For, through the middle of the blazing path, came a group of people facing this one, which caused me to stop and stare.
Lì veggio d’ogne parte farsi presta ciascun’ ombra e basciarsi una con una sanza restar, contente a brieve festa; così per entro loro schiera bruna s’ammusa l’una con l’altra formica, forse a spïar lor via e lor fortuna.
I see shadows on all sides hastening, each one kissing the other, without pause, content with a brief festivity. Thus, within their dusky throng, ant nuzzles ant, perhaps to spy on their path and their fortune.
Tosto che parton l’accoglienza amica, prima che ’l primo passo lì trascorra, sopragridar ciascuna s’affatica: la nova gente: «Soddoma e Gomorra»; e l’altra: «Ne la vacca entra Pasife, perché ’l torello a sua lussuria corra».
As soon as they depart the friendly greeting, before the first step has been taken there, each one strains to shout out: the new people: "Sodom and Gomorrah"; and the others: "Pasiphae enters the cow, so that the little bull might run to her lust."
Poi, come grue ch’a le montagne Rife volasser parte, e parte inver’ l’arene, queste del gel, quelle del sole schife, l’una gente sen va, l’altra sen vene; e tornan, lagrimando, a’ primi canti e al gridar che più lor si convene;
Then, as the flocks that share the Rhiphaean mountainsfly off, some toward the ice, others toward the sands,these shunning the cold, those the sun,one people departs, the other arrives;and they return, weeping, to their first songsand to the cries that suit them best.
e raccostansi a me, come davanti, essi medesmi che m’avean pregato, attenti ad ascoltar ne’ lor sembianti. Io, che due volte avea visto lor grato, incominciai: «O anime sicure d’aver, quando che sia, di pace stato,
And they drew near to me, as before, they themselves who had implored me, intent on listening in their countenances. I, who had twice seen them pleased, began: "O souls assured of having, whenever it may be, a state of peace,
non son rimase acerbe né mature le membra mie di là, ma son qui meco col sangue suo e con le sue giunture. Quinci sù vo per non esser più cieco; donna è di sopra che m’acquista grazia, per che ’l mortal per vostro mondo reco.
My limbs, beyond, were left neither unripe nor mature, but are here with me, with their blood and with their joints. Hence, I ascend to be no longer blind; a lady above wins me grace, for which I bring my mortal form into your world.
Ma se la vostra maggior voglia sazia tosto divegna, sì che ’l ciel v’alberghi ch’è pien d’amore e più ampio si spazia, ditemi, acciò ch’ancor carte ne verghi, chi siete voi, e chi è quella turba che se ne va di retro a’ vostri terghi».
But if your greatest desire is soon satisfied, so that Heaven may shelter you, which is filled with love and expands more widely, tell me, so that I may still write it down, who are you, and who is that crowd that goes behind your backs.”
Non altrimenti stupido si turba lo montanaro, e rimirando ammuta, quando rozzo e salvatico s’inurba, che ciascun’ ombra fece in sua paruta; ma poi che furon di stupore scarche, lo qual ne li alti cuor tosto s’attuta,
Like a mountaineer, bewildered and mute, when he first enters the city, crude and uncouth, so each shade appeared in that gaze. But when they were relieved of their wonder, which quickly fades in the noble heart,
«Beato te, che de le nostre marche», ricominciò colei che pria m’inchiese, «per morir meglio, esperïenza imbarche! La gente che non vien con noi, offese di ciò per che già Cesar, trïunfando, “Regina” contra sé chiamar s’intese:
“Blessed are you, who from our lands,” recommenced she who first questioned me, “in order to die better, set forth an experience! The people who do not come with us, offended by that for which Caesar, triumphant, was once called ‘Queen’ against himself:
però si parton “Soddoma” gridando, rimproverando a sé com’ hai udito, e aiutan l’arsura vergognando. Nostro peccato fu ermafrodito; ma perché non servammo umana legge, seguendo come bestie l’appetito,
yet, crying "Sodom," they depart, rebuking themselves, as you have heard, and aiding their burning, ashamed. Our sin was hermaphroditic; but because we did not keep human law, following our appetites like beasts,
in obbrobrio di noi, per noi si legge, quando partinci, il nome di colei che s’imbestiò ne le ’mbestiate schegge. Or sai nostri atti e di che fummo rei: se forse a nome vuo’ saper chi semo, tempo non è di dire, e non saprei.
In disgrace to us, it is written of us, when we departed, the name of her who became bestial in the bestial shards. Now you know our acts and of what we were guilty: if perhaps you want to know who we are by name, there is no time to say, and I would not know.
Farotti ben di me volere scemo: son Guido Guinizzelli, e già mi purgo per ben dolermi prima ch’a lo stremo». Quali ne la tristizia di Ligurgo si fer due figli a riveder la madre, tal mi fec’ io, ma non a tanto insurgo,
Good wishes diminished my desire for him: I am Guido Guinizzelli, and already I purify myself by grieving well before the end.” As in the sadness of Lycurgus, two sons became to see their mother, so did I, but I do not rise up so much,
quand’ io odo nomar sé stesso il padre mio e de li altri miei miglior che mai rime d’amore usar dolci e leggiadre; e sanza udire e dir pensoso andai lunga fïata rimirando lui, né, per lo foco, in là più m’appressai.
When I hear my father name himself, and the fathersof my other betters who everused sweet and graceful rhymes of love; and without hearing or speaking, I went thoughtfullyfor a long time, looking at him,nor, because of the fire, did I draw any closer.
Poi che di riguardar pasciuto fui, tutto m’offersi pronto al suo servigio con l’affermar che fa credere altrui. Ed elli a me: «Tu lasci tal vestigio, per quel ch’i’ odo, in me, e tanto chiaro, che Letè nol può tòrre né far bigio.
After I was sated with looking,I offered myself entirely for his service,with the affirmation that makes others believe.And he said to me: "You leave such a trace,from what I hear, in me, and so clear,that Lethe cannot erase it, nor make it dim."
Ma se le tue parole or ver giuraro, dimmi che è cagion per che dimostri nel dire e nel guardar d’avermi caro». E io a lui: «Li dolci detti vostri, che, quanto durerà l’uso moderno, faranno cari ancora i loro incostri».
But if your words have now sworn true, tell me the reason you demonstrate, in your speech and your gaze, that you hold me dear." And I to him: "Your sweet words, which, as long as modern usage lasts, will still make their imprints precious."
«O frate», disse, «questi ch’io ti cerno col dito», e additò un spirto innanzi, «fu miglior fabbro del parlar materno. Versi d’amore e prose di romanzi soverchiò tutti; e lascia dir li stolti che quel di Lemosì credon ch’avanzi.
“Oh brother,” he said, “this one, whom I point out to you with my finger,” and he indicated a spirit ahead, “was a better craftsman of the mother tongue. In verses of love and prose of romance, he surpassed all others; let the foolish speak who believe the one from Limoges excels.”
A voce più ch’al ver drizzan li volti, e così ferman sua oppinïone prima ch’arte o ragion per lor s’ascolti. Così fer molti antichi di Guittone, di grido in grido pur lui dando pregio, fin che l’ha vinto il ver con più persone.
They turn their faces to rumor more than to truth, and thus they fix their opinion before art or reason can be heard by them. So many of Guittone's ancients did, giving him praise from cry to cry, until truth with more people has vanquished him.
Or se tu hai sì ampio privilegio, che licito ti sia l’andare al chiostro nel quale è Cristo abate del collegio, falli per me un dir d’un paternostro, quanto bisogna a noi di questo mondo, dove poter peccar non è più nostro».
Now if you have such a great privilege, that it is permitted for you to go to the cloister in which Christ is abbot of the college, say for me a *pater noster*, as much as we need in this world, where it is no longer ours to sin.
Poi, forse per dar luogo altrui secondo che presso avea, disparve per lo foco, come per l’acqua il pesce andando al fondo. Io mi fei al mostrato innanzi un poco, e dissi ch’al suo nome il mio disire apparecchiava grazïoso loco.
Then, perhaps to give way to another, since he was near, he vanished into the fire, as a fish goes to the bottom through water. I drew near to the one who had been pointed out to me, and said that for his name my desire was preparing a gracious place.
El cominciò liberamente a dire: «Tan m’abellis vostre cortes deman, qu’ieu no me puesc ni voill a vos cobrire. Ieu sui Arnaut, que plor e vau cantan; consiros vei la passada folor, e vei jausen lo joi qu’esper, denan.
He began freely to speak:“Your courteous demand so pleases me, that I cannot nor wish to hide myself from you. I am Arnaut, who weeps and goes singing; contrite, I see my past folly, and joyful, I see before me the joy I await.”
Ara vos prec, per aquella valor que vos guida al som de l’escalina, sovenha vos a temps de ma dolor!». Poi s’ascose nel foco che li affina.
Now I beg you, by that virtuewhich guides you to the top of the ladder,remember my suffering in due time!” Then he hid himself in the fire that refines them.
