Inferno * Canto XXIX

As Dante lingers looking back at the ninth ditch, Virgil urges him onward, noting that their time is limited. Dante explains he was looking for a kinsman, Geri del Bello. Virgil states he saw the spirit pass by, making threatening gestures toward Dante, but Dante was distracted at the time. The two poets then arrive at the final ditch of Malebolge, from which arise loud lamentations and a foul stench. They descend to the bank and find the sinners, the Falsifiers, afflicted with horrible diseases. The souls lie in heaps, some on their bellies, others on their backs, while some crawl. Dante and Virgil approach two shades leaning against each other, covered from head to foot in scabs, which they furiously scratch. Virgil asks them if there are any Italians in the ditch. One spirit, Griffolino of Arezzo, identifies himself as an alchemist. The other shade, Capocchio of Siena, confirms he is also an alchemist and tells Dante he should remember him as a talented imitator of nature.
La molta gente e le diverse piaghe avean le luci mie sì inebrïate, che de lo stare a piangere eran vaghe. Ma Virgilio mi disse: «Che pur guate?
The great throng and the various wounds had so intoxicated my eyes that they longed to weep. But Virgil said to me: "Why do you still gaze?"
perché la vista tua pur si soffolge là giù tra l’ombre triste smozzicate? Tu non hai fatto sì a l’altre bolge; pensa, se tu annoverar le credi, che miglia ventidue la valle volge.
Why does your sight linger so down there among the wretched, tattered shades? You haven't gone through the other pouches like this; consider, if you wish to count them, that the valley winds for twenty-two miles.

And already the moon is beneath our feet; the time allotted to us is almost gone, and there is more to see that you do not. "If you had considered," I replied, "the reason for which I was gazing, perhaps you would have excused my lingering."
Parte sen giva, e io retro li andava, lo duca, già faccendo la risposta, e soggiugnendo: «Dentro a quella cava dov’ io tenea or li occhi sì a posta, credo ch’un spirto del mio sangue pianga la colpa che là giù cotanto costa».
Parting then, I followed behind him, the guide, already giving his response and adding: “Within that hollow where I had just been gazing so intently, I believe a spirit of my blood weeps for the sin that costs so dearly down there.”

Then the master said: "Do not let your thoughts dwell on him from here on. Focus on something else, and let him stay there;
ch’io vidi lui a piè del ponticello mostrarti e minacciar forte col dito, e udi’ ’l nominar Geri del Bello. Tu eri allor sì del tutto impedito sovra colui che già tenne Altaforte, che non guardasti in là, sì fu partito».
"For I saw him at the foot of the little bridge, pointing at you and fiercely threatening with his finger. I heard him mention Geri del Bello. You were then so completely preoccupied with the one who once held Altaforte, that you didn't look over there, and he was gone."
«O duca mio, la vïolenta morte che non li è vendicata ancor», diss’ io, «per alcun che de l’onta sia consorte, fece lui disdegnoso; ond’ el sen gio sanza parlarmi, sì com’ ïo estimo: e in ciò m’ha el fatto a sé più pio».
"My guide," I said, "the violent death he suffered, which has not yet been avenged by anyone who shares the shame, made him disdainful. That is why he left without speaking to me, as I believe. And in this, he has shown himself more merciful to me."
Così parlammo infino al loco primo che de lo scoglio l’altra valle mostra, se più lume vi fosse, tutto ad imo. Quando noi fummo sor l’ultima chiostra di Malebolge, sì che i suoi conversi potean parere a la veduta nostra,

Qual sovra ’l ventre e qual sovra le spalle l’un de l’altro giacea, e qual carpone si trasmutava per lo tristo calle. Passo passo andavam sanza sermone, guardando e ascoltando li ammalati, che non potean levar le lor persone.
Some lay upon their bellies, others upon their shoulders, one atop the other, and some were crawling along the wretched path. Step by step, we moved onward without speaking, watching and listening to the afflicted who could not lift themselves.
Io vidi due sedere a sé poggiati, com’ a scaldar si poggia tegghia a tegghia, dal capo al piè di schianze macolati; e non vidi già mai menare stregghia a ragazzo aspettato dal segnorso, né a colui che mal volontier vegghia,

«O tu che con le dita ti dismaglie», cominciò ’l duca mio a l’un di loro, «e che fai d’esse talvolta tanaglie, dinne s’alcun Latino è tra costoro che son quinc’ entro, se l’unghia ti basti etternalmente a cotesto lavoro».
"Oh, you there, who with your fingers are flaying yourself," my guide began, addressing one of them, "and with them sometimes make pincers, tell us if any Latin is among those here within, if your nails are sufficient to this eternal labor."
«Latin siam noi, che tu vedi sì guasti qui ambedue», rispuose l’un piangendo; «ma tu chi se’ che di noi dimandasti?». E ’l duca disse: «I’ son un che discendo con questo vivo giù di balzo in balzo, e di mostrar lo ’nferno a lui intendo».
"We are Latins, as you see, so marred," one of them responded, weeping, "we both are. But who are you, who have questioned us?"And the guide said: "I am one who descends with this living man down from crag to crag, and I intend to show Hell to him."
Allor si ruppe lo comun rincalzo; e tremando ciascuno a me si volse con altri che l’udiron di rimbalzo. Lo buon maestro a me tutto s’accolse, dicendo: «Dì a lor ciò che tu vuoli»; e io incominciai, poscia ch’ei volse:
Then the communal barrier crumbled; and trembling, each turned to me, along with others who heard it echoing. My good master fully embraced me, saying, "Tell them what you wish"; and I began, after he willed it.
«Se la vostra memoria non s’imboli nel primo mondo da l’umane menti, ma s’ella viva sotto molti soli, ditemi chi voi siete e di che genti; la vostra sconcia e fastidiosa pena di palesarvi a me non vi spaventi».
"If your memory doesn't fade, vanishing from the minds of men in the world above, but lives on beneath many suns, tell me who you are and from what people you come; may your unsightly and wretched suffering not frighten you from revealing yourselves to me."
«Io fui d’Arezzo, e Albero da Siena», rispuose l’un, «mi fé mettere al foco; ma quel per ch’io mori’ qui non mi mena. Vero è ch’i’ dissi lui, parlando a gioco: “I’ mi saprei levar per l’aere a volo”; e quei, ch’avea vaghezza e senno poco,
“I was from Arezzo,” one replied, “and Albero da Siena had me burned at the stake. But the reason I died there doesn’t bring me here. It is true that, joking, I said to him, ‘I know how to lift myself into the air by flying;’ and he, who had great desire and little sense,”
volle ch’i’ li mostrassi l’arte; e solo perch’ io nol feci Dedalo, mi fece ardere a tal che l’avea per figliuolo. Ma ne l’ultima bolgia de le diece me per l’alchìmia che nel mondo usai dannò Minòs, a cui fallar non lece».
He wanted me to show him the art. And just because I didn’t become a Daedalus, he had me burned by one who considered him his son. But in the final chasm of the ten, Minos damned me for the alchemy I practiced in the world, to whom it is not permissible to err.”
E io dissi al poeta: «Or fu già mai gente sì vana come la sanese? Certo non la francesca sì d’assai!».
And I said to the poet: "Was there ever a people as vain as the Sienese? Certainly not the French, not by a long shot!"
Onde l’altro lebbroso, che m’intese, rispuose al detto mio: «Tra’mene Stricca che seppe far le temperate spese, e Niccolò che la costuma ricca del garofano prima discoverse ne l’orto dove tal seme s’appicca;
Whereupon the other leper, who heard me, responded to my words: "Except for Stricca, who knew how to spend with moderation, and Niccolò, who first discovered the rich custom of cloves in the garden where such seeds take hold;
e tra’ne la brigata in che disperse Caccia d’Ascian la vigna e la gran fonda, e l’Abbagliato suo senno proferse. Ma perché sappi chi sì ti seconda contra i Sanesi, aguzza ver’ me l’occhio, sì che la faccia mia ben ti risponda:
and then that band in which Caccia d'Asciano squandered his vineyard and his vast estate, and Abbagliato showed his wit. But so you may know who supports you against the Sienese, fix your gaze on me, so that my face will fully inform you:
sì vedrai ch’io son l’ombra di Capocchio, che falsai li metalli con l’alchìmia; e te dee ricordar, se ben t’adocchio, com’ io fui di natura buona scimia».
Thus you will see that I am the shade of Capocchio, who falsified metals with alchemy; and you should remember, if I look at you correctly, how I was a good ape of nature.”
