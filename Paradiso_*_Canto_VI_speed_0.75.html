Paradiso * Canto VI

In the Heaven of Mercury, a shining soul identifies itself as the Emperor Justinian. He recounts his earthly achievements, including the codification of Roman law and his conversion from heresy to orthodox Christianity under the guidance of Pope Agapetus. Justinian then delivers a lengthy monologue tracing the history of the Roman imperial eagle. He follows its path from Aeneas in Troy, through the Roman Kingdom and Republic with figures like Julius Caesar, to the Roman Empire under Augustus, Tiberius, and Charlemagne. He criticizes the contemporary Guelphs and Ghibellines for their partisan abuse of the imperial standard. Finally, Justinian explains that the souls in Mercury were those who pursued earthly fame. He points to another soul, Romeo of Villeneuve, a pilgrim who served Count Raymond Berenger. After arranging for the count’s four daughters to become queens, Romeo was slandered, unjustly accused of mismanagement, and forced to leave the court, ending his life as an impoverished wanderer.
cento e cent’ anni e più l’uccel di Dio ne lo stremo d’Europa si ritenne, vicino a’ monti de’ quai prima uscìo; e sotto l’ombra de le sacre penne governò ’l mondo lì di mano in mano, e, sì cangiando, in su la mia pervenne.
A hundred years and more the bird of Godremained in Europe's westernmost domain,beside the mountains from which it first had trod.And under the shadow of its sacred feathersit governed the world there, from hand to hand,and, changing thus, at last reached mine.
Cesare fui e son Iustinïano, che, per voler del primo amor ch’i’ sento, d’entro le leggi trassi il troppo e ’l vano. E prima ch’io a l’ovra fossi attento, una natura in Cristo esser, non piùe, credea, e di tal fede era contento;
I was Caesar, and now I am Justinian, who, by the will of the first love I felt, removed the excess and the vanity from the laws. And before I turned my mind to this work, I believed, and was content with this faith, that there was only one nature in Christ, no more.
ma ’l benedetto Agapito, che fue sommo pastore, a la fede sincera mi dirizzò con le parole sue. Io li credetti; e ciò che ’n sua fede era, vegg’ io or chiaro sì, come tu vedi ogni contradizione e falsa e vera.
But blessed Agapetus, who was supreme shepherd, directed me to the sincere faith with his words. I believed him; and that which was in his faith, I now see so clearly, as you see every contradiction, false and true.
Tosto che con la Chiesa mossi i piedi, a Dio per grazia piacque di spirarmi l’alto lavoro, e tutto ’n lui mi diedi; e al mio Belisar commendai l’armi, cui la destra del ciel fu sì congiunta, che segno fu ch’i’ dovessi posarmi.
As soon as I set foot with the Church, by God's grace, it pleased Him to inspire me with the great work, and I gave myself to Him entirely; and I entrusted my arms to my Belisarius, whose right hand was so joined with Heaven, that it was a sign that I should rest.
Or qui a la question prima s’appunta la mia risposta; ma sua condizione mi stringe a seguitare alcuna giunta, perché tu veggi con quanta ragione si move contr’ al sacrosanto segno e chi ’l s’appropria e chi a lui s’oppone.
Now who the first question raised, my answer met;but its condition binds me to add some more,that you might see with what good reason it movesagainst the sacred sign, and who claims it,and who opposes it.
Vedi quanta virtù l’ha fatto degno di reverenza; e cominciò da l’ora che Pallante morì per darli regno. Tu sai ch’el fece in Alba sua dimora per trecento anni e oltre, infino al fine che i tre a’ tre pugnar per lui ancora.
See how much virtue made it worthy of reverence; and it began from the hour that Pallas died to give it reign. You know that it made its home in Alba for three hundred years and more, until the end when the three fought the three for it again.
E sai ch’el fé dal mal de le Sabine al dolor di Lucrezia in sette regi, vincendo intorno le genti vicine. Sai quel ch’el fé portato da li egregi Romani incontro a Brenno, incontro a Pirro, incontro a li altri principi e collegi;
And you know what it did from the suffering of the Sabines to the pain of Lucretia, in seven kings, conquering the neighboring peoples. You know what it did, carried by the noble Romans against Brennus, against Pyrrhus, against the other princes and their alliances;
onde Torquato e Quinzio, che dal cirro negletto fu nomato, i Deci e ’ Fabi ebber la fama che volontier mirro. Esso atterrò l’orgoglio de li Aràbi che di retro ad Anibale passaro l’alpestre rocce, Po, di che tu labi.
Hence Torquatus and Quinctius, who was named for his unkempt hair, the Decii and the Fabii achieved the fame that I gladly relate. This same power humbled the pride of the Arabs who, after Hannibal, crossed the alpine rocks, Po, from which you flow.
Sott’ esso giovanetti trïunfaro Scipïone e Pompeo; e a quel colle sotto ’l qual tu nascesti parve amaro. Poi, presso al tempo che tutto ’l ciel volle redur lo mondo a suo modo sereno, Cesare per voler di Roma il tolle.
Beneath them, young men triumphed: Scipio and Pompey; and it seemed bitter to the hill beneath which you were born. Then, close to the time when all of heaven wanted to restore the world to its serene state, Caesar took it, by the will of Rome.
E quel che fé da Varo infino a Reno, Isara vide ed Era e vide Senna e ogne valle onde Rodano è pieno. Quel che fé poi ch’elli uscì di Ravenna e saltò Rubicon, fu di tal volo, che nol seguiteria lingua né penna.
And what he did from the Var to the Rhine, the Isère saw and the Aare and the Seine, and every valley filled by the Rhône. What he did after he left Ravenna and leaped the Rubicon was of such flight that neither tongue nor pen could follow.
Inver’ la Spagna rivolse lo stuolo, poi ver’ Durazzo, e Farsalia percosse sì ch’al Nil caldo si sentì del duolo. Antandro e Simeonta, onde si mosse, rivide e là dov’ Ettore si cuba; e mal per Tolomeo poscia si scosse.
Toward Spain he turned his legion, then to Dyrrachium, and struck at Pharsalus, such that the warm Nile felt grief. He saw again Antandros and the Simois, from whence he set forth, and there where Hector lies; and then he moved against Ptolemy, and it went ill for him.
Da indi scese folgorando a Iuba; onde si volse nel vostro occidente, ove sentia la pompeana tuba. Di quel che fé col baiulo seguente, Bruto con Cassio ne l’inferno latra, e Modena e Perugia fu dolente.
From there, he descended like lightning upon Juba; whence he turned toward your west, where he heard the Pompeian trumpet. Of what he did with the subsequent lackey, Brutus with Cassius in hell howls, and Modena and Perugia were sorrowful.
Piangene ancor la trista Cleopatra, che, fuggendoli innanzi, dal colubro la morte prese subitana e atra. Con costui corse infino al lito rubro; con costui puose il mondo in tanta pace, che fu serrato a Giano il suo delubro.
Let weeping Cleopatra now be pitied,who, fleeing death, received her sudden, dark demisefrom the serpent's fangs. With him she ran to the Red Sea's shore;with him she brought such peace to the world,that Janus' temple was shut.
Ma ciò che ’l segno che parlar mi face fatto avea prima e poi era fatturo per lo regno mortal ch’a lui soggiace, diventa in apparenza poco e scuro, se in mano al terzo Cesare si mira con occhio chiaro e con affetto puro;
But what the sign that prompts me to speak had done before and would do later for the mortal realm subject to it becomes in appearance slight and obscure, if seen in the hands of the third Caesar with a clear eye and with pure affection.
ché la viva giustizia che mi spira, li concedette, in mano a quel ch’i’ dico, gloria di far vendetta a la sua ira. Or qui t’ammira in ciò ch’io ti replìco: poscia con Tito a far vendetta corse de la vendetta del peccato antico.
For living justice, which inspires me, granted him, in the hands of the one I speak of, glory in enacting vengeance for its wrath. Now, marvel here at what I reiterate to you: that afterwards he ran with Titus to take vengeance for the vengeance of the ancient sin.
E quando il dente longobardo morse la Santa Chiesa, sotto le sue ali Carlo Magno, vincendo, la soccorse. Omai puoi giudicar di quei cotali ch’io accusai di sopra e di lor falli, che son cagion di tutti vostri mali.
And when the Lombard tooth bit at the Holy Church, under its wings, Charlemagne, in victory, came to its aid. Now you can judge those I accused above and their faults, which are the cause of all your evils.
L’uno al pubblico segno i gigli gialli oppone, e l’altro appropria quello a parte, sì ch’è forte a veder chi più si falli. Faccian li Ghibellin, faccian lor arte sott’ altro segno, ché mal segue quello sempre chi la giustizia e lui diparte;
One sets yellow lilies against the public sign, and the other claims it for a faction, so it is hard to see who is more in the wrong. Let the Ghibellines do their work under another sign, for whoever separates justice from it always fares poorly.
e non l’abbatta esto Carlo novello coi Guelfi suoi, ma tema de li artigli ch’a più alto leon trasser lo vello. Molte fïate già pianser li figli per la colpa del padre, e non si creda che Dio trasmuti l’armi per suoi gigli!
And may this new Charles not bring it down with his Guelphs, but fear the talons that stripped the hide from a higher lion. Many times already have sons wept for their father's fault, and let no one believe that God will change his arms for his lilies!
Questa picciola stella si correda d’i buoni spirti che son stati attivi perché onore e fama li succeda: e quando li disiri poggian quivi, sì disvïando, pur convien che i raggi del vero amore in sù poggin men vivi.
This little star is adornedwith the good spirits who were activeso that honor and fame would succeed them; and when desires mount up there,thus straying, still it is necessary that the raysof true love ascend less brightly.
Ma nel commensurar d’i nostri gaggi col merto è parte di nostra letizia, perché non li vedem minor né maggi. Quindi addolcisce la viva giustizia in noi l’affetto sì, che non si puote torcer già mai ad alcuna nequizia.
But in the measuring of our rewardsagainst our merit lies a portion of our joy,because we see them neither less nor more.Thus the living justice sweetenswithin us the affection, so that it can neverbe turned to any wickedness.
Diverse voci fanno dolci note; così diversi scanni in nostra vita rendon dolce armonia tra queste rote. E dentro a la presente margarita luce la luce di Romeo, di cui fu l’ovra grande e bella mal gradita.
Diverse voices make sweet notes;so, diverse seats in our liferender sweet harmony among these spheres.And within this present pearlshines the light of Romeo, whosegreat and beautiful work was badly appreciated.
Ma i Provenzai che fecer contra lui non hanno riso; e però mal cammina qual si fa danno del ben fare altrui. Quattro figlie ebbe, e ciascuna reina, Ramondo Beringhiere, e ciò li fece Romeo, persona umìle e peregrina.
But those from Provence who rose against himhave not found joy; and therefore, he goes down the wrong roadwho harms himself by doing good for others.Raymond Berenger had four daughters, each a queen,and Romeo, a humble and wandering soul, did this for him.
E poi il mosser le parole biece a dimandar ragione a questo giusto, che li assegnò sette e cinque per diece, indi partissi povero e vetusto; e se ’l mondo sapesse il cor ch’elli ebbe mendicando sua vita a frusto a frusto,
And then wicked words moved himto demand an explanation from this just man,who assigned him seven and five for ten,then departed poor and aged;and if the world knew the heart he had,begging his life in bits and pieces.
