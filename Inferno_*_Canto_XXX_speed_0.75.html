Inferno * Canto XXX

In the tenth bolgia of the eighth circle, Dante and Virgil witness the punishment of the Falsifiers. Two spirits, Gianni Schicchi and Myrrha, run violently through the pit, biting other sinners. Schicchi seizes an alchemist named Capocchio and drags him away. The poets then observe Master Adam, a counterfeiter afflicted with dropsy, which bloats his torso while leaving him with an unquenchable thirst. He points out two other falsifiers nearby: Sinon the Greek and Potiphar’s wife, both suffering from a raging fever. Angered at being named, Sinon strikes Master Adam’s swollen belly. Adam retaliates by hitting Sinon in the face, and the two begin a coarse verbal argument, mocking each other’s punishments. Dante listens intently to their exchange until Virgil rebukes him for paying attention to such a vulgar spectacle. Dante feels immediate and profound shame. Virgil, seeing his contrition, forgives him and advises him that a desire to hear such arguments is base.

taking the one named Learco,he dashed him, and bashed him against a stone;and she drowned with the other burden.And when fortune cast downthe height of the Trojans, who dared all,so that the king was undone along with the kingdom,
Ecuba trista, misera e cattiva, poscia che vide Polissena morta, e del suo Polidoro in su la riva del mar si fu la dolorosa accorta, forsennata latrò sì come cane; tanto il dolor le fé la mente torta.
Hecuba, sad, wretched, and vile, after she saw Polyxena dead, and on the shore of the sea she learned of her Polydorus, the sorrowful woman, maddened, howled like a dog; so much did grief twist her mind.
Ma né di Tebe furie né troiane si vider mäi in alcun tanto crude, non punger bestie, nonché membra umane, quant’ io vidi in due ombre smorte e nude, che mordendo correvan di quel modo che ’l porco quando del porcil si schiude.
Neither the Furies of Thebes nor the Trojans were ever seen to be so cruel as those I saw in two pale, naked shades. They ran, biting in a way that a pig does when it bursts from the sty. They did not tear at beasts, much less at human limbs.
L’una giunse a Capocchio, e in sul nodo del collo l’assannò, sì che, tirando, grattar li fece il ventre al fondo sodo. E l’Aretin che rimase, tremando mi disse: «Quel folletto è Gianni Schicchi, e va rabbioso altrui così conciando».
One reached Capocchio and sunk its teeth into his neck, so that, pulling, it made him scrape his belly along the hard floor. And the Aretine who remained, trembling, said to me: “That goblin is Gianni Schicchi, and he goes about in a rage, treating others like this.”
«Oh», diss’ io lui, «se l’altro non ti ficchi li denti a dosso, non ti sia fatica a dir chi è, pria che di qui si spicchi». Ed elli a me: «Quell’ è l’anima antica di Mirra scellerata, che divenne al padre, fuor del dritto amore, amica.
“Oh,” I said to him, “if the other one doesn’t sink his teeth into you, it shouldn’t be too hard to tell me who he is before we leave here.”And he said to me, “That is the ancient soul of wicked Myrrha, who, outside of proper love, became intimate with her father.”
Questa a peccar con esso così venne, falsificando sé in altrui forma, come l’altro che là sen va, sostenne, per guadagnar la donna de la torma, falsificare in sé Buoso Donati, testando e dando al testamento norma».
This one came to sin with him,falsifying herself in another’s form,just as the other who goes there sustained, to win the woman of the herd,falsifying in himself Buoso Donati,testifying and giving rule to the testament.”
E poi che i due rabbiosi fuor passati sovra cu’ io avea l’occhio tenuto, rivolsilo a guardar li altri mal nati. Io vidi un, fatto a guisa di lëuto, pur ch’elli avesse avuta l’anguinaia tronca da l’altro che l’uomo ha forcuto.
And after the two furious ones passed by, over whom I had kept my eye, I turned it to look at the other ill-born. I saw one, made in the guise of a lute, even though it had had its groin cut off from the other that the forked man has.
La grave idropesì, che sì dispaia le membra con l’omor che mal converte, che ’l viso non risponde a la ventraia, faceva lui tener le labbra aperte come l’etico fa, che per la sete l’un verso ’l mento e l’altro in sù rinverte.
The heavy dropsy, which so distorts the limbs with the humor that it badly converts, so that the face doesn't match the belly, made him keep his lips open, as the consumptive does, who, for thirst, turns one toward the chin and the other upwards.
«O voi che sanz’ alcuna pena siete, e non so io perché, nel mondo gramo», diss’ elli a noi, «guardate e attendete a la miseria del maestro Adamo; io ebbi, vivo, assai di quel ch’i’ volli, e ora, lasso!, un gocciol d’acqua bramo.
"Oh, you who are without any suffering, and I don't know why, in this wretched world," he said to us, "look and pay attention to the misery of Master Adam; I had, when alive, plenty of what I wanted, and now, alas, I crave a single drop of water."
Li ruscelletti che d’i verdi colli del Casentin discendon giuso in Arno, faccendo i lor canali freddi e molli, sempre mi stanno innanzi, e non indarno, ché l’imagine lor vie più m’asciuga che ’l male ond’ io nel volto mi discarno.
The little streams that flow down into the Arno from the green hills of Casentino, making their channels cool and soft, are always before me, and not without reason, because their image dries me out even more than the malady that wastes my face.
La rigida giustizia che mi fruga tragge cagion del loco ov’ io peccai a metter più li miei sospiri in fuga. Ivi è Romena, là dov’ io falsai la lega suggellata del Batista; per ch’io il corpo sù arso lasciai.
The rigid justice that searches metakes cause from the place where I sinnedto put my sighs to greater flight.There is Romena, where I falsifiedthe alloy sealed with the Baptist's mark;because of this, I left my body burned.
Ma s’io vedessi qui l’anima trista di Guido o d’Alessandro o di lor frate, per Fonte Branda non darei la vista. Dentro c’è l’una già, se l’arrabbiate ombre che vanno intorno dicon vero; ma che mi val, c’ho le membra legate?
But if I saw here the wretched soulof Guido or Alessandro or their brother,I wouldn’t trade the sight for Fonte Branda.One is already within, if the angryshades that wander around tell the truth;but what use is it to me, with my limbs bound?
S’io fossi pur di tanto ancor leggero ch’i’ potessi in cent’ anni andare un’oncia, io sarei messo già per lo sentiero, cercando lui tra questa gente sconcia, con tutto ch’ella volge undici miglia, e men d’un mezzo di traverso non ci ha.
If I were still light enough,so that I could move an inch in a hundred years,I would already be on my way, searching for him among these wretched people,despite the fact that it circles eleven miles,and has less than half a mile across.
Io son per lor tra sì fatta famiglia; e’ m’indussero a batter li fiorini ch’avevan tre carati di mondiglia». E io a lui: «Chi son li due tapini che fumman come man bagnate ’l verno, giacendo stretti a’ tuoi destri confini?».
I am here among such a family, and they led me to strike the florins that had three carats of impurity." And I to him: "Who are the two wretches who are smoking like wet hands in winter, lying close to your right boundaries?"
«Qui li trovai—e poi volta non dierno—», rispuose, «quando piovvi in questo greppo, e non credo che dieno in sempiterno. L’una è la falsa ch’accusò Gioseppo; l’altr’ è ’l falso Sinon greco di Troia: per febbre aguta gittan tanto leppo».
"There I found them—and they never turned back," he replied, "when I rained down into this ditch, and I don't believe they will ever stop. The first is the false woman who accused Joseph; the other is the false Sinon, the Greek from Troy: from fever, they spew out such a stench."
E l’un di lor, che si recò a noia forse d’esser nomato sì oscuro, col pugno li percosse l’epa croia. Quella sonò come fosse un tamburo; e mastro Adamo li percosse il volto col braccio suo, che non parve men duro,
And one of them, who seemed annoyedat being called out so obscurely,punched him in the swollen gut.It sounded as if it were a drum;and Master Adam hit him in the facewith his arm, which seemed no less harsh.
dicendo a lui: «Ancor che mi sia tolto lo muover per le membra che son gravi, ho io il braccio a tal mestiere sciolto». Ond’ ei rispuose: «Quando tu andavi al fuoco, non l’avei tu così presto; ma sì e più l’avei quando coniavi».
Saying to him: “Although I am unable to move, due to these heavy limbs, my arm is free for this kind of work.” And he replied: “When you went to the fire, you weren't so quick; but you were quicker, and more so, when you were counterfeiting.”
E l’idropico: «Tu di’ ver di questo: ma tu non fosti sì ver testimonio là ’ve del ver fosti a Troia richesto». «S’io dissi falso, e tu falsasti il conio», disse Sinon; «e son qui per un fallo, e tu per più ch’alcun altro demonio!».
And the dropsical man said: "You speak the truth in this matter, but you were no truthful witness when you were asked for the truth in Troy." "If I spoke falsely," Sinon replied, "you counterfeited coin, and here I am for one offense, while you are here for more than any other demon!"
«Ricorditi, spergiuro, del cavallo», rispuose quel ch’avëa infiata l’epa; «e sieti reo che tutto il mondo sallo!». «E te sia rea la sete onde ti crepa», disse ’l Greco, «la lingua, e l’acqua marcia che ’l ventre innanzi a li occhi sì t’assiepa!».

Ad ascoltarli er’ io del tutto fisso, quando ’l maestro mi disse: «Or pur mira, che per poco che teco non mi risso!». Quand’ io ’l senti’ a me parlar con ira, volsimi verso lui con tal vergogna, ch’ancor per la memoria mi si gira.
I was completely fixated on listening to them, when the master said to me: “Now look closely, or I’ll scold you!”. When I heard him speak to me in anger, I turned towards him with such shame that it still troubles me to remember it.
Qual è colui che suo dannaggio sogna, che sognando desidera sognare, sì che quel ch’è, come non fosse, agogna, tal mi fec’ io, non possendo parlare, che disïava scusarmi, e scusava me tuttavia, e nol mi credea fare.
Such as the man who dreams of his own ruin, and dreaming, wishes he could dream again, so that he craves what is, as if it were not, so was I made, unable to speak, for I desired to excuse myself, and excused myself, though I did not believe I was doing so.
