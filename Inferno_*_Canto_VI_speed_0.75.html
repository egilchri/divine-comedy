Inferno * Canto VI

Dante awakens in the Third Circle of Hell, where he finds himself in a continuous, cold storm of hail and foul-smelling rain. The souls of the gluttonous lie in the mud. The guardian of this circle, the three-headed dog Cerberus, confronts Dante and his guide, Virgil. Virgil quiets the beast by throwing handfuls of earth into its mouths, allowing them to pass. As they walk over the prostrate spirits, one sits up and engages Dante in conversation. The soul identifies himself as Ciacco, a fellow Florentine. In response to Dante's questions, Ciacco delivers a prophecy about the political future of Florence and states that other prominent Florentines Dante asks about are in lower circles of Hell. After requesting to be remembered on Earth, Ciacco falls back among the other souls. As they depart, Virgil explains to Dante that the torments of the damned will be more intense after the Last Judgment. They then arrive at the entrance to the Fourth Circle.

When Cerberus, the great worm, saw us, he opened his mouths and showed us his fangs; he had no limb that held still. And my guide spread his hands, took up earth, and with full fists flung it into those ravenous gullets.

We moved onward through the shadows, which the heavy rain made, and placed our feet upon their vanity, which seemed like persons. They lay upon the ground, all of them, except for one, who quickly rose to sit when she saw us pass before her.
«O tu che se’ per questo ’nferno tratto», mi disse, «riconoscimi, se sai: tu fosti, prima ch’io disfatto, fatto». E io a lui: «L’angoscia che tu hai forse ti tira fuor de la mia mente, sì che non par ch’i’ ti vedessi mai.
"Oh, you who are drawn through this hell," he said, "recognize me, if you can: you existed before I was undone, before I was made." And I said to him, "The anguish that you suffer perhaps pulls you out of my memory, such that it does not seem I have ever seen you."
Ma dimmi chi tu se’ che ’n sì dolente loco se’ messo, e hai sì fatta pena, che, s’altra è maggio, nulla è sì spiacente». Ed elli a me: «La tua città, ch’è piena d’invidia sì che già trabocca il sacco, seco mi tenne in la vita serena.
Then tell me who you are, that you are placed in such a mournful place, and suffer such pain that if any is greater, none is as unpleasant." And he to me: "Your city, which is so full of envy that the sack already overflows, held me with it in the serene life."
Voi cittadini mi chiamaste Ciacco: per la dannosa colpa de la gola, come tu vedi, a la pioggia mi fiacco. E io anima trista non son sola, ché tutte queste a simil pena stanno per simil colpa».
You citizens called me Ciacco: for the damaging sin of gluttony, as you see, I am battered by the rain. And I, a wretched soul, am not alone, for all these are in similar torment for a similar sin.”

There are only two, and they are not understood; pride, envy, and greed are the three sparks that set hearts ablaze." Here, he ended the lamentable sound.
E io a lui: «Ancor vo’ che mi ’nsegni e che di più parlar mi facci dono. Farinata e ’l Tegghiaio, che fuor sì degni, Iacopo Rusticucci, Arrigo e ’l Mosca e li altri ch’a ben far puoser li ’ngegni,
And I to him: “I still desire you to teach me, and that you would bestow upon me the gift of further speech. Farinata and Tegghiaio, who were so worthy, Iacopo Rusticucci, Arrigo and Mosca, and the others who set their minds upon doing good,
dimmi ove sono e fa ch’io li conosca; ché gran disio mi stringe di savere se ’l ciel li addolcia o lo ’nferno li attosca». E quelli: «Ei son tra l’anime più nere; diverse colpe giù li grava al fondo: se tanto scendi, là i potrai vedere.
Tell me where they are, and let me know them. For a great longing urges me to learn if Heaven sweetens them or Hell poisons them." And he said: "They are among the blackest souls; different sins weigh them down at the bottom: if you descend that far, you will be able to see them.
Ma quando tu sarai nel dolce mondo, priegoti ch’a la mente altrui mi rechi: più non ti dico e più non ti rispondo». Li diritti occhi torse allora in biechi; guardommi un poco e poi chinò la testa: cadde con essa a par de li altri ciechi.
But when you return to the sweet world, I beg you, bring me back to the memory of others. I say no more to you, nor do I reply." Then he turned his direct eyes to a shadowed gaze; he looked at me for a moment and then lowered his head: he fell with it, along with the other blind.
E ’l duca disse a me: «Più non si desta di qua dal suon de l’angelica tromba, quando verrà la nimica podesta: ciascun rivederà la trista tomba, ripiglierà sua carne e sua figura, udirà quel ch’in etterno rimbomba».
And the guide said to me: "They will not awaken again this side of the sound of the angelic trumpet, when the hostile power arrives. Each will revisit their sorrowful tomb, reclaim their flesh and their form, and hear what echoes eternally."
Sì trapassammo per sozza mistura de l’ombre e de la pioggia, a passi lenti, toccando un poco la vita futura; per ch’io dissi: «Maestro, esti tormenti crescerann’ ei dopo la gran sentenza, o fier minori, o saran sì cocenti?».
We passed then through a vile blend of shadows and rain, our steps slow, touching slightly upon the life to come; for which I said: "Master, will these torments increase after the great judgment, or will they be lessened, or will they be as searing?"
Ed elli a me: «Ritorna a tua scïenza, che vuol, quanto la cosa è più perfetta, più senta il bene, e così la doglienza. Tutto che questa gente maladetta in vera perfezion già mai non vada, di là più che di qua essere aspetta».
And he to me: "Return now to your knowledge, which holds that the more perfect a thing is, the more it feels both good and pain. Though these accursed people will never arrive at true perfection, they will expect more suffering there than here."
Noi aggirammo a tondo quella strada, parlando più assai ch’i’ non ridico; venimmo al punto dove si digrada: quivi trovammo Pluto, il gran nemico.
We circled that road, speaking far more than I recount. We arrived at the point where it slopes downward: there we found Plutus, the great enemy.
