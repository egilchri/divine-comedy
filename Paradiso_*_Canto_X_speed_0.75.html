Paradiso * Canto X

Dante and Beatrice ascend to the Fourth Heaven, the sphere of the Sun. Dante perceives their arrival not through the sensation of movement, but by the intense increase in light. At Beatrice’s prompting, he offers thanks. A circle of twelve brilliant souls materializes, forming a crown around Dante and Beatrice. These spirits sing and rotate around the pair three times before coming to a halt. One of the lights speaks, identifying himself as Thomas Aquinas. He then points out the other eleven souls in the garland, naming Albertus Magnus, Gratian, Peter Lombard, Solomon, Dionysius the Areopagite, Paulus Orosius, Boethius, Isidore of Seville, Bede, Richard of Saint Victor, and Siger of Brabant. After completing these introductions, the luminous circle resumes its harmonious song and dance.

E Bëatrice cominciò: «Ringrazia, ringrazia il Sol de li angeli, ch’a questo sensibil t’ha levato per sua grazia». Cor di mortal non fu mai sì digesto a divozione e a rendersi a Dio con tutto ’l suo gradir cotanto presto,
And Beatrice began: "Give thanks, give thanks to the Sun of the angels, who has lifted you to this sensible state by His grace." A mortal heart was never so disposed toward devotion and surrender to God with all its approval, so swiftly,

At those words, I became as they were; and so completely did I give my love to Him, that Beatrice was eclipsed in oblivion. It did not displease her; but she laughed so that the splendor of her laughing eyes divided my mind into multiple thoughts.

I saw many living and conquering lights make us a center and a crown of themselves, sweeter in voice than bright in appearance: we sometimes see the daughter of Latona encircled in this way, when the air is heavy, so that it holds the thread that makes the zone.

In the court of Heaven, from which I return, many dear and beautiful jewels are found, so many that they cannot be removed from the kingdom; and the song of those lights was of those jewels; he who does not take wing to fly up there, let him await tidings from the mute.

Then, singing thus, those ardent suns spun round about us three times, like stars near the fixed poles. They seemed to me like women, not ones released from a dance, but ones who paused, silent, listening until they had gathered in the new notes.
