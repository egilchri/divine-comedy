Inferno * Canto XX

Dante and Virgil look down into the fourth trench of the eighth circle, where they see a procession of souls walking slowly, weeping in silence. Their heads are twisted completely backward on their necks, forcing them to walk in reverse and causing their tears to flow down their backs. Dante weeps at this sight, prompting a sharp rebuke from Virgil for feeling pity. Virgil then identifies several of the damned by name. He points out the seers Amphiaraus, Tiresias, and Arruns. He also indicates the sorceress Manto, which leads him into a long, detailed history of the founding of his native city, Mantua, which he says was built on the spot where Manto died. After Dante accepts this account, Virgil identifies more figures, including Eurypylus, Michael Scot, Guido Bonatti, and Asdente. He concludes by noting the time and urging Dante to move on to the next trench.
Io era già disposto tutto quanto a riguardar ne lo scoperto fondo, che si bagnava d’angoscioso pianto; e vidi gente per lo vallon tondo venir, tacendo e lagrimando, al passo che fanno le letane in questo mondo.
I was already fully prepared to observe the exposed bottom, which was soaked with anguished weeping. And I saw people coming through the round valley, silent and weeping, at the pace of the litanies in this world.
Come ’l viso mi scese in lor più basso, mirabilmente apparve esser travolto ciascun tra ’l mento e ’l principio del casso, ché da le reni era tornato ’l volto, e in dietro venir li convenia, perché ’l veder dinanzi era lor tolto.
As my gaze descended lower upon them, it seemed incredible that each was distorted between the chin and the start of the chest, for their faces were turned toward their loins, and they were compelled to come backward, because the power of sight had been taken from them.
Forse per forza già di parlasia si travolse così alcun del tutto; ma io nol vidi, né credo che sia. Se Dio ti lasci, lettor, prender frutto di tua lezione, or pensa per te stesso com’ io potea tener lo viso asciutto,

And did not cease to tumble down the slopeuntil it reached Minos, who seizes each soul.See how he has made a breast of his shoulders;because he wished to see too far ahead,he looks behind and makes a backward path.
Vedi Tiresia, che mutò sembiante quando di maschio femmina divenne, cangiandosi le membra tutte quante; e prima, poi, ribatter li convenne li duo serpenti avvolti, con la verga, che rïavesse le maschili penne.
See Tiresias, who changed his formwhen from male, he became female,altering all his limbs completely; and afterward, he had to strike againthe two entwined serpents with his staff,so he would regain his masculine form.
Aronta è quel ch’al ventre li s’atterga, che ne’ monti di Luni, dove ronca lo Carrarese che di sotto alberga, ebbe tra ’ bianchi marmi la spelonca per sua dimora; onde a guardar le stelle e ’l mar non li era la veduta tronca.
Aronta is the one whose back is to the belly,who, in the mountains of Luni, where the Carrara man, who dwells below, scrapes, had the caveas his dwelling among the white marbles; hence, to watch the starsand the sea, the view was not blocked for him.
E quella che ricuopre le mammelle, che tu non vedi, con le trecce sciolte, e ha di là ogne pilosa pelle, Manto fu, che cercò per terre molte; poscia si puose là dove nacqu’ io; onde un poco mi piace che m’ascolte.
And she who covers her breasts,which you do not see, with unbound tresses,and has on the other side all of her hairy skin, was Manto, who sought across many lands;then she settled where I was born;for this reason, I’d like for you to listen to me a little.
Poscia che ’l padre suo di vita uscìo e venne serva la città di Baco, questa gran tempo per lo mondo gio. Suso in Italia bella giace un laco, a piè de l’Alpe che serra Lamagna sovra Tiralli, c’ha nome Benaco.
After her father departed from life, and the city of Thebes became a servant to Bacchus, she wandered for a long time throughout the world. High up in beautiful Italy lies a lake, at the foot of the Alps that enclose Germany, above Tyrol, and it’s called Benaco.
Per mille fonti, credo, e più si bagna tra Garda e Val Camonica e Pennino de l’acqua che nel detto laco stagna. Loco è nel mezzo là dove ’l trentino pastore e quel di Brescia e ’l veronese segnar poria, s’e’ fesse quel cammino.
By a thousand springs, I believe, and more, it's nourished, between Garda and Val Camonica and Pennino, by the water that stagnates in the said lake. There is a place in the middle where the shepherd of Trentino and that of Brescia and the Veronese could mark, if they followed that path.
Siede Peschiera, bello e forte arnese da fronteggiar Bresciani e Bergamaschi, ove la riva ’ntorno più discese. Ivi convien che tutto quanto caschi ciò che ’n grembo a Benaco star non può, e fassi fiume giù per verdi paschi.
Peschiera sits, a beautiful and strong fortress, facing off against the people of Brescia and Bergamo, where the shore descends the most. There, everything that cannot stay in the lap of Lake Garda must fall, and becomes a river flowing down through green pastures.
Tosto che l’acqua a correr mette co, non più Benaco, ma Mencio si chiama fino a Governol, dove cade in Po. Non molto ha corso, ch’el trova una lama, ne la qual si distende e la ’mpaluda; e suol di state talor essere grama.
As soon as the water starts to flow from there, it is no longer called Benaco, but Mincio, all the way to Governolo, where it enters the Po. It has not flowed for long before it finds a marsh, in which it spreads and becomes a swamp; and sometimes in summer, it is troublesome.
Quindi passando la vergine cruda vide terra, nel mezzo del pantano, sanza coltura e d’abitanti nuda. Lì, per fuggire ogne consorzio umano, ristette con suoi servi a far sue arti, e visse, e vi lasciò suo corpo vano.
Then passing, the cruel virginsaw land, in the midst of the marsh,uncultivated and bare of inhabitants. There, to flee all human company,she stopped with her servants to practice her arts,and lived there, and left her empty body.
Li uomini poi che ’ntorno erano sparti s’accolsero a quel loco, ch’era forte per lo pantan ch’avea da tutte parti. Fer la città sovra quell’ ossa morte; e per colei che ’l loco prima elesse, Mantüa l’appellar sanz’ altra sorte.
The men who had scattered gathered around that place, which was fortified by the marsh on all sides. They built a city over those dead bones; and for her who first chose the place, they called it Mantua, without another name.
Già fuor le genti sue dentro più spesse, prima che la mattia da Casalodi da Pinamonte inganno ricevesse. Però t’assenno che, se tu mai odi originar la mia terra altrimenti, la verità nulla menzogna frodi».
Already her people were gathered more densely within, before the madness of Casalodi received deception from Pinamonte. Therefore I warn you, that if you ever hear my land originate in any other way, let the truth be unmarred by any lie.
E io: «Maestro, i tuoi ragionamenti mi son sì certi e prendon sì mia fede, che li altri mi sarien carboni spenti. Ma dimmi, de la gente che procede, se tu ne vedi alcun degno di nota; ché solo a ciò la mia mente rifiede».
And I: "Master, your reasoning is so certain, and it takes such hold of my faith, that the others would be extinguished coals to me. But tell me, of the people who are coming, if you see any worthy of note; because only to that does my mind return."
Allor mi disse: «Quel che da la gota porge la barba in su le spalle brune, fu—quando Grecia fu di maschi vòta, sì ch’a pena rimaser per le cune— augure, e diede ’l punto con Calcanta in Aulide a tagliar la prima fune.
Then he said to me: “He who offers his beard from his cheek, dark upon his shoulders, was an augur—when Greece was emptied of men, such that few remained for the cradles—and with Calchas he gave the signal in Aulis to cut the first cable.”
Euripilo ebbe nome, e così ’l canta l’alta mia tragedìa in alcun loco: ben lo sai tu che la sai tutta quanta. Quell’ altro che ne’ fianchi è così poco, Michele Scotto fu, che veramente de le magiche frode seppe ’l gioco.
Eurypylus was his name, as my high tragedy sings of him in a certain place: you know it well, you who know the whole of it. The other, whose sides are so meager, was Michael Scot, who truly knew the game of magical fraud.
Vedi Guido Bonatti; vedi Asdente, ch’avere inteso al cuoio e a lo spago ora vorrebbe, ma tardi si pente. Vedi le triste che lasciaron l’ago, la spuola e ’l fuso, e fecersi ’ndivine; fecer malie con erbe e con imago.
Behold Guido Bonatti; behold Asdente, who now wishes he had stuck to his leather and his thread, but it's too late, he repents. Behold the wretched women who forsook the needle, the shuttle, and the spindle, and became diviners; they worked spells with herbs and with images.
Ma vienne omai, ché già tiene ’l confine d’amendue li emisperi e tocca l’onda sotto Sobilia Caino e le spine; e già iernotte fu la luna tonda: ben ten de’ ricordar, ché non ti nocque alcuna volta per la selva fonda».
But come now, for the horizon already touches both hemispheres and the waves below Seville, Cain, and the thorns; and the moon was round last night: you should remember well, because it never harmed you in the deep wood.
