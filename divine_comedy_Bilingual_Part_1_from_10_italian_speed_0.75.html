La Divina Commedia di Dante - Part 1

Inferno • Canto I
Nel mezzo del cammin di nostra vita mi ritrovai per una selva oscura, ché la diritta via era smarrita. Ahi quanto a dir qual era è cosa dura esta selva selvaggia e aspra e forte che nel pensier rinova la paura!
Midway upon the journey of our life,I found myself within a forest dark,For the straightforward pathway had been lost.Ah me! How hard a thing it is to sayWhat was this forest savage, harsh, and grim,Which in the very thought renews my fear.
Tant’ è amara che poco è più morte; ma per trattar del ben ch’i’ vi trovai, dirò de l’altre cose ch’i’ v’ho scorte. Io non so ben ridir com’ i’ v’intrai, tant’ era pien di sonno a quel punto che la verace via abbandonai.
So bitter was it that death is hardly more so. But to speak of the good I found there, I will tell of the other things I saw. I cannot rightly say how I entered there, so full of sleep was I at that point when I abandoned the true way.
Ma poi ch’i’ fui al piè d’un colle giunto, là dove terminava quella valle che m’avea di paura il cor compunto, guardai in alto e vidi le sue spalle vestite già de’ raggi del pianeta che mena dritto altrui per ogne calle.
But when I came to the foot of a hill, there, where the valley ended, which had pierced my heart with fear, I looked up and saw its shoulders already dressed in the rays of the planet that guides men straight along every path.
Allor fu la paura un poco queta, che nel lago del cor m’era durata la notte ch’i’ passai con tanta pieta. E come quei che con lena affannata, uscito fuor del pelago a la riva, si volge a l’acqua perigliosa e guata,
Then my fear was somewhat calmed, which had endured in the lake of my heart the night that I passed with such pity. And as he who, with breath labored, having come out of the sea onto the shore, turns to the perilous water and stares,
così l’animo mio, ch’ancor fuggiva, si volse a retro a rimirar lo passo che non lasciò già mai persona viva. Poi ch’èi posato un poco il corpo lasso, ripresi via per la piaggia diserta, sì che ’l piè fermo sempre era ’l più basso.
So my spirit, which was still fleeing, turned back to contemplate the path that never left a living person. After I had rested my weary body a little, I resumed my journey along the deserted shore, so that my firmer foot was always the lower one.
Ed ecco, quasi al cominciar de l’erta, una lonza leggera e presta molto, che di pel macolato era coverta; e non mi si partia dinanzi al volto, anzi ’mpediva tanto il mio cammino, ch’i’ fui per ritornar più volte vòlto.
And then, almost at the start of the ascent, a leopard, sleek and very swift, which was covered in a mottled hide. And it did not depart from before my face, but rather it so impeded my path that I was many times on the verge of turning back.
Temp’ era dal principio del mattino, e ’l sol montava ’n sù con quelle stelle ch’eran con lui quando l’amor divino mosse di prima quelle cose belle; sì ch’a bene sperar m’era cagione di quella fiera a la gaetta pelle
It was the beginning of morning, and the sun was ascending with those stars that were with it when divine love first set those beautiful things in motion; so that hope was a cause for me, on account of that beast with the nimble hide.
l’ora del tempo e la dolce stagione; ma non sì che paura non mi desse la vista che m’apparve d’un leone. Questi parea che contra me venisse con la test’ alta e con rabbiosa fame, sì che parea che l’aere ne tremesse.
The hour of time and the sweet season; but not so much that fear did not seize me at the sight of a lion that appeared before me. This one seemed to advance against me with its head held high and with rabid hunger, so that the air itself seemed to tremble.
Ed una lupa, che di tutte brame sembiava carca ne la sua magrezza, e molte genti fé già viver grame, questa mi porse tanto di gravezza con la paura ch’uscia di sua vista, ch’io perdei la speranza de l’altezza.
And a she-wolf, that with every craving appeared burdened in its gauntness, and has made many people live wretched lives, this one brought me such weight with the fear that emanated from its sight, that I lost all hope of the summit.
E qual è quei che volontieri acquista, e giugne ’l tempo che perder lo face, che ’n tutti suoi pensier piange e s’attrista; tal mi fece la bestia sanza pace, che, venendomi ’ncontro, a poco a poco mi ripigneva là dove ’l sol tace.
And like a man who eagerly amasses, only to reach the moment when he must lose it all, and who, in every thought, weeps and grieves; such did the beast without peace make of me, for, coming toward me, little by little, it pushed me back to where the sun is silent.
Mentre ch’i’ rovinava in basso loco, dinanzi a li occhi mi si fu offerto chi per lungo silenzio parea fioco. Quando vidi costui nel gran diserto, «Miserere di me», gridai a lui, «qual che tu sii, od ombra od omo certo!».
While I was falling into the deep place, there appeared before my eyes one who seemed faint from a long silence. When I saw this figure in the great desert, I cried out, "Have pity on me, whoever you are, shade or true man!"
Rispuosemi: «Non omo, omo già fui, e li parenti miei furon lombardi, mantoani per patrïa ambedui. Nacqui sub Iulio, ancor che fosse tardi, e vissi a Roma sotto ’l buono Augusto nel tempo de li dèi falsi e bugiardi.
He answered me: “Not a man, though a man I once was, and my parents were Lombards, both of Mantua by ancestry. I was born sub Iulio, though it was late, and I lived in Rome under the good Augustus during the time of the false and lying gods.”
Poeta fui, e cantai di quel giusto figliuol d’Anchise che venne di Troia, poi che ’l superbo Ilïón fu combusto. Ma tu perché ritorni a tanta noia?

"Why don't you climb the delightful mountain, which is the beginning and the cause of all joy?" "Are you then that Virgil, and that fountain which pours forth so wide a river of speech?" I answered him with a shamefaced countenance.

E io a lui: «Poeta, io ti richeggio per quello Dio che tu non conoscesti, acciò ch’io fugga questo male e peggio, che tu mi meni là dov’ or dicesti, sì ch’io veggia la porta di san Pietro e color cui tu fai cotanto mesti».
And I to him: "Poet, I beg you, by that God whom you did not know, so that I may flee this evil and worse, lead me there where you just spoke, so that I may see the gate of Saint Peter and those whom you make so sad."

Through this journey, of which you boast, he understood the things that caused his victory and his papal vestments. Then went the vessel of election, to bring comfort to that faith which is the beginning of the path to salvation.

But I, why do I come here? Or who permits it?

I am not Aeneas, I am not Paul; neither I nor anyone else believes me worthy of this. Therefore, if I abandon the journey here, I fear my coming will be in vain.

These miserable souls, who never truly lived, were naked and constantly tormented by flies and wasps that swarmed there. These insects stung their faces, drawing blood and tears that, mixing together, flowed down to their feet and were gathered by vile worms.

And then, when I turned my gaze further on, I saw a multitude on the banks of a great river; I said: "Master, now grant that I know who they are, and what custom makes them so eager to cross, as I discern in the dim light."

They blasphemed God and their parents, the human race, the place, the time, and the seed of their conception and of their birth. Then they all drew together, weeping loudly, toward the wretched shore that awaits every man who does not fear God.

Oscura e profonda era e nebulosa tanto che, per ficcar lo viso a fondo, io non vi discernea alcuna cosa. «Or discendiam qua giù nel cieco mondo», cominciò il poeta tutto smorto.
Dark and deep and clouded was that place, so that, to peer into the depths, I could make out nothing there. "Now let us descend into the blind world," the poet began, completely pale.
«Io sarò primo, e tu sarai secondo». E io, che del color mi fui accorto, dissi: «Come verrò, se tu paventi che suoli al mio dubbiare esser conforto?».

Ed elli a me: «L’angoscia de le genti che son qua giù, nel viso mi dipigne quella pietà che tu per tema senti. Andiam, ché la via lunga ne sospigne».
And he said to me: "The anguish of the people who are down here paints in my face that pity which you feel as fear. Let us go, for the long road drives us on."
