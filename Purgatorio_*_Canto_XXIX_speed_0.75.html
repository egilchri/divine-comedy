Purgatorio * Canto XXIX

Dante and Matelda walk along the river Lethe. A brilliant, persistent light appears through the forest, accompanied by a sweet melody. A procession emerges, led by seven golden candelabra that leave seven colored trails in the air like banners. Following them are twenty-four elders, dressed in white and crowned with lilies, walking in pairs and singing. Next, four six-winged creatures covered in eyes advance, flanking a two-wheeled triumphal chariot drawn by a griffin with a golden head and wings and a white-and-red body. By the chariot’s right wheel, three women—one red, one green, one white—dance in a circle. To the left, four women in purple dance. Behind the chariot walk two old men, followed by four men of humble appearance, and finally, a single old man walking alone. When the chariot arrives directly across the river from Dante, a sound like thunder is heard, and the entire procession halts.
E come ninfe che si givan sole per le salvatiche ombre, disïando qual di veder, qual di fuggir lo sole, allor si mosse contra ’l fiume, andando su per la riva; e io pari di lei, picciol passo con picciol seguitando.
And like nymphs who wander alonethrough wild shadows, desiringeither to see or to flee the sun, then she moved toward the stream, goingup along the bank; and I, beside her,followed her small steps with my own.
Non eran cento tra ’ suoi passi e ’ miei, quando le ripe igualmente dier volta, per modo ch’a levante mi rendei. Né ancor fu così nostra via molta, quando la donna tutta a me si torse, dicendo: «Frate mio, guarda e ascolta».
No more than a hundred of her steps and minehad passed when the banks curved equally,so that I found myself facing east.Our journey had not proceeded much further when the woman turned fully toward me, saying,"My brother, look and listen."
Ed ecco un lustro sùbito trascorse da tutte parti per la gran foresta, tal che di balenar mi mise in forse. Ma perché ’l balenar, come vien, resta, e quel, durando, più e più splendeva, nel mio pensier dicea: ‘Che cosa è questa?’.
And, behold, five years suddenly elapsed on all sides through the great forest, such that it put me in doubt if it were lightning. But because lightning, as it comes, ceases, and that, enduring, shone more and more, in my thought I said: ‘What is this?’
E una melodia dolce correva per l’aere luminoso; onde buon zelo mi fé riprender l’ardimento d’Eva, che là dove ubidia la terra e ’l cielo, femmina, sola e pur testé formata, non sofferse di star sotto alcun velo;
And a sweet melody ran through the luminous air; thus, good zeal made me resume Eve's daring, who, where earth and heaven obeyed, a woman, alone and only just formed, did not suffer to remain under any veil.
sotto ’l qual se divota fosse stata, avrei quelle ineffabili delizie sentite prima e più lunga fïata. Mentr’ io m’andava tra tante primizie de l’etterno piacer tutto sospeso, e disïoso ancora a più letizie,
under which, if she had been devout, I would have felt those ineffable delights sooner and for a longer time. While I moved among such first fruits of eternal pleasure, completely suspended and still desirous of greater joys,
dinanzi a noi, tal quale un foco acceso, ci si fé l’aere sotto i verdi rami; e ’l dolce suon per canti era già inteso. O sacrosante Vergini, se fami, freddi o vigilie mai per voi soffersi, cagion mi sprona ch’io mercé vi chiami.
Before us, like a fire alight, the air took form beneath the verdant boughs; and the sweet sound of song was already heard. O most holy Virgins, if I ever suffered hunger, cold, or vigil for you, a reason spurs me to call upon you for grace.
Or convien che Elicona per me versi, e Uranìe m’aiuti col suo coro forti cose a pensar mettere in versi. Poco più oltre, sette alberi d’oro falsava nel parere il lungo tratto del mezzo ch’era ancor tra noi e loro;
Now may Helicon pour forth for me,and Urania assist me with her choir,to set strong things to thought in verse.A little further on, seven golden treeswere falsely rendered in appearance by the long stretchof the space that still lay between us and them;
ma quand’ i’ fui sì presso di lor fatto, che l’obietto comun, che ’l senso inganna, non perdea per distanza alcun suo atto, la virtù ch’a ragion discorso ammanna, sì com’ elli eran candelabri apprese, e ne le voci del cantare ‘Osanna’.
But when I was so close to them, I sawthat the common object, which deceives the senses,did not lose any of its power by distance;the power that prepares reason's discourse,I perceived them to be like candelabras,and in the voices singing 'Hosanna.'
Di sopra fiammeggiava il bello arnese più chiaro assai che luna per sereno di mezza notte nel suo mezzo mese. Io mi rivolsi d’ammirazion pieno al buon Virgilio, ed esso mi rispuose con vista carca di stupor non meno.
Above, the beautiful object blazed,far brighter than the moon on a clear nightat its midpoint. I turned, filled with wonder,to the good Virgil, and he answered mewith a gaze burdened with no less amazement.
Indi rendei l’aspetto a l’alte cose che si movieno incontr’ a noi sì tardi, che foran vinte da novelle spose. La donna mi sgridò: «Perché pur ardi sì ne l’affetto de le vive luci, e ciò che vien di retro a lor non guardi?».
Then I turned my gaze back to those high thingsthat moved towards us so slowly,they could have been outrun by new brides.The woman scolded me: "Why do you burnso in the affection for the living lights,and do not look at what comes behind them?".
Genti vid’ io allor, come a lor duci, venire appresso, vestite di bianco; e tal candor di qua già mai non fuci. L’acqua imprendëa dal sinistro fianco, e rendea me la mia sinistra costa, s’io riguardava in lei, come specchio anco.
Then I saw those people, following their leaders, come behind, dressed in white; such a whiteness has never been seen on earth. The water took hold on the left side, and if I looked at it, it reflected my left side, like a mirror.
Quand’ io da la mia riva ebbi tal posta, che solo il fiume mi facea distante, per veder meglio ai passi diedi sosta, e vidi le fiammelle andar davante, lasciando dietro a sé l’aere dipinto, e di tratti pennelli avean sembiante;
When I had reached a place on the bank that left the river as the only separation, I paused to get a better view of their movements, and I saw the little flames moving ahead, leaving the air behind them painted, resembling brushstrokes.
sì che lì sopra rimanea distinto di sette liste, tutte in quei colori onde fa l’arco il Sole e Delia il cinto. Questi ostendali in dietro eran maggiori che la mia vista; e, quanto a mio avviso, diece passi distavan quei di fori.
So that above, it remained distinct, with seven bands, all in those colors that form the rainbow of the Sun and the belt of Delia. These uppermost were larger than my sight; and, as far as I could judge, ten paces separated the outer ones.
Sotto così bel ciel com’ io diviso, ventiquattro seniori, a due a due, coronati venien di fiordaliso. Tutti cantavan: «Benedicta tue ne le figlie d’Adamo, e benedette sieno in etterno le bellezze tue!».
Beneath so lovely a sky, as I have described it, twenty-four elders, two by two, came, crowned with cornflowers. All were singing: “Blessed are you among the daughters of Adam, and blessed eternally be your beauty!”
Poscia che i fiori e l’altre fresche erbette a rimpetto di me da l’altra sponda libere fuor da quelle genti elette, sì come luce luce in ciel seconda, vennero appresso lor quattro animali, coronati ciascun di verde fronda.
After the flowers and the other fresh grasses, opposite me on the other bank, were free from those chosen people, just as light follows light in the heavens, four animals came after them, each crowned with green foliage.
Ognuno era pennuto di sei ali; le penne piene d’occhi; e li occhi d’Argo, se fosser vivi, sarebber cotali. A descriver lor forme più non spargo rime, lettor; ch’altra spesa mi strigne, tanto ch’a questa non posso esser largo;
Each was feathered with six wings, the feathers filled with eyes, and the eyes were like those of Argus, if he were alive. Reader, I will not spend any more rhymes on describing their forms, for another expense presses me so closely that I cannot be lavish with this one.
ma leggi Ezechïel, che li dipigne come li vide da la fredda parte venir con vento e con nube e con igne; e quali i troverai ne le sue carte, tali eran quivi, salvo ch’a le penne Giovanni è meco e da lui si diparte.
But read Ezekiel, who depicts themas he saw them coming from the cold side,with wind and cloud and fire; and as you find them in his writings,such were they here, save that John departs from me in their feathers.
Lo spazio dentro a lor quattro contenne un carro, in su due rote, trïunfale, ch’al collo d’un grifon tirato venne. Esso tendeva in sù l’una e l’altra ale tra la mezzana e le tre e tre liste, sì ch’a nulla, fendendo, facea male.
The space within the four helda chariot, triumphal, upon two wheels,drawn at the neck by a griffin.It stretched upward one wing and the otherbetween the middle and the three and three stripes,so that, cleaving, it harmed nothing.
Tanto salivan che non eran viste; le membra d’oro avea quant’ era uccello, e bianche l’altre, di vermiglio miste. Non che Roma di carro così bello rallegrasse Affricano, o vero Augusto, ma quel del Sol saria pover con ello;
They slobbered so much that they were unseen; the bird-creature's limbs were gold, and the rest were white, mixed with red. Not even Rome, with a chariot so beautiful, cheered Africanus, or even Augustus, but that of the Sun would be poor compared to it.
quel del Sol che, svïando, fu combusto per l’orazion de la Terra devota, quando fu Giove arcanamente giusto. Tre donne in giro da la destra rota venian danzando; l’una tanto rossa ch’a pena fora dentro al foco nota;
That of the Sun, veering off course, was consumed by the prayer of the devout Earth, when Jove was secretly just. Three women came dancing in a circle from the right side; one so red that she could barely be seen within the fire.
l’altr’ era come se le carni e l’ossa fossero state di smeraldo fatte; la terza parea neve testé mossa; e or parëan da la bianca tratte, or da la rossa; e dal canto di questa l’altre toglien l’andare e tarde e ratte.
The other appeared as though her flesh and bones were made of emerald. The third seemed like snow, newly fallen. Now they appeared drawn from the white, now from the red; and from this one's song, the others took their steps, slow and swift.
Da la sinistra quattro facean festa, in porpore vestite, dietro al modo d’una di lor ch’avea tre occhi in testa. Appresso tutto il pertrattato nodo vidi due vecchi in abito dispari, ma pari in atto e onesto e sodo.
From the left, four celebrated, robed in purple, following the lead of one amongst them, who had three eyes upon her head. Beside this grouping, I saw two elders, in mismatched garments, yet equal in their actions, both honest and firm.
L’un si mostrava alcun de’ famigliari di quel sommo Ipocràte che natura a li animali fé ch’ell’ ha più cari; mostrava l’altro la contraria cura con una spada lucida e aguta, tal che di qua dal rio mi fé paura.
One displayed a member of the familyof that supreme Hippocrates, who naturemade for the animals she loves most; the other showed the opposing carewith a shining, sharp sword,so that on this side of the river it made me afraid.
Poi vidi quattro in umile paruta; e di retro da tutti un vecchio solo venir, dormendo, con la faccia arguta. E questi sette col primaio stuolo erano abitüati, ma di gigli dintorno al capo non facëan brolo,
Then I saw four figures, humble in their appearance, and behind them, a single old man came, sleeping, with a shrewd face. These seven were accustomed to the first throng, but they did not weave garlands of lilies around their heads.
