Inferno * Canto II

As evening falls, Dante the pilgrim expresses his fear and inadequacy to his guide, Virgil. He argues that unlike Aeneas or St. Paul, he is not worthy of a journey into the afterlife and his initial resolve falters. Virgil responds by calling this hesitation cowardice and explains the reason for his arrival. He recounts being in Limbo when a blessed lady, Beatrice, descended from Heaven. She told Virgil that Dante was lost and in peril on a dark slope and asked him to go to Dante's aid. Virgil then relates what Beatrice told him: her mission was prompted by another heavenly lady, Lucia, who was in turn sent by a "gentle lady" (the Virgin Mary) who pitied Dante's plight. Hearing that these three figures in Heaven are concerned for his safety, Dante’s courage is restored. He reaffirms his commitment to the journey, and Virgil sets out with Dante following close behind.
Lo giorno se n’andava, e l’aere bruno toglieva li animai che sono in terra da le fatiche loro; e io sol uno m’apparecchiava a sostener la guerra sì del cammino e sì de la pietate, che ritrarrà la mente che non erra.
The day was fading, and the darkening air was freeing the creatures on earth from their labors. I alone prepared myself to endure the war, both of the journey and of the pity, that will recall the mind that does not err.

Through this journey, of which you boast, he understood the things that caused his victory and his papal vestments. Then went the vessel of election, to bring comfort to that faith which is the beginning of the path to salvation.

But I, why do I come here? Or who permits it?

I am not Aeneas, I am not Paul; neither I nor anyone else believes me worthy of this. Therefore, if I abandon the journey here, I fear my coming will be in vain.

I was among those in limbo, and a woman, blessed and beautiful, called to me, such that I asked her for a command. Her eyes shone brighter than the stars; and she began to speak to me, softly and gently, with angelic voice, in her own language:

“Since you desire to know so deeply,” he replied, “I will tell you briefly why I do not fear to come here within. One should fear only those things that have the power to inflict harm on others; not the others, for they are not frightening.”

I am created by God, and by His grace, I am such that your suffering does not touch me, nor does the flame of this fire assault me. A noble woman in Heaven pities this impediment to which I send you, so that she breaks harsh judgment above.

This one, Lucia, beseeched in her plea, and said: "Now your faithful one has need of you, and I commend him to you." Lucia, enemy of all things cruel, moved, and came to the place where I was, who sat with ancient Rachel.

She said: “Beatrice, true praise of God, why do you not help the one who loved you so much, who left the vulgar throng for you? Do you not hear the pity of his weeping, do you not see the death that fights him on the river where the sea holds no dominion?”
Al mondo non fur mai persone ratte a far lor pro o a fuggir lor danno, com’ io, dopo cotai parole fatte, venni qua giù del mio beato scanno, fidandomi del tuo parlare onesto, ch’onora te e quei ch’udito l’hanno”.
