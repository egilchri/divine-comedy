Paradiso * Canto XI

In the Sphere of the Sun, the circle of twelve radiant souls becomes stationary. The spirit of Thomas Aquinas, which had spoken in the previous canto, glows more brightly and addresses Dante. Sensing Dante’s unspoken questions about two of his earlier statements, Aquinas begins a long discourse. He narrates the life of Francis of Assisi, beginning with his birth in Assisi. The account details Francis's early defiance of his father, his devotion to "Lady Poverty," and the gathering of his first followers, including Bernard of Quintavalle. Aquinas recounts Francis’s journey to Rome to receive the first papal seal for his order from Pope Innocent III, and later a second confirmation from Pope Honorius III. The narrative continues with Francis’s mission to preach before the Sultan in Egypt and concludes with his return to Italy, where he received the stigmata from Christ on Mount La Verna. After recounting Francis’s death, Aquinas laments how his own Dominican order has strayed from its founder’s path.
O insensata cura de’ mortali, quanto son difettivi silogismi quei che ti fanno in basso batter l’ali! Chi dietro a iura e chi ad amforismi sen giva, e chi seguendo sacerdozio, e chi regnar per forza o per sofismi,
Oh, foolish concern of mortals, how defective are the syllogisms that make you beat your wings low! Some went after laws and some after aphorisms, and some following priesthood, and some to reign through force or through sophistry.
e chi rubare e chi civil negozio, chi nel diletto de la carne involto s’affaticava e chi si dava a l’ozio, quando, da tutte queste cose sciolto, con Bëatrice m’era suso in cielo cotanto glorïosamente accolto.
And some to theft, some to legal dealings, some wrapped in the delights of the flesh, toiling, and some given over to idleness, when, free from all these things, with Beatrice I was received so gloriously on high in Heaven.
Poi che ciascuno fu tornato ne lo punto del cerchio in che avanti s’era, fermossi, come a candellier candelo. E io senti’ dentro a quella lumera che pria m’avea parlato, sorridendo incominciar, faccendosi più mera:
After each spirit had returned to the point on the circle that it had previously occupied, it came to a halt, like a candle in a candelabra. And within that light that had first spoken to me, I heard it begin to speak again, smiling, and becoming more pure:
«Così com’ io del suo raggio resplendo, sì, riguardando ne la luce etterna, li tuoi pensieri onde cagioni apprendo. Tu dubbi, e hai voler che si ricerna in sì aperta e ’n sì distesa lingua lo dicer mio, ch’al tuo sentir si sterna,
“Just as I shine from its ray, so, gazing into the eternal light, I perceive the origins of your thoughts. You doubt, and you desire that my words be retold in language so open and expansive that they are plain to your understanding,
ove dinanzi dissi: “U’ ben s’impingua”, e là u’ dissi: “Non nacque il secondo”; e qui è uopo che ben si distingua. La provedenza, che governa il mondo con quel consiglio nel quale ogne aspetto creato è vinto pria che vada al fondo,
I began by saying, “Where good is nourished,”and there, “The second never was,”and here it is necessary that I make myself clear.Providence, which governs the worldwith the counsel in which every created aspectis overcome before it can reach its depths,
però che andasse ver’ lo suo diletto la sposa di colui ch’ad alte grida disposò lei col sangue benedetto, in sé sicura e anche a lui più fida, due principi ordinò in suo favore, che quinci e quindi le fosser per guida.
So that she might go toward her delight,the bride of he who, with loud cries,espoused her with blessed blood, secure in herself and even more faithful to him,ordained two princes in her favor,who would be her guides from here and there.
L’un fu tutto serafico in ardore; l’altro per sapïenza in terra fue di cherubica luce uno splendore. De l’un dirò, però che d’amendue si dice l’un pregiando, qual ch’om prende, perch’ ad un fine fur l’opere sue.
One was all seraphic in ardor;the other, through wisdom on Earth, wasa splendor of cherubic light. I will speak of the one, because in praising both,one says whatever one chooses,because their works were for a single end.
Intra Tupino e l’acqua che discende del colle eletto dal beato Ubaldo, fertile costa d’alto monte pende, onde Perugia sente freddo e caldo da Porta Sole; e di rietro le piange per grave giogo Nocera con Gualdo.
Within Tupino and the water that flows downfrom the hill chosen by blessed Ubaldo,a fertile slope of a high mountain hangs, from which Perugia feels cold and heatfrom Porta Sole; and behind it lamentthrough a heavy burden Nocera with Gualdo.
Di questa costa, là dov’ ella frange più sua rattezza, nacque al mondo un sole, come fa questo talvolta di Gange. Però chi d’esso loco fa parole, non dica Ascesi, ché direbbe corto, ma Orïente, se proprio dir vuole.
From this hillside, where it breaksits steeper incline, a sun was born into the world,just as this one sometimes does from the Ganges.Therefore, whoever speaks of that placeshould not say Assisi, for that would be too brief,but rather, the Orient, if they wish to speak truly.
Non era ancor molto lontan da l’orto, ch’el cominciò a far sentir la terra de la sua gran virtute alcun conforto; ché per tal donna, giovinetto, in guerra del padre corse, a cui, come a la morte, la porta del piacer nessun diserra;
Not far from the garden then, he began to make the earth feel the comfort of his great virtue. For the sake of such a woman, as a youth, he ran to war against his father, to whom, like death, the door of pleasure does not open to anyone.
e dinanzi a la sua spirital corte et coram patre le si fece unito; poscia di dì in dì l’amò più forte. Questa, privata del primo marito, millecent’ anni e più dispetta e scura fino a costui si stette sanza invito;
And before her spiritual court, he made himself one with her father; then day by day he loved her more and more. She, widowed of her first husband, scorned and obscure for a thousand one hundred years and more, remained uninvited until he came;
né valse udir che la trovò sicura con Amiclate, al suon de la sua voce, colui ch’a tutto ’l mondo fé paura; né valse esser costante né feroce, sì che, dove Maria rimase giuso, ella con Cristo pianse in su la croce.
Nor did the waltz, which found her safewith Amyclas, at the sound of his voice,he who made all the world afraid, avail.Nor did being constant or fierce avail,so that, where Mary stayed below,she wept with Christ upon the cross.
Ma perch’ io non proceda troppo chiuso, Francesco e Povertà per questi amanti prendi oramai nel mio parlar diffuso. La lor concordia e i lor lieti sembianti, amore e maraviglia e dolce sguardo facieno esser cagion di pensier santi;
But lest I speak too obscurely, take now Francis and Poverty as these lovers in my extended discourse. Their harmony, their joyful appearances, love, wonder, and sweet gaze made holy thoughts arise.
tanto che ’l venerabile Bernardo si scalzò prima, e dietro a tanta pace corse e, correndo, li parve esser tardo. Oh ignota ricchezza!
So much so that the venerable Bernard first removed his shoes, and after such peace he ran, and while running, he felt he was delaying. Oh, unknown wealth!
oh ben ferace! Scalzasi Egidio, scalzasi Silvestro dietro a lo sposo, sì la sposa piace.
Oh, fertile wellspring! Egidio took off his shoes, Silvestro took off his shoes. Following the groom, the bride is pleased.
Indi sen va quel padre e quel maestro con la sua donna e con quella famiglia che già legava l’umile capestro. Né li gravò viltà di cuor le ciglia per esser fi’ di Pietro Bernardone, né per parer dispetto a maraviglia;
Then went that father and that master, along with his woman and with that family who had already bound the humble halter. Nor did any baseness of heart weigh down his brow for being the son of Pietro Bernardone, nor to appear a marvel of disdain.
ma regalmente sua dura intenzione ad Innocenzio aperse, e da lui ebbe primo sigillo a sua religïone. Poi che la gente poverella crebbe dietro a costui, la cui mirabil vita meglio in gloria del ciel si canterebbe,
But regally he revealed his steadfast intention to Innocent, and from him received the first seal for his order. Then, as the humble people grew in number behind him, whose wondrous life would be better sung in the glory of heaven,
di seconda corona redimita fu per Onorio da l’Etterno Spiro la santa voglia d’esto archimandrita. E poi che, per la sete del martiro, ne la presenza del Soldan superba predicò Cristo e li altri che ’l seguiro,
of a second crown, redeemed,by the Eternal Breath, was the holy willof this archimandrite, for Honorius.And then, consumed by the thirst of martyrdom,in the proud Sultan’s presence,he preached Christ, and those who followed Him.
e per trovare a conversione acerba troppo la gente e per non stare indarno, redissi al frutto de l’italica erba, nel crudo sasso intra Tevero e Arno da Cristo prese l’ultimo sigillo, che le sue membra due anni portarno.
And finding the populace too unripe for conversion, and unwilling to remain idle, I returned to the fruits of the Italian earth, in the harsh stone between the Tiber and Arno, where he took from Christ his final seal, which his limbs bore for two years.
Quando a colui ch’a tanto ben sortillo piacque di trarlo suso a la mercede ch’el meritò nel suo farsi pusillo, a’ frati suoi, sì com’ a giuste rede, raccomandò la donna sua più cara, e comandò che l’amassero a fede;
When it pleased Him who had chosen him for such goodness to raise him up to the reward he had earned in his humility, he commended his dearest lady to his brothers, as to just heirs, and commanded that they love her faithfully.
e del suo grembo l’anima preclara mover si volle, tornando al suo regno, e al suo corpo non volle altra bara. Pensa oramai qual fu colui che degno collega fu a mantener la barca di Pietro in alto mar per dritto segno;
and from her womb, the illustrious soul,wished to depart, returning to its kingdom,and for her body, desired no other tomb. Now consider who was the worthycolleague in guiding Peter's barqueon the high seas, towards its true course;
e questo fu il nostro patrïarca; per che qual segue lui, com’ el comanda, discerner puoi che buone merce carca. Ma ’l suo pecuglio di nova vivanda è fatto ghiotto, sì ch’esser non puote che per diversi salti non si spanda;
And this was our patriarch; thus, whoever follows him as he commands, you can discern he carries good merchandise. But his flock has grown greedy for new food, so it cannot help but scatter in various leaps.
e quanto le sue pecore remote e vagabunde più da esso vanno, più tornano a l’ovil di latte vòte. Ben son di quelle che temono ’l danno e stringonsi al pastor; ma son sì poche, che le cappe fornisce poco panno.
And the further his scattered, wandering sheep stray from him, the emptier they return to the milk-filled fold. Many are those who fear harm and cling to the shepherd; but they are so few that there is little cloth to clothe the cloaks.
Or, se le mie parole non son fioche, se la tua audïenza è stata attenta, se ciò ch’è detto a la mente revoche, in parte fia la tua voglia contenta, perché vedrai la pianta onde si scheggia, e vedra’ il corrègger che argomenta
Or, if my words are not faint, if your attention has been keen, if you recall to mind what has been said, in part your desire will be sated, because you will see the plant from which it splinters, and you will see the one who corrects it, who reasons.
