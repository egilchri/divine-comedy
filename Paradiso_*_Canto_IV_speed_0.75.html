Paradiso * Canto IV

In the Heaven of the Moon, Dante remains silent, unable to choose between two questions he wishes to ask. Beatrice, perceiving his internal conflict, articulates his two doubts for him. The first is why souls like Piccarda, who were forced by violence to break their vows, hold a lower rank in heaven. The second is whether souls return to their specific stars after death, as Plato's philosophy suggests. Beatrice answers the second question first, explaining that all blessed souls reside in the Empyrean, the highest heaven. They only appeared in the Moon to accommodate Dante's human understanding. Next, she addresses the issue of broken vows, explaining the difference between an "absolute will," which never consents to sin, and a "conditional will," which can yield to force to avoid a worse outcome. Her explanations resolve Dante's doubts. He then asks a new question: whether a broken vow can be compensated for by other good deeds. Beatrice looks at him intensely, and he is overcome.

Così parlar conviensi al vostro ingegno, però che solo da sensato apprende ciò che fa poscia d’intelletto degno. Per questo la Scrittura condescende a vostra facultate, e piedi e mano attribuisce a Dio e altro intende;
Thus it is fitting to speak to your intellect, because it only learns through sensation what it later makes worthy of the intellect. For this reason, Scripture condescends to your capacity and attributes feet and hands to God, but means something else;

The Holy Church, in human form, shows Gabriel and Michael, and the other who healed Tobit. What Timaeus argues about souls is not like what is seen here, because, as he says, it seems to have feeling.
Dice che l’alma a la sua stella riede, credendo quella quindi esser decisa quando natura per forma la diede; e forse sua sentenza è d’altra guisa che la voce non suona, ed esser puote con intenzion da non esser derisa.
He says that the soul returns to its star, believing that it was sent from there when nature gave it form; and perhaps his judgment is different from what his words sound like, and could be with an intention that should not be mocked.
S’elli intende tornare a queste ruote l’onor de la influenza e ’l biasmo, forse in alcun vero suo arco percuote. Questo principio, male inteso, torse già tutto il mondo quasi, sì che Giove, Mercurio e Marte a nominar trascorse.
If he means that the honor and the blame return to these celestial spheres, perhaps his bow strikes upon some truth. This principle, misunderstood, almost twisted the whole world, so that they rushed to name Jove, Mercury, and Mars.
L’altra dubitazion che ti commove ha men velen, però che sua malizia non ti poria menar da me altrove. Parere ingiusta la nostra giustizia ne li occhi d’i mortali, è argomento di fede e non d’eretica nequizia.
The other doubt that troubles youhas less venom, since its malicecould not lead you away from me elsewhere.That our justice seems unjustin the eyes of mortals is a signof faith and not of heretical wickedness.
Ma perché puote vostro accorgimento ben penetrare a questa veritate, come disiri, ti farò contento. Se vïolenza è quando quel che pate nïente conferisce a quel che sforza, non fuor quest’ alme per essa scusate:

because the will, if unwilling, is not extinguished,but acts as nature does in fire,even if violence twists it a thousand times. Therefore, if it yields much or little,it follows the force; and so these souls did,being able to take refuge in the holy place.
Se fosse stato lor volere intero, come tenne Lorenzo in su la grada, e fece Muzio a la sua man severo, così l’avria ripinte per la strada ond’ eran tratte, come fuoro sciolte; ma così salda voglia è troppo rada.
If their will had been complete,as Lawrence was on the grate,and Mucius was severe with his own hand,they would have been pushed back along the pathfrom which they had been drawn, as soon as they were free;but such a steadfast will is far too rare.
E per queste parole, se ricolte l’hai come dei, è l’argomento casso che t’avria fatto noia ancor più volte. Ma or ti s’attraversa un altro passo dinanzi a li occhi, tal che per te stesso non usciresti: pria saresti lasso.
And with these words, if you have understood them as the gods have, the argument is futile, one that would have annoyed you even more times. But now, another path crosses before your eyes, such that you would not escape it on your own: you would be weary first.
Io t’ho per certo ne la mente messo ch’alma beata non poria mentire, però ch’è sempre al primo vero appresso; e poi potesti da Piccarda udire che l’affezion del vel Costanza tenne; sì ch’ella par qui meco contradire.
I have certainly placed in your mind,such that a blessed soul could not lie,because it always adheres to the first truth;and then you were able to hear from Piccardathat Costanza held the affection of the veil;so that she seems to contradict me here.
Molte fïate già, frate, addivenne che, per fuggir periglio, contra grato si fé di quel che far non si convenne; come Almeone, che, di ciò pregato dal padre suo, la propria madre spense, per non perder pietà si fé spietato.
Brother, it has happened many times that, to avoid danger, one has acted against their will in a way that was not right; as did Alcmaeon, who, at his father's urging, killed his own mother, becoming pitiless to avoid losing pity.
A questo punto voglio che tu pense che la forza al voler si mischia, e fanno sì che scusar non si posson l’offense. Voglia assoluta non consente al danno; ma consentevi in tanto in quanto teme, se si ritrae, cadere in più affanno.
At this point, I want you to consider that force mixes with will, and they act in such a way that offenses cannot be excused. Absolute will does not consent to harm, but consents to it only to the extent that it fears, if it withdraws, falling into greater suffering.

«O amanza del primo amante, o diva», diss’ io appresso, «il cui parlar m’inonda e scalda sì, che più e più m’avviva, non è l’affezion mia tanto profonda, che basti a render voi grazia per grazia; ma quei che vede e puote a ciò risponda.
"O love of the First Lover, O divine one," I said then, "whose words inundate me and warm me so that I am more and more alive, my affection is not so profound that it suffices to repay you grace for grace; but may He who sees and can, respond to this."
Io veggio ben che già mai non si sazia nostro intelletto, se ’l ver non lo illustra di fuor dal qual nessun vero si spazia. Posasi in esso, come fera in lustra, tosto che giunto l’ha; e giugner puollo: se non, ciascun disio sarebbe frustra.
I see well that our intellect is never sated if truth does not illuminate it, beyond which no truth can be found. It rests in truth, like a wild beast in its lair, as soon as it has reached it, and it can reach it; otherwise, every desire would be in vain.
Nasce per quello, a guisa di rampollo, a piè del vero il dubbio; ed è natura ch’al sommo pinge noi di collo in collo. Questo m’invita, questo m’assicura con reverenza, donna, a dimandarvi d’un’altra verità che m’è oscura.
Doubt is born from truth, like a sapling,at its foot; and it is in our natureto push us from one summit to the next.This invites me, this assures me,with reverence, lady, to ask youabout another truth that is obscure to me.
