Inferno * Canto XIX

Dante and Virgil arrive at the third bolgia of the eighth circle, a trench filled with round holes in the stone. From each hole, a sinner is placed upside down, with only their legs protruding from the calf up. Flames burn on the soles of their feet, causing them to kick. Dante notices one sinner whose legs thrash more violently than the others. Virgil carries Dante down the embankment to this specific spirit. The sinner mistakes Dante for Pope Boniface VIII, questioning his early arrival. On Virgil's instruction, Dante corrects him. The spirit then identifies himself as Pope Nicholas III and states that he is waiting for Boniface to arrive and take his place, who will then be followed by another, Clement V. Dante delivers a long, stern rebuke to the sinner, who kicks his feet furiously in response. Pleased by Dante's speech, Virgil embraces him, carries him back up to the bridge, and they proceed onward.

From each sinner's mouth, the feet and legs protruded, up to the thickest part, while the rest of him was inside. Both soles were aflame on all of them; their joints writhed with such force that they would have shattered ropes and twisted fibers.
Qual suole il fiammeggiar de le cose unte muoversi pur su per la strema buccia, tal era lì dai calcagni a le punte. «Chi è colui, maestro, che si cruccia guizzando più che li altri suoi consorti», diss’ io, «e cui più roggia fiamma succia?».
As flames lick at oily things, racing up their outer surfaces, so did the burning spread across them, from heels to toes. "Master, who is that one, writhing in torment more than the others," I asked, "and on whom a fiercer flame is feeding?"
Ed elli a me: «Se tu vuo’ ch’i’ ti porti là giù per quella ripa che più giace, da lui saprai di sé e de’ suoi torti». E io: «Tanto m’è bel, quanto a te piace: tu se’ segnore, e sai ch’i’ non mi parto dal tuo volere, e sai quel che si tace».
And he to me: "If you want me to carry you down that ledge which slopes lowest, you will learn about him, and his offenses, from him." And I said: "It is as pleasing to me as it is to you: you are my lord, and you know that I do not depart from your will, and you know what I keep silent."
Allor venimmo in su l’argine quarto; volgemmo e discendemmo a mano stanca là giù nel fondo foracchiato e arto. Lo buon maestro ancor de la sua anca non mi dipuose, sì mi giunse al rotto di quel che si piangeva con la zanca.
Then we arrived upon the fourth embankment; we turned and descended to the left, down into the perforated and narrow pit. The good master did not set me down from his hip, but brought me to the broken place of that sinner, who wept with his leg.
«O qual che se’ che ’l di sù tien di sotto, anima trista come pal commessa», comincia’ io a dir, «se puoi, fa motto». Io stava come ’l frate che confessa lo perfido assessin, che, poi ch’è fitto, richiama lui per che la morte cessa.
“Oh, you, whatever you are, kept under by the day above, wretched soul bound like a stake,” I began to say, “if you can, speak.”I stood like the friar who confesses the perfidious assassin, who, once impaled, calls him back so that death is stayed.

And he cried: “Are you already standing there? Are you already standing there, Boniface? The written word deceived me by several years.”
Se’ tu sì tosto di quell’ aver sazio per lo qual non temesti tòrre a ’nganno la bella donna, e poi di farne strazio?». Tal mi fec’ io, quai son color che stanno, per non intender ciò ch’è lor risposto, quasi scornati, e risponder non sanno.
“Are you so quickly sated with that wealth,” I asked, “for which you did not fear to deceive the fair lady, and then to ruin her?” I became like those who stand, not understanding what is said to them, as if scorned, and unable to respond.
Allor Virgilio disse: «Dilli tosto: “Non son colui, non son colui che credi”»; e io rispuosi come a me fu imposto. Per che lo spirto tutti storse i piedi; poi, sospirando e con voce di pianto, mi disse: «Dunque che a me richiedi?
Then Virgil said: “Tell him quickly: ‘I am not the one, I am not the one you believe me to be’”; and I replied as I was told. At that the spirit twisted its feet, then, sighing and with a weeping voice, it said to me: “So, what do you ask of me?”
Se di saper ch’i’ sia ti cal cotanto, che tu abbi però la ripa corsa, sappi ch’i’ fui vestito del gran manto; e veramente fui figliuol de l’orsa, cupido sì per avanzar li orsatti, che sù l’avere e qui me misi in borsa.
If you care so much to know who I was, that you ran down the bank to ask, know that I wore the great mantle; and truly I was the son of the she-bear, so greedy to advance my cubs, that I put my fortune and myself in the purse.
Di sotto al capo mio son li altri tratti che precedetter me simoneggiando, per le fessure de la pietra piatti. Là giù cascherò io altresì quando verrà colui ch’i’ credea che tu fossi, allor ch’i’ feci ’l sùbito dimando.
Beneath my head, the others are drawn, those who went before me, simoniacs, flattened within the stone's fissures. I too will fall down there when he arrives, the one I thought you were, when I made my sudden demand.

Our Lord, did he first ask of Saint Peterthat he place the keys within his care? Surely he asked for nothing more than “Follow me.”
Né Pier né li altri tolsero a Matia oro od argento, quando fu sortito al loco che perdé l’anima ria. Però ti sta, ché tu se’ ben punito; e guarda ben la mal tolta moneta ch’esser ti fece contra Carlo ardito.
Neither Peter nor the others took gold or silver from Matthias, when he was chosen for the place that the wicked soul had forfeited. Therefore, remain, for you are rightly punished; and look well upon the ill-gotten coin that made you bold against Charles.

And if it were not that reverence for the sacred keys, which you held in your life of joy, still restrains me, I would use words even more severe; for your avarice afflicts the world, trampling the good and elevating the wicked.
Di voi pastor s’accorse il Vangelista, quando colei che siede sopra l’acque puttaneggiar coi regi a lui fu vista; quella che con le sette teste nacque, e da le diece corna ebbe argomento, fin che virtute al suo marito piacque.
Of you, shepherds, the Evangelist took note, when she who sits upon the waters was seen by him to fornicate with the kings; that one who was born with the seven heads, and from the ten horns had argument, until virtue pleased her husband.

You have made yourselves gods of gold and silver; and what else are you compared to idolaters, save that they worship one, and you worship a hundred? Alas, Constantine, of how much evil was the mother, not your conversion, but that dowry which the first wealthy father took from you!

And while I sang them these notes, either rage or conscience that gnawed him, he shoved strongly with both of his feet. I believe that it pleased my guide, with such a contented mouth he always listened to the sound of the true words expressed.
Però con ambo le braccia mi prese; e poi che tutto su mi s’ebbe al petto, rimontò per la via onde discese. Né si stancò d’avermi a sé distretto, sì men portò sovra ’l colmo de l’arco che dal quarto al quinto argine è tragetto.
But with both of his arms he embraced me; and after he had me fully at his chest, he climbed back up the path where he had descended. Nor did he tire of holding me close, thus he bore me over the top of the archway that serves as passage from the fourth to the fifth bank.
Quivi soavemente spuose il carco, soave per lo scoglio sconcio ed erto che sarebbe a le capre duro varco. Indi un altro vallon mi fu scoperto.
There, he gently put down the burden, gently across the rough and steep rock, a difficult passage even for goats. Then another valley was revealed to me.
